# Keyframe Strategy — Week 1 Videos

**Purpose:** Anchor each video's visual story at its peak concepts, then work backwards to find tweens and cuts. Frame outlines must exist BEFORE any visual generation begins.

---

## Part 1: The Methodology

### What is a peak concept?

A peak concept is a moment in the audio where:
- A counterintuitive idea lands
- A reframe shifts the viewer's mental model
- A specific exercise/technique is named for the first time
- A consequence or failure state is described
- A quotable line is delivered

**Test:** If a viewer paused here and screenshotted the frame, would the screenshot communicate the concept on its own? If yes, it's a peak.

A 4-minute video has roughly **8-10 peaks**. A 7-minute video has **10-13**. More than that and the visuals become hyperactive; fewer and the video drifts.

### What makes a great keyframe?

A keyframe is the single image that crystallizes a peak concept. Six criteria:

1. **One central element.** No competing focal points. A diagram OR a label OR a metaphor — not all three at once.
2. **Concept-first text.** If text is on screen, it names the concept (1-4 words). Not the explanation — the label.
3. **Brand-consistent.** Dark slate gradient (#0f172a → #1e293b → #334155), white serif text, red/orange (#ef4444 / #f97316) for the single accent word.
4. **Pause-worthy.** A viewer screenshotting at this moment gets a self-contained idea.
5. **Citation-friendly.** The visual structure (label + element + minimal text) is something AI image-recognition systems and accessibility tools can describe accurately.
6. **Visually distinct from the previous peak.** No two adjacent peaks should look identical — the cut must feel like progress, not loop.

### The peak → tween → cut workflow

**Step 1: Identify peaks.** Read/listen to the full script. Mark every spot where a peak concept appears. Note the verbal anchor (the exact words being said).

**Step 2: Design keyframes.** For each peak, sketch the keyframe. What's the central element? What's the label? What's the brand layout? What is this image without sound?

**Step 3: Work backwards to tweens.** What does the viewer see in the SPACE between two adjacent peaks? Tweens carry attention from one anchor to the next. They can be:
- Slow zoom on a single element
- Building diagram (additive reveals)
- Camera pan across a scene
- Subtle motion (breathing, particles)
- Or static — letting the audio carry alone for 5-10 seconds

**Step 4: Find cuts.** Cuts are the moments where one peak's visual ENDS and the next BEGINS. They should align with:
- A natural verbal pause in the audio
- A shift in topic in the script
- A pattern interrupt opportunity (every 30-60 seconds)
- The end of a chapter

**Cut types:**
- **Hard cut** (instantaneous switch): for surprise/contrast
- **Dissolve** (cross-fade 0.3-0.5s): for related concepts flowing together
- **Whip pan** (motion blur transition): for high-energy moments
- **Black flash** (frame of black): for major topic changes (use sparingly, max 2x per video)

### AI citation considerations

Frames that get cited well by AI tools and accessibility scanners share traits:
- **Readable in alt-text.** "A whiteboard diagram showing two arrows: 'planning' and 'receiving' splitting from a central brain icon" should match what's actually visible.
- **Text-readable.** Any on-screen text should be high contrast and large enough that OCR captures it.
- **Single-frame screenshottable.** This is the same as pause-worthy — but specifically: the screenshot should NOT need temporal context to make sense.

For our channel: every keyframe should have a corresponding alt-text-ready 1-sentence description in this doc, ready to paste into the YouTube description's chapter heading or used as caption supplement.

### Pause-worthiness scoring

Rate each keyframe 1-5 on pause-worthiness:
- **5** — Could be a standalone Instagram post or shareable image
- **4** — Strong on its own, mild context dependency
- **3** — Functional within the video flow, weak alone
- **2** — Filler frame
- **1** — Don't use; redesign

**Rule:** No keyframe scoring below 4 should ship. Below 3 means redesign.

## Part 3: Tween Strategy (Per Video)

For each adjacent peak pair, define what happens BETWEEN them — the tween that carries attention.

### L1 tweens (between 10 peaks)

| From | To | Tween (what's on screen between) | Duration |
|------|-----|----------------------------------|----------|
| Peak 1 (OVERTHINKING) | Peak 2 (simulation) | Title fades down, brain icon fades up | 0.5s |
| Peak 2 (simulation) | Peak 3 (safe and dead) | Bubbles wilt/fade out one by one | 1-2s |
| Peak 3 (safe and dead) | Peak 4 (BANDWIDTH) | Black flash (topic shift) | 0.2s |
| Peak 4 (BANDWIDTH) | Peak 5 (internal computation) | Diagram updates: planning arrow thickens (animated) | 1s |
| Peak 5 (internal computation) | Peak 6 (forget yourself) | Camera pulls back from diagram to reveal stage | 1s |
| Peak 6 (forget yourself) | Peak 7 (Mirroring) | Whip pan with motion blur | 0.3s |
| Peak 7 (Mirroring) | Peak 8 (First Line) | Hard cut + new card slides in | 0.1s |
| Peak 8 (First Line) | Peak 9 (Last Word) | Hard cut + new card slides in | 0.1s |
| Peak 9 (Last Word) | Peak 10 (Redirect) | Slow dissolve as exercise figure becomes single figure with redirected arrow | 1.5s |

### L23 tweens (rapid-pace tutorial)

For tutorial-style videos, use FAST cuts between exercise cards (0.1-0.2s hard cuts) to maintain instructional momentum. Tweens between thesis peaks (1, 2, 9, 10) should be slower (1-2s dissolves) to emphasize the teaching frame.

### L31 tweens (citation-style)

For knowledge-dense content, use longer dissolves (1-2s) between major peaks to give viewers time to absorb the citation-worthy content. Hard cuts only between within-rule sub-points.

## Part 4: Cut Alignment with Audio

Each cut should align with a verbal pause in the audio. Producer task before final edit:

1. Listen to the full audio track in audio editor (Audacity / DaVinci Resolve)
2. Mark every silence/pause point > 0.3s
3. Map peaks-to-pauses: ideally each cut lands on a natural verbal break
4. Where a peak's verbal anchor doesn't end on a pause, use a soft dissolve instead of hard cut (the audio carries the transition; the visual eases)

**Rule:** No cut should happen mid-word. No exception.

## Part 5: Generation Workflow (After Frame Outlines)

With keyframe outlines locked, visual generation proceeds in this order:

1. **Build template Figma file** — brand colors, fonts, base canvas (1920×1080), pillar icons. One file per pillar (P1, P3, P4 for Week 1).
2. **Render each keyframe as a static slide** — one Figma frame per peak, using the spec from this doc.
3. **Animate transitions** — in DaVinci Resolve, import keyframes as image stack. Apply tweens (slow zoom, slide-in, dissolve) per the tween strategy.
4. **Layer onto audio** — import the existing TTS audio, align cuts to verbal pauses, add subtle ambient music bed (optional, low volume).
5. **Captions** — add YouTube auto-generated captions on upload, then review per per-video file checklist.
6. **Thumbnail** — export a custom thumbnail from the keyframe matching peak score 5 (i.e., for L1 it's Peak 4 BANDWIDTH; for L23 it's Peak 9 sequence; for L31 it's Peak 9 GET OUT OF YOUR HEAD).
7. **Final review** — scroll through the rendered video, screenshot every peak, verify it's a 5-pause-worthy frame.

**No generation begins until this doc's keyframe outlines are signed off.**
