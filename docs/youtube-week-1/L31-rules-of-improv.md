Quick context: I've been building a knowledge graph that maps the five major improv traditions (Johnstone, Spolin, Close/Halpern, UCB, Annoyance/Napier). One of the things that became clear quickly is that the standard "rules of improv" list — Yes And, don't ask questions, make your partner look good, don't negate, there are no mistakes — gets taught as if it's universal, but the traditions actually disagree on most of them.

This video walks through each rule, what each tradition says it means, and where they clash. Specifically:
- Yes And means three different things in Johnstone, UCB, and Napier
- "Don't ask questions" was a fix for one beginner mistake; banning all questions kills natural dialogue
- "Make your partner look good" is the one all five traditions actually agree on
- "Don't negate" — Johnstone's blocking taxonomy is way more nuanced than "don't say no"
- "No mistakes" — Del Close's actual line was "fall, then figure out what to do on the way down"

The deeper point: the rules aren't a checklist. They're training wheels for a single skill (being present), and once you can do that, the rules become unnecessary.
