Google's Project Aristotle found psychological safety is the #1 factor in team performance. More important than talent, experience, or leadership style.

Everyone agrees it matters. Nobody tells you how to actually build it.

Improv ensembles have been solving this problem for 60 years. Not through research — through nightly live experiments in front of paying audiences.

Five things they do:

1. Support the offer, even when it's strange. First response = exploration, not evaluation.

2. Make failure survivable. Respond to mistakes with "next" — not analysis, not blame.

3. Give specific, generous feedback. About the work, never the person.

4. Go first. Someone has to be vulnerable first. In improv, the leader takes the risk. Everyone else follows.

5. Protect the quiet voices. Create explicit invitations for people who default to silence. Not cold-calling — genuine openings.

The key: safety isn't a policy. You can't memo it into existence. It's built through repeated micro-interactions where risk-taking is met with support.

Each of these five practices takes 5 minutes to try in your next meeting.
