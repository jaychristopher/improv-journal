Assertiveness isn't a personality trait. It's a set of physical behaviors.

Keith Johnstone — one of the founders of modern improv — spent decades studying what he called "status." Not formal authority. The moment-to-moment physical negotiation of social position.

Three specific behaviors that read as assertive:

1. Stillness. Stop fidgeting. Restless movement signals discomfort. Stillness signals "I'm comfortable here."

2. The pause. Before answering a question, wait two seconds. Low-status speakers rush to fill silence. High-status speakers let it breathe.

3. Initiatory eye contact. Look at people when YOU choose, not when they look at you. Hold it as long as YOU decide.

The key insight: these aren't things you either have or don't. They're adjustable. Like a volume knob.

The most socially skilled people — and the best improv performers — don't stay high-status. They fluidly adjust. High to lead. Low to connect. High to redirect. Low to invite.

Try one deliberate pause in your next meeting. Just one. Notice what shifts.
