### [1:00-3:00] Rule 1: "Say Yes, And"
- What people think: agree with everything and add
- What it actually means: accept the REALITY your partner established and contribute to it
- Characters can disagree, argue, refuse — that's drama. The PERFORMER can't refuse the PERFORMER's offer.
- Johnstone doesn't use the phrase "Yes, And" at all — his version is "be changed by what happens"
- UCB teaches it as structure. Napier says obsessing over it produces "wimpy, compliant" scenes.
- THE REAL PRINCIPLE: Accept the shared reality and bring yourself to it.

### [3:00-4:00] Rule 2: "Don't Ask Questions"
- This is mostly wrong. It was invented for a specific beginner problem — using questions to avoid making choices
- "What do you do for a living?" = empty question (avoiding commitment)
- "Are you seriously wearing that to Dad's funeral?" = loaded question (making 4 choices at once)
- THE REAL PRINCIPLE: Don't use questions to avoid committing. Questions that contain offers are great.

### [4:00-5:00] Rule 3: "Make Your Partner Look Good"
- This one is actually right — all 5 traditions agree
- But it doesn't mean "be nice." It means: create conditions where your partner can succeed.
- Sometimes that means setting up their joke. Sometimes it means challenging them.
- THE REAL PRINCIPLE: The scene is a shared creation. Your success and theirs are the same thing.

### [5:00-6:00] Rule 4: "Don't Negate"
- Oversimplified. Negation in improv = destroying established reality. Not the same as character-level disagreement.
- "We're not in a hospital" = negation. "I refuse to go to the hospital" = drama.
- Johnstone identified a whole family beyond simple "no": wimping, cancelling, hedging, pimping
- Napier: the FEAR of negation can be worse. Robotic agreement = scene-death too.
- THE REAL PRINCIPLE: Don't destroy the shared reality. Within it, conflict is essential.

### [6:00-7:00] Rule 5: "There Are No Mistakes"
- Doesn't mean quality doesn't matter
- Means: unplanned moments are material, not errors
- Del Close: "Fall, then figure out what to do on the way down"
- A mistake you ignore = missed offer. A mistake you build on = gift.
- THE REAL PRINCIPLE: Mistakes are unintentional offers. Treat them as gifts.

### [7:00-7:30] What All the Rules Are Actually About
- Strip away the specific instructions and every rule solves the same problem: get out of your head and into the scene
- Yes And = stop evaluating, start participating
- Don't negate = stop controlling, start building
- No mistakes = stop judging, start using
- The rules are training wheels for presence. Once you can be present, they're unnecessary.
