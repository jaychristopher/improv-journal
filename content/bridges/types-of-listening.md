# Types of Listening: The Improv Framework for Understanding How We Hear

**There are three listening modes that matter: broadcast (composing your reply while they talk), evaluative (judging what they say in real time), and receptive (fully receiving their complete communication without agenda).** Most people default to broadcast. Improv performers train receptive listening because their art form dies without it.

You've probably heard that there are different "types of listening" — active vs. passive, empathic vs. critical. Most frameworks list five to seven types and advise you to use the right one for the right context. This is technically correct and practically useless, because in the moment, you're not choosing a listening type from a menu. You're doing whatever your brain defaults to.

Improv offers a more useful framework — not a taxonomy of types, but a way of understanding what your attention is actually doing when you listen, and how to redirect it.

## The Three Attention Modes

Every moment you're "listening," your attention is doing one of three things. These aren't types you choose — they're modes your brain defaults to based on habit, context, and cognitive load.

### Mode 1: Broadcast Listening (the default)

**What it is:** You hear the other person's words while simultaneously composing your response. Part of your attention is on them; most of it is on you.

**What it feels like from the inside:** You're waiting for a gap. You're scanning for the thing they said that connects to what you want to say. You're tracking the conversation while running a parallel process.

**What it feels like to the other person:** They can feel that you're not fully there. The responses come too quickly (you had them ready before they finished). The responses are slightly off-target (you were responding to the gist, not to what was actually said).

**When it happens:** This is the default mode for most social interaction. It's especially strong in meetings, debates, and any conversation where you feel the need to contribute.

Improv calls the parallel process **internal computation** — the planning, evaluating, and composing that runs in the background and steals bandwidth from reception. Every experienced improviser identifies internal computation as the number one thing that kills their scene work.

### Mode 2: Evaluative Listening

**What it is:** You're hearing the content and judging it in real time. Agreeing, disagreeing, assessing, categorizing. "That's a good point." "That's wrong." "I've heard this before." "This is interesting."

**What it feels like from the inside:** Engaged. You're processing what they're saying. It feels like good listening — and it's better than broadcast mode — but the judgment layer is filtering everything through your own framework.

**What it feels like to the other person:** Better than broadcast listening. But they can sense that you're evaluating, and evaluation creates subtle defensiveness. People share less when they sense they're being scored.

**When it's useful:** Analysis, decision-making, learning. When someone presents an argument and you need to assess it, evaluative listening is appropriate.

**When it hurts:** Emotional conversations, trust-building, early-stage relationships. When someone needs to be heard, evaluation is a barrier.

### Mode 3: Receptive Listening (the improv mode)

**What it is:** Your attention is fully on the other person. You've let go of composing your response. You've suspended evaluation. You're tracking the complete signal — words, tone, body language, emotion, what's not being said — without trying to do anything with it.

**What it feels like from the inside:** Quiet. Your internal monologue has gone mostly silent. You feel the other person's communication rather than analyzing it. When they stop talking, a response emerges naturally rather than being retrieved from a queue.

**What it feels like to the other person:** Transformative. They feel *seen* — not just heard, but fully received. The quality of attention is so rare that it registers as intimacy. This is what people mean when they say "she's such a good listener."

**When it's useful:** Every conversation that matters. Every relationship you want to deepen. Every moment where the other person's experience is more important than your contribution.

This is what Viola Spolin meant by "receiving" and what improv performers train as **active listening**. It's not a technique you apply — it's a state you enter by quieting the internal computation that normally runs in the background.

## How to Shift Between Modes

The shift from broadcast/evaluative to receptive isn't intellectual — it's attentional. You can't think your way into it. But you can use these specific practices:

### Drop the response queue

Notice the moment you start composing your reply. That's the signal that you've shifted from receiving to broadcasting. When you notice it, let the prepared response go. Trust that a better one will appear when the other person finishes.

This is terrifying the first few times. The silence after they stop talking — before your response has formed — feels enormous. But the response that emerges from full reception is always more relevant, more specific, and more connecting than the one you had prepared.

### Track the thing beneath the words

Shift your attention from *what* they're saying to *how* they're saying it. The emotion in their voice. The energy shift mid-sentence. The thing they almost said but pulled back from. This moves your attention from content (which triggers evaluation) to signal (which triggers reception).

In improv, this is called tracking **offers** — not just the verbal content, but the complete communication. The performer who tracks offers builds scenes that feel alive. The listener who tracks offers has conversations that feel real.

### Use the body channel

Your body receives communication that your conscious mind misses. Notice what you feel in your body when the other person is talking. Tension, warmth, restlessness, openness — these are your body's interpretation of their signal, and they're often more accurate than your mind's analysis.

Spolin's training centered on this: the body knows before the mind does. By attending to your physical response, you bypass the analytical layer and access a deeper kind of listening.

## The Practical Version

You don't need to be in receptive mode all the time. That would be exhausting and inappropriate — broadcast and evaluative listening serve real purposes. The skill is **knowing which mode you're in** and being able to shift when the situation calls for it.

The next time someone is telling you something that matters to them — not a casual update, but something they care about — try this: let your response queue empty. Stop evaluating whether you agree. Track their energy instead of their argument. Let your body feel what they're communicating.

You'll notice two things immediately: the conversation deepens, and the other person opens up. Not because you used a technique. Because you gave them something rare — the experience of being fully received by another person.
