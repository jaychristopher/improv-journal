You've been doing improv for a year or two. You know yes-and. You've done Harold. You've had great scenes and terrible ones. But here's what keeps you up at night: you can't tell WHY the great ones were great or WHY the terrible ones died. All you have is a feeling — and feelings don't give you a practice plan.

This path gives you the vocabulary.

We start with **diagnosing scene failure** — a specific taxonomy of how scenes break. Three collapse modes (latency, fracture, decay) and the antipatterns that cause them (bulldozing, steering, hesitation, performing cleverness, overcomplication, judgment). After this, you'll never say "I don't know what happened" again. You'll say "that was a fracture — I was in a different emotional reality than my partner" or "that was latency — I retreated into internal computation and missed two offers."

Then we go deeper into **discovery** — the skill of finding what the scene is about after it starts, instead of deciding in advance. This is where the intermediate plateau lives: you've learned enough to have preferences, and those preferences are now cages. Discovery is the practice of surrendering the plan and trusting what emerges.

Then **signal clarity** — the principle that in a bandwidth-limited system, simplicity isn't laziness but engineering. Many intermediate improvisers overcomplicate because they think complexity equals quality. It doesn't. The clearest signal wins.

Finally, **the plateau itself** — what it is (the knowledge-ability gap), why it feels like regression (your standards outgrew your execution), and specific recovery strategies for each collapse mode. Plus the insight that nobody talks about: reconnecting with joy is a technical skill, not a platitude. Gratitude isn't self-help. It's a system requirement.

This path won't make you funnier. It'll make you diagnosable — to yourself. And that's how you break through.
