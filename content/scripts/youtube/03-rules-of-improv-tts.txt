[curious] Search "rules of improv" and you'll find the same list everywhere. Say Yes And. Don't ask questions. Make your partner look good. Don't negate. There are no mistakes.

[contemplative] Neat. Clean. Easy to remember.

[emphatic] And about half of it is either misleading or flat-out wrong, depending on which improv tradition you're drawing from.

[short pause]

[warm] I wrote a full breakdown at physicsofconnection.com slash rules-of-improv — link in the description. It's the most thorough treatment of this topic on the internet. But let me give you the highlights.

[short pause]

[teaching] Rule one. Yes, And.

[curious] What people think it means: agree with everything and add something.

[contemplative] What it actually means: accept the reality your partner has established and contribute to it.

[emphatic] Here's where it gets misleading. Yes And is not about agreement. Characters can disagree. Characters can argue, lie, refuse, and fight. What can't happen is the performer refusing the performer's offer. If your scene partner says we're in a hospital, you don't have to say yes I love this hospital. You just can't say no, we're in a restaurant.

[teaching] Keith Johnstone doesn't use the phrase Yes And at all. His version is "be changed by what happens" — which is deeper. UCB teaches Yes And as a specific technique with structure. Mick Napier from the Annoyance argues that obsessing over Yes And produces wimpy, compliant, passionless scenes.

[contemplative] The real principle: accept the shared reality and bring something of yourself to it. The acceptance is non-negotiable. The "and" is where your personality, your point of view, your creative contribution lives.

[short pause]

[teaching] Rule two. Don't ask questions.

[emphatic] This is mostly wrong.

[curious] The rule was invented to solve a specific beginner problem — improvisers who ask questions to avoid making choices. "What do you do for a living?" is an attempt to make your partner do the creative work.

[teaching] But questions are a normal part of how humans talk. Banning them entirely creates stilted, unnatural dialogue. The real issue isn't questions — it's empty questions. "What do you do?" is empty. "Are you seriously wearing that to Dad's funeral?" is loaded with information. There's a funeral. There's a father. There's a relationship. There's a judgment. That's not avoiding a choice — it's making several at once.

[contemplative] The real principle: don't use questions to avoid committing. Do use questions when they contain offers.

[short pause]

[teaching] Rule three. Make your partner look good.

[warm] This is one of the few rules that all five major improv traditions essentially agree on, though they frame it differently.

[teaching] What it actually means: create conditions where your partner can succeed.

Johnstone talks about lowering your status so your partner's offers can shine. Close and Halpern frame it as ensemble responsibility. UCB builds it into game structure — if you've found the game, your job is to set up your partner to heighten it.

[emphatic] Making your partner look good doesn't mean avoiding conflict, being agreeable, or suppressing your own contributions. It means tracking what your partner needs to succeed and providing it. Sometimes that means setting up their joke. Sometimes it means giving them space. Sometimes it means playing a character who challenges them, because the scene needs it.

[contemplative] The scene is a shared creation. Your success and your partner's success are the same thing.

[short pause]

[teaching] Rule four. Don't negate.

[curious] What people think: never say no.

[emphatic] Oversimplified. Negation in improv is a specific term with a specific meaning — destroying the established reality. "We're not in a hospital" when your partner established a hospital — that's negation. "I refuse to go to the hospital" — that's drama.

[teaching] Johnstone identified an entire taxonomy of blocking behaviors that go far beyond saying no. Wimping — accepting without adding. Cancelling — making an offer and immediately retracting it. Hedging — making vague, uncommitted choices. Pimping — forcing your partner to do the work. All of these are forms of negation that don't involve the word no.

[contemplative] Meanwhile, Napier argues that the fear of negation can be worse than negation itself. Performers so terrified of saying no that they agree with everything produce scenes with no tension, no stakes, and no point of view.

[warm] The real principle: don't destroy the shared reality. But within that reality, conflict, disagreement, and refusal are not just allowed — they're necessary.

[short pause]

[teaching] Rule five. There are no mistakes.

[curious] What people think: everything is fine, don't worry about quality.

[contemplative] What it actually means: unplanned moments are material, not errors.

[emphatic] Del Close put it precisely: fall, then figure out what to do on the way down. An accidental word, a weird physical choice, a moment of confusion — these aren't mistakes to be ignored. They're offers from your unconscious mind. The best scenes often come from what happened by accident rather than what anyone planned.

[teaching] But this doesn't mean quality doesn't matter. It means the standard of quality shifts. From "did I execute my plan?" to "did I use what happened?" A mistake you ignore is a missed offer. A mistake you build on is a gift.

[short pause]

[contemplative] What all the rules are actually about — strip away the specific instructions and every rule is trying to solve the same problem: get out of your head and into the scene.

[teaching] Yes And — stop evaluating, start participating. Don't ask questions — stop deflecting, start committing. Make your partner look good — stop competing, start collaborating. Don't negate — stop controlling, start building. There are no mistakes — stop judging, start using.

[emphatic] The rules aren't a checklist. They're training wheels for a single skill: being present enough to respond to what's actually happening instead of what you planned. Once you can do that, the rules become unnecessary.
