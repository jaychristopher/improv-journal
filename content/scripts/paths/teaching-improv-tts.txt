Here's the script:

[conversational] You know what's funny? Nobody tells you how different it is. You've been doing improv for years, you're solid, someone says "hey, can you teach a class?" and you think — sure. I know this stuff. How hard can it be?

[warm] And then you're standing in front of twelve people who are terrified, and you realize knowing how to ride a bicycle does not make you a cycling instructor.

[emphatic] Not even close. I could do a great scene. But ask me to explain why it worked? I'd just stare at you.

[teaching] That's the gap. And it's not a knowledge gap — it's an articulation gap. You have the instincts. What you don't have is the explanatory layer underneath them. The part that turns "it just felt right" into something you can actually teach.

[pause]

[curious] So where do you start? Because "go study pedagogy" feels overwhelming.

[teaching] You start with the system. Not your system for teaching — the system underneath improv itself. There are six laws that govern why this art form works. Irreversibility. Bandwidth limits. Fragility. Continuous signaling. Relational meaning. Interdependence.

[curious] And you're not teaching those to your students.

[teaching] No. These are for you. They're your foundation. Because here's what happens without them — a student raises their hand and says "why does yes-and matter?" and you say "because it's the rule." And they nod. And they don't really get it. And you feel it.

[conversational] But with the system —

[confident] You say "because shared reality is fragile. Every denial corrupts the database both performers are building from. Yes-and isn't a courtesy — it's structural integrity." Now they get it. And you got it deeper by saying it.

[short pause]

[curious] What about the actual mechanics of teaching? Because understanding why improv works and being able to run a room are also different skills.

[teaching] Completely different. And the first one is safety. Not as a nice-to-have. As the prerequisite for everything else.

[curious] Everything?

[emphatic] Everything. A student who doesn't feel safe won't take risks. A student who won't take risks can't discover anything. And discovery is the whole game. So before you teach a single exercise, you're building a container where failure is genuinely okay. Not "we say it's okay" okay. Actually okay.

[teaching] Then structure. Skills build on prerequisites, and the sequence matters. You wouldn't teach game-of-the-scene before someone can make and accept an offer. Sounds obvious, but I've watched teachers throw pattern games at people who can't sustain eye contact yet. Curriculum design is just knowing what unlocks what.

[short pause]

[curious] What about in the moment? When a scene's happening and you can see something going wrong?

[teaching] Side-coaching. And this is an art. You're giving real-time guidance without stopping the work. Too much and you're directing — the students are performing for you instead of each other. Too little and they're floundering without language for what's happening.

[conversational] It's like improv itself. You're reading and responding.

[warm] Exactly. Teaching is a scene. You're making offers, reading your scene partners, adjusting in real time. The same instincts that make you a good performer make you a good teacher — you just have to point them in a different direction.

[pause]

[curious] And giving notes after scenes? That's the part I dread.

[teaching] Less is more. That's the whole thing. One note per scene. Maybe two if they're related. Because a student who gets five notes remembers zero of them.

[curious] Just one?

[confident] The one that unlocks the most. And here's the real move — exercises beat verbal feedback almost every time. If someone's not listening to their partner, don't tell them to listen harder. Put them in a repetition exercise where listening is the only option. Let the structure teach what words can't.

[short pause]

[curious] After all that — the system, the toolkit — the path revisits fundamentals. Why?

[teaching] Because building on offers and presence and commitment look different when you're teaching them versus doing them. As a performer, you just accept offers. As a teacher, you need to see why a student isn't accepting them. Are they afraid? Are they in their head planning? Are they so focused on being funny that they can't hear what's actually being given to them? The diagnosis changes the intervention.

[conversational] So you're not relearning the basics. You're learning to see them in other people.

[teaching] Which requires understanding them at a level you never needed as a performer. The best teachers I know understand fundamentals more deeply than they understand advanced technique. Because fundamentals are where students live.

[pause]

[warm] Here's the thing nobody tells new teachers. You're going to want to copy your best teacher. Same exercises, same energy, same notes. And that makes sense — they're your model for what good teaching looks like.

[teaching] But improv teachers should all teach differently. That's the whole point. Your voice, your metaphors, your way of seeing — that's what makes your class yours. What every good teacher shares isn't a method. It's the ability to explain, to create safety, and to design experiences where students discover principles for themselves.

[short pause]

[contemplative] You know what's underneath all of this? The same thing that's underneath good improv. Trust. Trust that the system works. Trust that your students will find it if you build the right container. Trust that you don't have to control every moment for the learning to happen.

[warm] You already know how to surrender to a scene. Now learn to surrender to a classroom. The principles are the same. The stakes feel different — but the practice is identical.
