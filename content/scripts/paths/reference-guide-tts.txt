[curious] Have you ever noticed that every improv book you pick up feels like it was written in a different language?

[teaching] Not literally, but yes. Johnstone talks about status and spontaneity. Close and Halpern talk about group mind and game. UCB talks about unusual things and base realities. Spolin talks about space and focus. And they're all describing the same art form — allegedly.

[curious] Right, and you read one, you think you get it, and then someone from a different tradition gives you a note and you realize you've been standing in one room of a very large house.

[teaching] That's exactly the problem. Every tradition gives you a complete-feeling system. Johnstone's world makes total sense on its own. UCB's framework is internally consistent. But they contradict each other in specific, important ways — and nobody maps where.

[curious] Like what?

[teaching] Take "yes, and." Johnstone means accept the offer and let it change you. UCB means yes, and heighten the game. Annoyance means yes, and follow your honest impulse even if it destroys the scene's premise. Same two words. Three genuinely different instructions for what to do in the moment.

[short pause]

[curious] And we just... pretend that's not happening?

[matter-of-fact] Mostly. Teachers pick a lineage, teach within it, and treat the others as either compatible or wrong. Which means if you've trained in multiple places — and most serious performers have — you're carrying contradictions you've never been given language for.

[contemplative] That's what this whole project is, actually. Naming those contradictions. Not to resolve them — some of them can't be resolved — but to make them visible so you can make real choices instead of just defaulting to whoever taught you last.

[curious] So it's like a map of improv knowledge that includes the disagreements.

[teaching] A knowledge graph. One hundred nineteen atoms — small, precise units of improv knowledge — organized by type. Laws, principles, techniques, exercises, antipatterns, pedagogy. Each one sourced. Each one carrying counter-positions from the traditions that see it differently.

[curious] Give me an example of what an atom looks like.

[warm] Take game-of-the-scene. The atom defines it precisely, cites where the concept originates — Close and Halpern through the Harold, refined by UCB into their manual — and then flags the counter-positions. Johnstone doesn't use the concept at all. TJ and Dave actively argue against identifying game as a discrete move. The atom holds all of that without picking a winner.

[curious] That's different from how it usually gets taught.

[emphatic] Because usually it gets taught as gospel or heresy. Either game is the thing or game is a trap. The actual interesting question is under what conditions does finding game serve the scene, and when does it constrain it? But you can't even ask that question until you see the full landscape.

[pause]

[curious] Okay, so there's this graph with atoms and traditions. How does a performer actually use it?

[conversational] A few ways in. If you're chasing a concept — say you keep hearing "status" and you want to really understand it — you find the atom, read it, and follow the links. It connects to related atoms, to the source texts, to the counter-positions. You go as deep as you want.

[teaching] If you're interested in a tradition, there are reference atoms for each major text. Follow Johnstone's Impro through the graph and you see every concept it touches and where other traditions push back. Same for Truth in Comedy, the UCB Manual, Spolin's book, all of them.

[curious] So you can trace a lineage.

[confident] Exactly. Or trace a type. Want every antipattern? Every exercise? Every law? They're categorized. The types aren't arbitrary buckets — they reflect how the knowledge actually functions. A law explains why something works. A principle guides what to do. A technique is a specific move. An antipattern names a specific way scenes die.

[curious] You said laws. What laws?

[teaching] Six of them. Drawn from cognitive science, sociology, performance studies. They're the physics underneath everything — the constraints that explain why improv works the way it does, regardless of tradition. Things like why offers decay if you don't build on them immediately. Why status is always being negotiated whether you want it to be or not. Why group agreement is structurally fragile.

[contemplative] They bridge this gap between how performers talk about improv and how researchers talk about human interaction. Not to make improv academic — to make the academic insights useful on stage.

[curious] That's the part that always frustrated me. The research exists, but it never connects to what I'm actually doing in a scene.

[warm] Right. And the practitioner wisdom exists, but it's often just "trust it" or "you'll feel it." The laws give you a third language — one that explains the feel in terms of mechanism, without killing the feel.

[curious] Where should someone actually start?

[teaching] Traditions in Tension, if you want to understand where the schools agree and disagree. The System Underneath, if you want the deeper mechanics. Diagnosing Scene Failure, if you're a director or teacher who needs precise vocabulary for what's going wrong. And there's a thread on what improv's discoveries actually mean beyond the stage — honestly assessed, not oversold.

[curious] "Not oversold" — say more about that.

[serious] There's a whole industry of people claiming improv fixes everything. Leadership, communication, creativity, trauma. Some of that transfers. Some of it really doesn't, and the reasons why are interesting. The graph is honest about the boundary.

[curious] I think what strikes me is the sourcing. Every claim has a citation?

[matter-of-fact] Every claim. Where the source is oral tradition — something a teacher said in a workshop that became community knowledge — it's flagged as oral tradition. Where the synthesis is original — a connection the graph draws that no single source makes — it's labeled as original. Where something is unverifiable, it says so.

[warm] The methodology is the credibility. It's not "trust me, I've been doing this for twenty years." It's "here's the claim, here's where it comes from, here's who disagrees, decide for yourself."
