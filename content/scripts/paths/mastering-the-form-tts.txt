[curious] Here's something nobody tells you when you start doing Harolds. The Harold is not the thing. The Harold is the way you learn the thing.

[teaching] Del Close designed it as a gym circuit. Every core skill, every rep, every show. Opening. Group game. Scene work. Callbacks. Thematic thinking. You can't run a Harold without touching all of it. That's the genius of it as a training form.

[conversational] But nobody thinks the gym circuit is the sport.

[warm] And most improvisers hit a moment — could be six months in, could be three years — where they realize the Harold has become the ceiling and the floor. It's the only longform they know, so it's the only longform they can imagine.

[curious] So what's on the other side of that ceiling?

[teaching] Six formats, at least. Each one built around something the Harold doesn't prioritize. The Armando strips away all that structural complexity and replaces it with a real person telling a real story. Your scenes come from someone's actual life, not from an opening game. And something shifts when the source material is true.

[conversational] The scenes get warmer.

[warm] They get specific in a way that's hard to manufacture. You're not riffing on an abstraction — you're riffing on someone's actual Tuesday.

[short pause]

[teaching] La Ronde does something the Harold almost never lets you do. It shows you how a single character changes depending on who they're across from. Same person with their mother, their boss, a stranger — and you watch them become three different people. The Harold is built around theme and game. La Ronde is built around the fact that you are partially assembled by whoever's in the room with you.

[contemplative] [pause] That's not just an improv insight. That's a life insight.

[curious] Then there's narrative longform, organic longform, two-person, genre formats — each one demanding a different muscle entirely.

[teaching] Narrative makes you track consequence. What happens in scene three has to be caused by scene two. Organic makes you throw away the scaffolding and trust what the show is asking for. Two-person strips you down to nothing but listening — TJ and Dave did it for twenty years, one suggestion, one hour, and the whole thing assembles itself out of the paying attention. Genre formats hand you a set of conventions the audience already knows, which frees you to do the actually hard work of being a real person inside a familiar shape.

[emphatic] The Harold taught you the grammar. These formats teach you the dialects.

[curious] [short pause] Okay. But here's what I want to know. Say you learn all six. You can do an Armando, you can do a La Ronde, you can run narrative or organic or whatever the show needs. Are you done?

[teaching] No. And this is where it gets interesting. Because knowing formats is like knowing how to build rooms. It matters. But the audience doesn't live in a room. The audience lives in the house.

[conversational] Meaning the whole show.

[warm] Meaning the whole show. And shaping a full show — not just individual scenes, not just your format — is a completely different skill. It's architecture.

[curious] What does that look like in practice?

[teaching] Start with the opening. An organic opening isn't a warm-up you get through. It's a palette. It generates themes, images, emotional textures — raw material the ensemble mines for the next thirty minutes. The skill isn't in doing the opening. It's in extraction. Listening for what recurs, what resonates, what the show wants to be about tonight.

[conversational] So the opening tells you what kind of show you're in.

[matter-of-fact] If you're listening. Most ensembles treat the opening like a checkbox. The great ones treat it like a seismograph.

[short pause]

[teaching] Then there's heat and weight. This is the thing that separates shows that work from shows that are just a collection of good scenes. Heat is high energy — fast, loud, physical, absurd. Weight is quiet — still, honest, vulnerable. And the art is in alternation.

[curious] The roller coaster thing.

[teaching] Besser's metaphor. You need the slow climb to make the drop exciting. A show that's all heat becomes exhausting. A show that's all weight becomes ponderous. But a show that swings — big physical scene, then something quiet and grounded, then a group game that builds to something unhinged, then two people just talking — that show breathes.

[contemplative] [pause] The audience doesn't know why it works. They just feel it.

[warm] They feel it because contrast is how humans process experience. It's not an improv principle, it's a nervous system principle. You can't feel how loud something is without the quiet before it.

[curious] And who's managing all this? Who decides "okay, we need weight now"?

[teaching] Everyone. And no one. The backline — the performers not in the scene — they're tracking the whole show. What themes have come up, what energy they've been running, what connections are forming. And the best backline performers know something counterintuitive: the most generous move is often staying out. Not entering. Letting the scene that's working keep working.

[conversational] And then editing.

[emphatic] Editing is pacing at the show level. Edit at the top — sweep when the scene is at its best, not when it's dying. The audience should feel slight loss, not relief. That one principle changes everything about how a show feels.

[curious] [short pause] And the ending?

[teaching] The run. Rapid-fire callbacks, collisions, scenes getting shorter and faster. Content becomes almost secondary to momentum. The audience is carried by recognition — they're watching everything they invested in over the last twenty-five minutes come back at triple speed.

[warm] And the strongest endings circle back to the opening. When a show returns to where it started, it feels authored. It feels like someone wrote it. And no one did.

[contemplative] [pause] That's the whole game, isn't it. Making something feel shaped when nothing was planned.

[teaching] That's the whole game. The Harold teaches you the moves. The formats teach you the languages. But show-level craft — heat, weight, pacing, editing, the run, the callback to the opening — that's where improv becomes architecture.

[emphatic] You're not just building rooms anymore. You're building a house. And the house is what the audience lives in for the next forty-five minutes of their life.

[warm] The rooms matter. But the house is the thing they remember walking out.
