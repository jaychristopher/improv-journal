# TTS Script Guide

## The Listener

An **improv player** — someone who's taken classes, does shows, and thinks about the craft. They've been on stage enough to recognize what you're describing. They don't need improv explained to them; they need it *named* and *connected*.

They're also a person who lives in the world. The best moments are when a concept clicks not just for their scene work but for their Tuesday morning meeting, their relationship, their inner monologue. Tie it into life — but start from improv, don't shoehorn it.

**They are NOT:**
- A corporate audience hearing about improv for the first time
- A complete beginner who needs "yes, and" explained
- An academic who wants citations

## Voice & Tone

Two hosts. One is curious and probing (asks the questions the listener is thinking). The other is knowledgeable and warm (teaches through stories and reframes, not lectures).

- **Conversational, not academic.** "You know that moment when..." not "Research demonstrates that..."
- **Specific, not abstract.** Name the scene. Describe the move. Make them see it.
- **Concise.** Every line should earn its place. If it doesn't teach, reframe, or land emotionally — cut it.
- **Pattern beats.** Use the back-and-forth to create rhythm. Short question → substantive answer → surprised reframe → deeper truth. The dialogue structure IS the pedagogy.

## What to include

- The **core insight** — what is this concept, really? Name it precisely.
- **One or two concrete examples** — scenes, moments, exercises. Make the listener go "oh, THAT's what that is."
- **The connection to life** — where does this show up off stage? One sentence is enough if it lands.
- **The counterintuitive bit** — what do people get wrong? What surprises?
- **Why it matters** — not "this is important" but the specific consequence of getting it right or wrong.

## What to skip

- **Citations and references.** They're on the page. Don't say "Johnstone argues in Impro that..." — say "Johnstone's frame: spontaneity isn't something you summon, it's something you stop blocking."
- **Exhaustive coverage.** The written atom is the full treatment. The audio is the hook that makes you want to read it.
- **Summaries at the end.** Don't recap. End on the strongest thought.
- **Meta-commentary.** Don't say "this is a really important concept" or "let's talk about X." Just talk about X.

## Organizational content

Most content is for the **individual performer**. Only these items should use team/workplace framing:

**Atoms (pedagogy type):**
- curriculum-design
- giving-notes
- safety-in-the-room
- side-coaching

**Bridges:**
- team-building-activities
- psychological-safety
- how-to-give-feedback
- how-to-deal-with-conflict

For these, the listener shifts to someone applying improv principles in a team or classroom context. Still conversational, still specific — just different stakes.

## Format

```
[emote] Dialogue line.

[emote] Response line.
```

- Each paragraph = one speaker turn (they alternate)
- Start each with an emote tag: [curious], [teaching], [conversational], [warm], [emphatic], [contemplative], [serious], [matter-of-fact], [confident]
- Pacing tags: [short pause], [pause], [long pause]
- No markdown, no headers, no bold
- No speaker labels (A:/B:)
- **Target: 800–1200 words** (3–5 minutes of audio)

## Thread scripts

Threads are longer — they weave multiple atoms into a coherent arc. Target 1500–2500 words (6–10 minutes). The structure should feel like a journey: setup the question, explore it from multiple angles, arrive somewhere unexpected.
