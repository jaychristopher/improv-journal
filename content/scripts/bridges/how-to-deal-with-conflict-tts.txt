[contemplative] Johnstone called blocking a form of aggression. Not because the blocker means to be aggressive, but because negating someone's reality is functionally aggressive. It forces them to either submit or fight.

[curious] Okay, so I see the diagnosis. But here's where I get stuck — because in a scene, accepting is easy. Someone says "we need to talk about dinner last night," I say "yes, and it was terrible." Done. In real life, accepting feels like losing.

[warm] Your partner says, "You never listen to me." The offer — the actual offer — is "I'm experiencing a pattern where I don't feel heard." The demand, if you read it that way, is "admit you're a bad listener." Most people respond to the demand. They defend. Block. Escalation.

[curious] So what does accepting the offer sound like?

[teaching] "You're feeling like I haven't been hearing you." Full stop. That's it. That's not agreement. It's not "you're right and I'm wrong." It's acknowledging that their experience is real to them.

[curious] The "yes."

[teaching] The "yes." And then the "and" is where you add your reality. "And from my side, I've been trying to listen, but I get overwhelmed when we talk about this topic." [teaching] That's exactly it. Johnstone saw this — most conflict isn't about the stated issue. It's about a status transaction that felt threatening. [curious] Which is why arguments about dishes turn into relationship-threatening fights.

[teaching] The dish isn't the issue. The status transaction embedded in the dish — "I don't matter enough for you to do this small thing" — that's the issue. And if you can see that, you can address the real problem instead of optimizing the chore chart.

[curious] So how do you actually do this? In the moment, when your blood is up?

[teaching] Three moves. Same thing you'd do in a scene, just slower. First — receive the offer. Before you respond, silently name what they're actually putting forward. Not their argument. Their offer. "You completely blindsided me in that meeting" — the offer is "I felt unsafe and unprepared."

[curious] Second move?

[teaching] Accept before adding. Acknowledge the offer out loud before you introduce your own reality. And here's the key — pause between the acceptance and the addition. That pause is where de-escalation lives.

[contemplative] Because their nervous system is running a threat-detection loop. They're scanning for evidence that you actually heard them. If you rush to your point, the detector reads "they're not hearing me, keep fighting." If you pause after acknowledging them, it reads "they received it, I can lower my defenses."

[curious] It's like the difference between a player who genuinely receives your initiation and a player who says "yes and" but is already steering toward their idea.

[teaching] You can feel it, right? You always know which one is actually listening. Third move — add your reality, not your rebuttal. That's a second offer alongside the first.

[serious] Now — honest caveat. This works when both people want resolution more than victory. It works for the disagreements between partners, friends, colleagues, collaborators. [curious] Right. You can't "yes, and" your way through gaslighting.

[teaching] No. And you can't "yes, and" bad faith either. If someone's not trying to resolve but to win, the acceptance framework just rewards their escalation.

[contemplative] When you block that data — "you shouldn't feel that way," "that's not what happened" — you're not resolving anything. You're refusing to receive the information you need to resolve it.
