[curious] Okay so here's something I've been chasing. You watch two scenes back to back. Same premise, same level of experience, same everything on paper. One is fine. The other one is electric. What is that?

[teaching] Almost always, it's two things. Presence and commitment. And here's the thing — neither of them is a technique. You can't add them to your toolkit like a game or a move. They're states you're in, or you're not.

[curious] Okay but that sounds mystical. Give me something I can hold.

[teaching] Fair. Start with presence. Most people hear "presence" and think "active listening." Which, yes — but that's the doorway, not the room. Active listening is you receiving your partner. Presence is you actually being in the kitchen with them. Smelling the coffee you both agreed was burning. Feeling the weight of the knife you're holding. When you're present, the scene is physically real to you.

[conversational] Say more about that. Because I can tell when I'm doing it and when I'm not, but I can't always tell *why*.

[teaching] Here's the tell. When you're present, you stop thinking about what to do next. The scene tells you. Your partner sighs and puts the mug down, and your body already knows to soften. You didn't decide. You responded. [short pause] When you're not present, you're in your head running options. "Should I heighten? Should I call back to the top? What would be funny here?" That little committee in your skull — that's the sound of absence.

[curious] Oh. [pause] That's uncomfortable, actually. Because I know that committee.

[warm] Everyone knows that committee. The committee isn't a flaw, it's what minds do when they're scared. Presence isn't the absence of the committee — it's choosing to put your attention somewhere else. On your partner's eyes. On the imaginary window. On the temperature of the room you just invented. You keep putting it there. That's the practice.

[curious] Okay. So presence is the internal thing. Where does commitment come in?

[teaching] Commitment is what presence looks like from the outside. If presence is happening inside you, commitment is what the audience sees. You make a choice and you *mean* it. Full voice. Full body. No air quotes.

[curious] Air quotes — what do you mean by that?

[emphatic] You know it when you see it. Somebody plays a character but they're slightly winking at the audience. "I'm doing a Southern grandma, isn't this fun." Their shoulders are still theirs. Their eyes are still asking if it's landing. That's air quotes. [pause] Commitment is when the Southern grandma walks in and you forget there was ever a performer there. The shoulders change. The breath changes. The *pause before she answers* is her pause, not yours.

[conversational] And you can feel the difference in the room.

[teaching] Immediately. The audience leans in, even if they don't know why. Because something real is happening. A human being made a choice and didn't apologize for it.

[curious] So here's my question. Can you have one without the other? Can you be present but not committed, or committed but not present?

[contemplative] Sort of, and it's instructive to look at both failure modes. [short pause] Present but not committed — that's the thoughtful player who's really *with* their partner, really listening, but every choice is small. Safe. They're there, but they're whispering. You feel care but you don't feel stakes.

[curious] And the other way?

[teaching] Committed but not present — that's the bulldozer. Big voice, big choices, full body. But they're not tracking their partner. They're performing *at* the scene instead of *in* it. It looks committed from ten feet away, but up close you notice their eyes aren't actually seeing anyone. They'd make the same choice no matter what you gave them.

[conversational] Huh. So the bulldozer is actually a presence problem wearing commitment clothes.

[emphatic] Yes. Exactly that. Real commitment can't be faked without presence, because real commitment is a *response* to something. It's the body saying yes to what's actually in the room. If nothing's in the room for you, the best you can do is volume.

[curious] Okay so these two things — they're linked. Presence is the source, commitment is the signal.

[teaching] Right. And here's the part that took me years to understand. Both of them are practices, not talents. Nobody is born present. Nobody is born committed. You build them the same way you build anything — repetition, noticing when you've drifted, and then the small brave act of coming back.

[curious] Coming back. Say more.

[warm] In any scene, you *will* drift. Your attention will catch on something — a laugh you wanted, a line you wish you hadn't said, a thought about the show last week. That's not failure. Failure is staying gone. The practice is noticing "oh, I'm not here anymore" and gently returning. To your partner's face. To the chair you're sitting in. To the breath in your chest. [pause] You'll do it fifty times in a ten-minute scene. That's not a bug. That *is* the skill.

[contemplative] And this is the thing that doesn't stay on stage, is it.

[teaching] No. This one bleeds into everything. The Tuesday morning meeting where you're half-listening and running your reply in your head — that's absence. The conversation with your partner where you're there but hedging, giving half of yourself because you're not sure it's safe — that's presence without commitment. [short pause] Every room you walk into is asking the same two questions. Are you actually here? And when you speak, do you mean it?

[conversational] Those are big questions.

[matter-of-fact] They are. But they're also small. You don't answer them once. You answer them every minute. The gift of improv is that it gives you a stage to practice on — a place where the cost of drifting is low and the reward for coming back is immediate. The audience laughs. Your partner's eyes light up. The scene finds its shape. You get feedback right away that says *yes, there you are*.

[curious] So if somebody's listening to this and they want to actually work on this — where do they start?

[teaching] Pick one. Not both. For a week, just practice noticing when you've left the scene. Don't fix it, don't judge it, just notice. "Oh. Gone again. Okay." The noticing is the whole exercise. [pause] Then the next week, work on the coming back. One breath. One look at your partner. That's it. [short pause] Commitment you can work on separately, but honestly — if you get the presence piece, the commitment starts showing up on its own. Because when you're really there, hedging stops making sense. Why would you half-mean something that's actually happening to you?

[contemplative] That last line is going to stick with me.

[warm] It's the whole thing, really. Presence makes commitment inevitable. And commitment is just the body telling the truth about where you are.
