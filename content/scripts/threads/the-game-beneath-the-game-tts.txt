[curious] Okay, so I want to talk about game at the mastery level. Because I think most of us get taught game like it's one skill. You find it, you heighten it, you rest, you heighten again. And that works. That gets you through a lot of scenes. But there's a ceiling you hit, right? Where you know you're doing it "correctly" and the scene still feels mechanical.

[teaching] Yeah. And the ceiling is usually because you're treating game as one thing. It isn't. It's a taxonomy. There are at least seven kinds of games, and they don't all behave the same way. If you heighten a status game the way you'd heighten a behavioral game, you're going to flatten it.

[curious] Wait, seven? Break that down for me.

[teaching] Sure. Behavioral game — a character has a tic, a habit, a way of moving through the world. Emotional game — the wrong feeling in the wrong place, like grief at a birthday party. Verbal game — a speech pattern, a word they can't stop saying, a rhythm. Physical game — the body is the engine, a posture or a gesture that keeps generating comedy. Status game — power is the pattern, who's up, who's down, who thinks they're up and isn't. Mapping game — you're treating one thing as another, the board meeting as a funeral, the dog as a teenager. And scenic game — the space itself is generating the pattern. A waiting room where nobody can stop apologizing.

[curious] Okay, so why does this taxonomy matter? I mean, isn't it still "find the unusual thing and do more of it"?

[teaching] [short pause] At the surface, yes. But each type heightens differently. A behavioral game heightens by escalation — the tic becomes more disruptive, more costly. A mapping game heightens by drawing more of the mapped world in — if the board meeting is a funeral, now someone's giving a eulogy, now someone's choosing hymns. A status game heightens by stakes, not by volume. If you try to heighten a status game by making the high-status person louder, you're not heightening, you're just yelling.

[conversational] Oh. Yeah. I've seen that scene. I've *been* in that scene.

[teaching] Everyone has. And rests work differently too. You can rest a behavioral game by just letting the character do normal things for thirty seconds. You cannot rest a scenic game that way, because the moment you stop playing the space, the game dies. Scenic games rest by shifting focus within the space, not by leaving it.

[curious] So knowing the type is like knowing which tool you're holding.

[teaching] Right. And the second thing — this is the part that took me years to really get — games don't just get bigger. They evolve.

[curious] What do you mean by evolve?

[teaching] There are four moves. A game can pivot. It can invert. It can combine. Or it can transcend. Pivoting is when the underlying pattern stays but the expression changes. Say the game is "this character can't receive a compliment." Pivot: now they can't receive an apology. Same pattern — inability to accept something offered — new surface.

[curious] So the engine is the same, but the fuel changes.

[teaching] Exactly. Inverting is when you reverse the mechanism. The character who can't receive compliments suddenly demands them. Same territory, opposite vector. The audience gets the rhyme without the repetition. Combining is when two games merge. You've got a verbal game going in one scene and a mapping game in another, and by the third beat they fuse into something that's neither one alone.

[curious] And transcending?

[teaching] [pause] Transcending is the one that matters most. That's when the mechanical pattern reveals an actual emotional truth underneath. The character who can't receive compliments — turns out it's because their mother only praised them when she wanted something. And now the game isn't a game anymore. It's a person.

[contemplative] That's the thing that separates a clever scene from a moving one.

[teaching] That's *exactly* the thing. And you can't force transcendence. You can't decide to do it. What you can do is stay open to it. Keep asking what the pattern means for this person, not just what it means for the laugh. And when it opens up, you follow it down. That's when people leave a show remembering a specific moment instead of just remembering they had a good time.

[curious] Okay. Third thing. Because I know there's a third thing.

[teaching] [warm] Yeah. Sometimes you need to break the pattern. And this is where improvisers get into trouble, because we all know the rule of three. Two iterations establish, the third subverts. Fine. Everyone learns that. The real discipline isn't knowing the rule — it's knowing *when*.

[curious] When's the wrong time to break?

[teaching] Too early, the audience feels cheated. You showed them a pattern, they started investing in it, and you yanked it before they got to enjoy recognizing it. They feel like they were set up and then ignored. The other wrong time is when you break for cleverness instead of truth. You subvert because you thought of a funny thing to subvert with. The audience can tell. It reads as abandonment. Like you got bored with your own scene.

[curious] So what's the right time?

[teaching] When the break reveals something the pattern alone couldn't. The character who's been cheerfully denying a problem for two beats — the third beat, they break, and we see the real cost. The pattern was hiding the pain. Breaking it shows us the pain. Now the audience feels something they couldn't have felt if you just kept going.

[conversational] It's almost like the pattern earns the break.

[teaching] That's the right way to say it. The pattern does work, and the work it does is making the break land. If the break comes free, it's worth nothing.

[curious] [short pause] Fourth thing.

[teaching] The analogous scene. This is the Harold's second-beat engine, but it's not just a Harold thing. It's the move where you take a game from one scene and transplant it into a completely different context. Different characters, different world, same underlying logic.

[curious] Give me one.

[teaching] First beat — a surgeon is casually lying to a patient about how the surgery went. The game is, let's say, a trusted professional performing calm while hiding disaster. Second beat, analogous — it's a wedding, the officiant has lost the rings, and they're improvising a ceremony with total confidence. Totally different scene. Same engine. Trusted professional performing calm while hiding disaster.

[curious] Oh that's nice. It's not the same joke, but the audience *feels* the connection.

[teaching] Yeah. And the reason that move is so powerful is that it proves the pattern is a *pattern*. One instance could be a quirk. Two instances across different worlds — now it's a theme. Now the show has a through-line. The audience didn't know they were watching a show about people performing competence through crisis until beat two told them.

[curious] What's the most common mistake on analogous scenes?

[teaching] Abstraction level. People abstract too literally — "the doctor who lies" becomes "the dentist who lies" — and it's not an analogous scene, it's the same scene with different props. Or they abstract too loosely — they keep only "professional" and drop the lying and the stakes, and the audience can't feel the echo. The sweet spot is abstract enough to generate a fresh situation, specific enough that when it lands, everyone in the audience feels the click of recognition without being able to articulate why.

[contemplative] Not "the doctor who lies." "A trusted professional who betrays trust."

[teaching] Exactly. That's the right altitude.

[curious] Okay. So taxonomy, evolution, breaking, analogous. What ties it all together?

[teaching] [pause] One question. The same question you've been asking since your first level one class. "If this is true, what else is true?"

[curious] Really? That's the master question?

[teaching] It never stops being the master question. What changes is the scope. At level one, you're asking it inside a single moment — if my scene partner just said their mother is a ghost, what else is true in this world? At level three, you're asking it across a scene — if this character is terrified of silence, what else is true about them? At level five, you're asking it across the whole show. If beat one established that professionals hide disaster, what else is true about this show? What's the show *about*, and what hasn't been said yet that needs to be?

[conversational] It's the same move. Just at a higher altitude.

[teaching] Yeah. And honestly, this is where improv stops being different from other art forms. Good novelists ask it. Good directors ask it. Good therapists ask it. The difference is we have to ask it in real time, standing up, with three other people.

[curious] [warm] I want to say one thing about the life piece, because I keep thinking about it while you're talking. This taxonomy thing — knowing which *type* of pattern you're in — that's a thing I wish I could do in my own head. Like, I'll be in a bad mood, and I'll heighten it the wrong way. I'll treat an emotional pattern like it's a behavioral one. I'll try to *do* something about it when what it needed was for me to just sit with it for thirty seconds.

[teaching] [warm] Yeah. And "if this is true, what else is true?" works in a marriage. It works in a meeting. It works in the conversation you're having with yourself at two in the morning. You're already doing it on stage. The question is whether you bring it with you when you step off.

[contemplative] [pause] The mechanical pattern, revealing the emotional truth underneath. That's not just a definition of transcendence in a scene. That's a definition of growing up.
