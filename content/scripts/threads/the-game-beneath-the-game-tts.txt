[matter-of-fact] Behavioral games, where a character has a tic or habit. Emotional games, where someone has an inappropriate feeling for the situation. Verbal games built on speech patterns. [teaching] They heighten differently, rest differently, and resolve differently. A behavioral game heightens by the behavior becoming more extreme or appearing in higher-stakes contexts. [contemplative] That's a huge shift. From "find the game and make it bigger" to "identify what kind of game this is and heighten it in the way that suits its type."

[conversational] A game can pivot, shifting to a new expression of the same underlying pattern. It can invert, where the mechanism reverses entirely. [serious] That last one. That's what separates a clever scene from a moving one.

[teaching] Game evolution is the advanced skill. Anyone can make a pattern bigger. The mastery is knowing when to let the pattern transform. When the audience has gotten the joke, and the scene needs to go somewhere deeper.
