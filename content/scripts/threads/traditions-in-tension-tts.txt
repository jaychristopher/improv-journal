[curious] Okay, so when someone says "improv," what are they actually talking about? Because I feel like I've been using that word for years like it means one thing.

[teaching] Yeah, that's the trap. There's no single improv. There are at least five major traditions, and they don't just have different exercises. They disagree on fundamental questions about what the form even is.

[curious] Five? Name them.

[matter-of-fact] Johnstone — Theatresports, Loose Moose, story-first, status as the engine. Spolin — Theater Games, the Second City lineage, present-moment awareness, the body as the instrument. Close and Halpern — iO, the Harold, group mind, improv as something close to spiritual practice. UCB — game-first, pattern recognition, comedy as the goal. And then Annoyance, TJ and Dave — commitment-first, honest behavior, trust the relationship and the game shows up or it doesn't.

[contemplative] Five lineages. Five different answers to "what are we doing up there."

[curious] Okay, here's the one I want to start with. Yes-and. Because everybody — every TED talk, every corporate workshop — yes-and is THE rule. That's universal, right?

[teaching] It's universal in the Chicago lineage. Spolin to Sills to Compass to Second City to iO to UCB. That whole genealogical tree treats yes-and as foundational. First thing you teach.

[curious] But?

[teaching] But Johnstone doesn't use the phrase. He writes about accepting offers and blocking. Which overlaps with yes-and, but it's not identical. And Mick Napier at Annoyance argues that yes-and actually paralyzes beginners.

[curious] Wait, paralyzes?

[warm] His point is that yes-and describes good work better than it teaches it. You watch a great scene and yes, the players are accepting and adding. But if you're a beginner trying to remember the rule mid-scene, you freeze. You're managing a checklist instead of being a person. Napier's alternative is just — commit fully. And the agreement takes care of itself.

[contemplative] So the principle is real. But the framing is contested.

[emphatic] Right. And that distinction matters because the framing is what gets taught. The framing is what becomes "the rule."

[curious] Let's do another one. Game of the scene. Because in the last five years it feels like everyone says "find the game, find the game."

[teaching] That's UCB. UCB puts game at the absolute center. The repeatable pattern of unusual behavior that you identify and heighten. That's the engine of every scene, in their view.

[curious] And the others?

[matter-of-fact] iO acknowledges game, but emphasizes organic discovery. Don't hunt for it — let it emerge. Johnstone doesn't use the concept at all. His narrative engine is reincorporation. You bring back what you planted. Annoyance says if you commit honestly, the game shows up on its own. And TJ and Dave have done hour-long shows without ever identifying a "game." Two people, one hour, no pattern hunt.

[curious] So is game the point, or a byproduct?

[teaching] That IS the debate. And it's not academic. If you train at UCB, you spend every class learning to spot the unusual thing and crank it. If you train with TJ and Dave, you spend every class learning to listen so completely that the scene becomes inevitable. Different students. Different muscles.

[short pause]

[curious] Okay, status. I always thought status was just... a thing improvisers know about.

[warm] Status is Johnstone. Almost entirely. He built his pedagogy around it. There's a full chapter in Impro about status transactions — the micro-negotiations of dominance and submission happening in every interaction, all the time. Every glance. Every pause before responding.

[curious] And the other traditions don't use it?

[teaching] Spolin barely touches it. She's interested in the body, the space, present-moment awareness. UCB treats status as one tool among many. Not the central lens. So when a therapist starts talking about "status games" in a client's relationship, they're channeling Johnstone — they probably don't know it, but they are. They're inside one tradition's worldview.

[contemplative] Which means the question is: is status the deep grammar of human interaction? Or is it one useful frame among several?

[emphatic] And Johnstone's answer and Spolin's answer would be very different.

[curious] What about presence? Because everyone talks about presence.

[teaching] Same word, completely different practices. Spolin's "Point of Concentration" is a specific technique — direct your attention to a defined focus while you play. It's almost mechanical in a beautiful way. Johnstone's spontaneity comes from surrendering control, which is a different kind of presence — it's letting go, not focusing in.

[curious] Those feel almost opposite.

[teaching] They are. And then Meisner's repetition exercise trains you to attend to your partner's actual behavior, moment to moment. And the mindfulness traditions, which keep getting cited in improv pedagogy now, frame presence as non-judgmental awareness. Four practices. One word.

[curious] So when someone says "you weren't present in that scene"...

[warm] You have to ask which presence they mean. Because the fix is different depending on the answer.

[short pause]

[curious] Editing. Tell me about editing.

[matter-of-fact] UCB teaches specific edit types. The time dash. The tag-run. The swinging door. There's a vocabulary. Annoyance prefers organic transitions — the scene ends when it ends, and the ensemble feels it together. Harold editing has its own formal convention, the sweep. And TJ and Dave's long-form two-person work doesn't edit in any traditional sense at all.

[curious] So is editing a craft or an instinct?

[teaching] That's the question. UCB says it's a craft — here are the techniques, learn them. Annoyance says it's an instinct that only develops through deep ensemble experience. You can't shortcut it. And both traditions produce great work. So it's not that one is right.

[contemplative] It's that they're training different things.

[emphatic] And they're training students to value different things.

[short pause]

[curious] Here's the big one. What is improv FOR?

[warm] This is where it gets interesting. Close called the Harold a spiritual endeavor. UCB calls it comedy. Spolin called it a way of life. Johnstone called it "a technique for rescuing creativity from education." And applied improvisers call it a toolkit for human interaction.

[contemplative] Those are five different answers to the same question.

[teaching] And here's the thing — the answer determines everything else. What you teach, what you value, what you measure success by. If improv is comedy, you measure laughs. If improv is spiritual, you measure connection. If it's a way of life, you measure how it changes you. If it's a creativity rescue, you measure what students dare to do that they didn't dare to do before.

[curious] And those measurements would point to completely different scenes as "good."

[emphatic] Completely different.

[short pause]

[curious] So why does this matter? Like, practically. I'm a player. I do shows. Why do I need to know any of this?

[warm] Because every improv book you read, every blog post, every workshop — comes from a tradition. Usually just one. And usually without saying so. When a business writer says "improv teaches us to yes-and," they're citing the Chicago lineage and they probably don't know it. When someone in your jam says "find the game," they're speaking UCB. When your teacher talks about status, they're channeling Johnstone. The traditions are not interchangeable.

[curious] So we're all walking around mixing dialects without realizing it.

[teaching] And sometimes the dialects contradict each other. You go to one workshop where the teacher says "stop hunting for the game, just be honest," and then a week later another teacher says "you missed the game in that scene, you have to identify it faster." And you think YOU are the problem. You're not the problem. You're standing between two traditions that disagree.

[contemplative] That's such a relief, actually. To know it's not you.

[warm] It's not you. It's the form. The form has factions. Knowing which one you're drawing from — and what the others would say instead — is the difference between understanding improv and citing it.

[curious] And I guess that's true off stage too, right? Every field has this. You think you're learning "psychology" or "leadership" and you're actually learning one school's version of it.

[teaching] Yeah. The discipline always looks unified from outside and looks like a war from inside. Improv is no different. The trick is to stop asking "what does improv say about this" and start asking "which tradition is this coming from, and what would the others say."

[contemplative] Because the disagreements are where the real knowledge lives.

[emphatic] The disagreements are the map.
