[curious] Wait, really? I thought improv was improv. Yes-and, make your partner look good, find the game. That's the deal, right?

[leaning in] Okay. Give me the five traditions.

[conversational] First, Johnstone. Keith Johnstone, Theatresports, Loose Moose in Calgary. Story-first. Status as the engine of all human interaction. Spontaneity through surrender of control.

[warm] Second, Spolin. Viola Spolin, Theater Games, the lineage that leads to Second City. Present-moment awareness. The body as the primary instrument. Her "Point of Concentration" technique for focusing attention during play.

[teaching] Third, Close and Halpern. Del Close, Charna Halpern, iO Theater, the Harold. Group mind. Connections across scenes. Improv as a spiritual practice.

[matter-of-fact] Fourth, UCB. Upright Citizens Brigade. Game-first. Pattern recognition and heightening. Comedy as the explicit goal.

[conversational] And fifth, the Annoyance and TJ and Dave lineage. Commitment-first. Honest behavior. Trust the relationship. Game emerges organically, or it doesn't.

[curious] So where do they actually disagree?

[teaching] Let's start with the most famous concept in improv. Yes-and. The Chicago lineage, which runs from Spolin through Second City through iO through UCB, treats yes-and as foundational. The first rule taught.

[contemplative] But not everyone agrees.

[conversational] Johnstone doesn't use the phrase. He writes about "accepting offers" and "blocking," which overlap with yes-and but aren't identical. Mick Napier at the Annoyance argues yes-and actually paralyzes beginners because it describes good work better than it teaches it. His alternative: commit fully and the agreement follows naturally.

[curious] So the principle is universal but the framing is contested.

[warm] Exactly. Everyone agrees you should accept your partner's reality. They disagree about whether "yes-and" is the best way to teach that acceptance.

[leaning in] What about game of the scene? Because that feels pretty central to how I learned improv.

[teaching] UCB places game at the center of all improv comedy. The repeatable pattern of unusual behavior that gets heightened. But iO acknowledges game while emphasizing organic discovery over explicit identification. Johnstone doesn't use the concept at all. His narrative engine is reincorporation, not pattern repetition.

[serious] And TJ and Dave performed for an hour at a time without ever identifying a "game."

[conversational] The Annoyance position is: if you commit honestly, the game takes care of itself. So the debate is really about whether game is the point of a scene, or a byproduct of doing other things well.

[curious] What about status? Because I read Johnstone's book and it's basically all status, all the time.

[warm] Johnstone built his entire pedagogy around status transactions. The micro-negotiations of dominance and submission in every human interaction. He devoted a full chapter of Impro to it. It's his central lens for understanding all human behavior.

[teaching] Spolin barely addresses status. Her interest is the body, the space, present-moment awareness. UCB treats status as one tool among many. So the question becomes: is status the deep grammar of human interaction, as Johnstone believes, or just one useful framework among several?

[contemplative] Same word, different practices. That's true for "presence" too, isn't it?

[conversational] Very much so. Spolin's Point of Concentration is a specific technique: directing attention to a defined focus during play. Johnstone's spontaneity comes from surrendering control, which requires a different kind of presence entirely. Meisner's repetition exercise trains attention to your partner's actual behavior, moment to moment. Mindfulness traditions frame presence as non-judgmental awareness.

[matter-of-fact] Four different practices, all called "presence."

[curious] What about editing? I know UCB has very specific edit types.

[teaching] UCB teaches the time dash, the tag-run, the swinging door. Named techniques with specific applications. Annoyance prefers organic transitions where the scene ends when it ends and the ensemble feels it. Harold editing uses sweep edits as a formal convention. And TJ and Dave's long-form two-person work doesn't use traditional editing at all.

[leaning in] So is editing a craft with learnable techniques, or an instinct you can only develop through deep ensemble experience?

[warm] Both. And which answer you give depends on which tradition you're coming from.

[curious] This is starting to feel really important. Because every improv book I've read, every workshop I've taken, they all speak with such authority. Like this is the way.

[serious] And that's exactly why this matters.

[teaching] Every improv book, blog post, and workshop comes from a tradition. Usually one tradition. When a business writer says "improv teaches us to say yes-and," they're citing the Chicago lineage and probably don't know it. When a therapist uses status games, they're channeling Johnstone. When someone says "find the game," they're speaking UCB.

[conversational] The traditions aren't interchangeable. Knowing which one you're drawing from, and what the others would say instead, is the difference between understanding improv and just citing it.

[contemplative] And what improv is for. That's the deepest disagreement.

[warm] Del Close called the Harold a spiritual endeavor. UCB calls it comedy. Spolin called improv a way of life. Johnstone called it a technique for rescuing creativity from education. Applied improvisers call it a toolkit for human interaction.

[teaching] The answer to "what is improv for" determines everything. What you teach. What you value. What you measure success by. There's no single right answer. But knowing which answer you're operating from, and which ones you're not, makes you a far more thoughtful practitioner.
