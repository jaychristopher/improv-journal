[conversational] We've been talking a lot about receiving. Accepting offers, staying present, integrating surprises. But now I want to flip it. Because you're always transmitting, whether you intend to or not. And the question is: what kind of signal are you putting out?

[teaching] Let me paint two scenes and you tell me which one is alive.

[curious] Go for it.

[conversational] Scene one. A husband and wife are doing their taxes. He finds a receipt he doesn't recognize. He looks at her. She looks at the table. He's scared to ask. She's scared he'll ask. Neither of them says anything for three seconds.

[pause]

[teaching] Scene two. A cyborg from the future bursts through a door. "I'm here to steal your toaster!" The other player blinks. "Why a toaster?" "Because in the future, all toasters are sentient and I need to save them before..." And now we're five lines deep in exposition and no one is connecting about anything.

[leaning in] The tax scene. Obviously the tax scene. But that's weird, right? Because on paper the cyborg scene has way more going on.

[warm] And that's exactly the point. The first scene is honest. Every signal is clear. He's afraid. She's hiding something. The audience reads it instantly because the emotions are real, or at least the performers are fully committed to them being real. No winking, no ironic distance. Just two people in a situation, behaving as that situation would actually make them behave.

[contemplative] And the cyborg?

[serious] The cyborg is clever. And cleverness is armor. If I'm a robot from the future, I can't really fail, because I was never really me up there. The irony is a shield. The complexity is a display. And every layer of cleverness adds noise to the signal.

[curious] So it's not that clever premises are bad. It's that they interfere with the transmission.

[teaching] Exactly. It's a transmission quality issue. When you're genuinely scared, your partner receives a clear signal. "He's scared." They can build on that. When you're performing scared while winking at the audience to show you know it's silly, the signal fractures. Your partner doesn't know if you're scared or joking. The audience can smell the fake.

[conversational] Think about how often this happens in real life. Someone asks how you're doing and you say "fine" in a tone that clearly means not fine. You've sent a contradictory signal. Your partner now has to decode which channel to trust. That decoding costs bandwidth and creates ambiguity.

[leaning in] Versus just saying "honestly, I'm having a rough day."

[warm] Clear signal. Your partner knows exactly where they are and what to do with it. Honesty gives your partner something solid to grab. Cleverness gives them nothing. You can't build on a joke that's aware it's a joke.

[pause]

[curious] Okay, so clarity matters. But you're also saying the signal needs to be simple?

[teaching] Even if the signal is perfectly clear, it also needs to be simple. Think of your partner, or your audience, or the person across the meeting table, as connecting via a slow modem. Limited bandwidth. If you send a complex, twisting plot with three layers of irony and an eighteenth-century literary reference, the file is too large. The modem chokes. Packet loss. They disengage.

[contemplative] But if you send "I'm sad because you ate my cake"...

[warm] Small file. Downloads instantly. Everyone understands. And because the channel is clear, the relationship has room to develop. That's where the real scene lives.

[conversational] This is counterintuitive because our egos want complexity. We want to be the smartest person in the room. In meetings, we reach for the elaborate framing to signal competence. On stage, beginners invent elaborate premises to show range. But complexity is a tax on everyone else's attention.

[curious] Keith Johnstone had that phrase, right? The cult of the obvious?

[teaching] "The more obvious an improviser is, the more original he appears." Obvious moves feel risky because they feel exposed. Complex moves feel safe because they have layers to hide behind. But the obvious choice leaves bandwidth for connection. The complex choice consumes it.

[leaning in] So a master walks on stage and says "happy birthday" and that's it.

[warm] And because the premise costs almost nothing to process, the entire scene can be about the relationship. Compare that to the cyborg toaster opener, where five minutes of exposition are required before anyone can interact about anything that matters.

[contemplative] Simplicity isn't lazy.

[teaching] Simplicity is generous. It means your partner can immediately receive what you're offering and build on it without cognitive overhead. In collaboration, on stage, in a meeting, in a marriage, high status comes from clarity, not complexity.

[conversational] There's an exercise for this. Do a scene using only one word per turn, alternating. "I." "Think." "We." "Should." "Leave." You can't overcomplicate. And stories still emerge.

[warm] And then try a mundane scene, folding laundry, washing dishes, with one rule. No jokes, no irony. Every line is emotionally honest. If it's boring, be bored. If something your partner says makes you feel something, feel it. Notice how compelling sincerity is when nothing is competing with it for bandwidth.
