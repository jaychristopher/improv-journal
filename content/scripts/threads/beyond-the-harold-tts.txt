[curious] So you've done your Harold. You've run the beats, found the game, made the callbacks land. And now there's this question sitting there — is this it? Is this what longform is?

[teaching] And the honest answer is no. The Harold is one format. A brilliant one, but one. It's like spending four years learning the sonnet and then discovering poetry.

[curious] That's a big reframe. Because in a lot of schools, the Harold isn't taught as *a* form. It's taught as *the* form.

[teaching] Right. And there's a reason for that. The Harold is a teaching tool as much as it is a performance structure. Del designed it to force you to practice every core skill at once. Opening. Group game. Scene work. Callbacks. Connections. Thematic thinking. You can't do a Harold without rehearsing all of it.

[conversational] It's like a gym circuit.

[warm] Exactly that. It's a gym circuit for improvisers. Which is why it's such a good training form. But nobody thinks the gym circuit is the sport.

[curious] [short pause] Okay. So what's the sport? Or — what are the sports?

[teaching] The longform landscape is much wider than any one school teaches. Let's walk through it. Start with the Armando.

[curious] The Armando. That's the monologue one.

[teaching] A monologist — usually a comedian, sometimes a guest — tells a true story from their life based on a suggestion. And the players pull scenes out of that story. Not the events of the story. The *feeling* of it. The texture. The characters lurking inside it.

[conversational] So instead of an opening game generating your material, a human being generating their actual life generates your material.

[warm] Yeah. And something happens when the source is real. The scenes get warmer. They get weirder in a more specific way. You're not improvising off an abstraction anymore, you're improvising off someone's actual Tuesday.

[curious] ASSSSCAT is an Armando.

[teaching] ASSSSCAT is the Armando. UCB has been running that show in New York and LA for decades. It's one of the most performed longform structures in the world, and most improvisers don't realize that's what they're watching.

[curious] [short pause] What's next — La Ronde?

[teaching] La Ronde. This one I love because it's sneaky. On the surface it looks structural — each scene shares one character with the next scene, and the chain eventually closes back on the first character. Mathematical. Tidy.

[conversational] So it's a daisy chain of two-handers.

[warm] Right. But what it actually is, is a character study. Because you watch the same person across three scenes — with their mother, with their boss, with a stranger on a train — and you see something the Harold almost never lets you see.

[curious] Which is?

[teaching] How someone changes depending on who they're with. The Harold is built around theme and game, so characters serve the idea. La Ronde inverts that. The theme emerges from watching a person code-switch through their own life.

[contemplative] [pause] That's a real thing off stage too. The version of you at work is not the version of you on the phone with your dad.

[warm] Right. And most of us pretend it's one self that's just showing different faces. La Ronde quietly argues the opposite — that you are partially assembled by the person across from you. Every scene partner is building a slightly different you.

[curious] [short pause] Okay. Narrative longform.

[teaching] Narrative is where improv tries to tell an actual story. Beginning, middle, end. Protagonist, complication, resolution. The engine isn't game — it's consequence. What happens in scene three is *caused* by what happened in scene two.

[conversational] That sounds hard.

[matter-of-fact] It's harder than it looks and easier than people think. Harder because you have to remember things. You have to track who wanted what and who got hurt and what promise hasn't been kept yet. Easier because the audience is meeting you halfway — they know how stories work, so you can lean on their instincts.

[curious] This is Johnstone territory, right?

[teaching] Very much. Johnstone's whole thing is the improviser walking backwards. You don't plan ahead, you reincorporate behind. Whatever you already put on the table is your next move. The gun on the mantel in scene one fires in scene five — not because you knew it would, but because you noticed it was still sitting there.

[emphatic] That's the discipline. Noticing what you've already made true.

[curious] [short pause] Then there's organic longform.

[teaching] Organic is the post-Harold move. No opening, no mandated beats, no group game requirement, no preset scene order. The form is whatever the show needs it to be tonight.

[curious] That sounds terrifying.

[warm] It's terrifying until it's the most natural thing in the world. Because here's the secret — when experienced players do a Harold, they're already ignoring half the structure. They're letting scenes run long when the scene is alive. They're cutting the group game when the group game isn't serving anything. Organic just makes the permission explicit.

[conversational] So it's what the Harold becomes when you stop pretending you're doing a Harold.

[teaching] That's exactly it. And it's what Del was actually aiming for. There's a quote — "the first rule is, there are no rules." People treat that like a slogan. It's not a slogan. It's a destination.

[contemplative] [pause] He wanted you to earn your way to no form.

[warm] He wanted you to internalize so much form that you didn't need it hung on the wall anymore.

[curious] [short pause] Two-person longform.

[teaching] The purest test of improv partnership. No ensemble to hide behind. No backline to edit you out when you're drowning. No group game to cover a slow start. Just you, one other person, and an hour.

[curious] TJ and Dave.

[warm] TJ and Dave. They've been doing it for twenty-plus years. One suggestion, one hour, full show. And what you learn watching them is that longform at its highest level is almost entirely about listening. They make almost no big moves. They just pay attention to each other so hard that the story assembles itself out of the paying attention.

[conversational] Middleditch and Schwartz brought that form to Netflix.

[teaching] They did. And it's worth watching those specials for a specific reason — they laugh. On stage. At each other. And it's not a bit. They're genuinely delighted, and the audience can *feel* that the delight is real.

[emphatic] Two-person forms reveal whether you actually like the person across from you. There's nowhere to hide it.

[curious] [short pause] And then genre formats.

[teaching] Genre formats use shared cultural literacy as structure. Film noir. Horror. Musical. The conventions of the genre become your scaffolding. You don't have to invent what the rules of the world are — the audience already knows.

[curious] So a horror improv show — everyone already knows someone's going in the basement.

[warm] Everyone knows someone's going in the basement. Everyone knows the dumb one dies first. Everyone knows the twist is coming in act three. You're not building the genre, you're inhabiting it, which frees you to do the actually hard work of being a specific person inside a familiar shape.

[conversational] Baby Wants Candy is the musical one.

[teaching] Baby Wants Candy. Over four thousand fully improvised musicals at this point. That's not a typo. Four thousand. Different title every night, new songs every night, full band, no writing. And it works because the musical as a form has such a strong spine that they can hang anything on it.

[curious] [short pause] Okay. So — six formats. Armando, La Ronde, narrative, organic, two-person, genre. Why does any of this matter if I already have a Harold I can do?

[teaching] Because each of these teaches you something the Harold can't. La Ronde teaches you that character is relational. Narrative teaches you consequence. Two-person teaches you attention. Organic teaches you to trust what the show is asking for instead of what the form is asking for. Genre teaches you how much you can borrow from the audience's own brain.

[warm] The Harold taught you the grammar. These formats teach you the dialects. And the point of learning dialects is never that one is better — it's that each one makes certain sentences possible that no other one can.

[contemplative] [pause] Same as life, honestly. You don't get one shape for being a person. You get a different shape for every room you walk into.

[emphatic] And the improvisers who stop at the Harold are the ones who think the gym circuit is the sport. The ones who go further — they figure out they were always training for a much bigger game.
