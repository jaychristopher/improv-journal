[curious] So you've done your Harold. You can run the beats, find the game, hit the connections. What comes next?

[teaching] Honestly? Everything else. [short pause] The Harold is a training format. It's brilliantly designed to make you practice every core skill at once — but it's one structure among many. Most experienced players eventually graduate into other forms.

[curious] Graduate feels like a strong word. It's not that the Harold stops working, right?

[warm] No, not at all. The Harold gave you grammar. [pause] What's out there now is dialects. Different forms that demand different skills, reward different strengths, reveal different truths about what improv can even be.

[curious] Okay, walk me through a few. Where would you start?

[teaching] Let's start with the Armando. You strip away all of Harold's structural complexity — the opening, the group game, the beats — and replace it with one thing. A monologist. A real person telling real stories from their life, and the team inspires scenes from what they just heard.

[conversational] It's the format behind ASSSSCAT at UCB. Probably the most performed longform in the world right now.

[curious] What does that change for the performer?

[teaching] Everything, because you're not mining an abstract word suggestion anymore. You're mining a human being. [short pause] Someone just told you about their dad's funeral, or the time they got fired, or a weird thing their neighbor does. Your job is to find what's *true* in that — and play it back in a way that surprises them.

[contemplative] It makes you a better listener off stage too. Armando trains the muscle of hearing what someone actually said versus what you expected them to say.

[curious] What else?

[teaching] La Ronde. [short pause] It's a character study disguised as a format. Each scene shares one character with the next scene. So you open with A and B. Next scene is B and C. Then C and D. Eventually the chain circles back to A.

[curious] Oh, that's clever. So every character shows up twice, with two different partners.

[warm] Exactly. And here's the thing — it reveals something the Harold almost never does. How the same person *changes* depending on who they're with. [pause] You are not one character. You're the version of you that exists in relation to this specific other human. La Ronde puts that under a microscope.

[contemplative] Which is a real thing. Think about how you are with your boss versus your best friend versus your mother. Same you. Completely different operating system.

[curious] Okay. What about story? Harold isn't really about story, is it?

[teaching] Not really. Harold runs on *game* — patterns, connections, heightening. Narrative longform runs on *consequence*. [short pause] What happens in scene three is caused by what happened in scene two. Beginning, middle, end. Somebody wants something, something gets in the way, something changes.

[curious] That sounds like a completely different muscle.

[emphatic] It is. This is Johnstone's territory. He talks about the improviser walking backwards — you reincorporate what you've already established. Everything that's happened has to matter. You can't just drop a choice and move on.

[conversational] Harold players sometimes struggle with this at first because they're trained to find the pattern and heighten it. Narrative asks you to find the *stakes* and honor them.

[curious] So where does that leave, like, pure freedom? What if you don't want *any* predetermined structure?

[teaching] Then you're doing organic longform. No mandated beats. No required group game. No preset scene order. The form emerges from the content.

[warm] This is actually what Del Close was pointing at. When he said "the first rule is there are no rules" — the Harold was a teaching tool on the way there. Organic is the destination. [short pause] You trust the ensemble, you trust the first suggestion, and whatever shape the show wants to take, you follow it.

[curious] That sounds terrifying.

[matter-of-fact] It is, until it isn't. [pause] Once you've played it a few times you realize structure was never the thing holding the show up. Attention was. Structure just gave you something to hide behind while you learned to pay attention.

[curious] Speaking of nowhere to hide — what about two-person longform?

[teaching] That's the purest test of improv partnership. No ensemble. No backline to tag you out. No group game to bail you out of a dying scene. Just two people and one suggestion for a full hour.

[confident] TJ and Dave built careers on this. Middleditch and Schwartz took it to Netflix. [short pause] When you watch them work, the thing you notice is how little they're *inventing*. They're mostly just agreeing with what the other person already half-committed to. Noticing a look. Honoring a breath.

[contemplative] Two-person form teaches you that improv isn't really about being clever. It's about being *present enough* that your partner never has to work alone.

[curious] That applies way past the stage.

[warm] Oh, completely. Any real relationship is two-person longform. You only get good at it by paying attention, not by having better material.

[curious] Last one — what about genre formats? Musical, noir, horror, all that.

[teaching] Genre formats use shared cultural literacy as the structure. Everyone in the room — players and audience — already knows the rules of a film noir or a haunted house movie or a Broadway musical. The conventions become the scaffold.

[conversational] Baby Wants Candy has done over four thousand fully improvised musicals. Four *thousand*. [short pause] That only works because nobody in the theater needs to be told what a musical is. The form is pre-installed in all of us.

[curious] So the skill shift there is...

[teaching] Fluency in the language you picked. If you're doing noir, you need to actually know noir — the voiceover, the femme fatale, the moral compromise, the rain. Genre formats punish people who only know the parody version. They reward people who love the real thing.

[contemplative] Which is a nice lesson by itself. [pause] You can't riff well on something you don't actually respect.

[curious] So if someone's standing at this crossroads — Harold done, what now — what do you tell them?

[warm] Try all of them. [short pause] Each format is a different question the art form is asking you. Armando asks, can you listen to a real person? La Ronde asks, do you know who you are in relationship? Narrative asks, can you honor consequence? Organic asks, can you trust attention alone? Two-person asks, can you be fully present with one other human? Genre asks, what do you actually love?

[emphatic] The Harold taught you the grammar. [pause] These formats — they're how you find out what you actually have to say.
