[conversational] You know the feeling. The scene ends and you know it didn't work. You can feel it in your body. But when someone asks "what happened?" all you can say is "I don't know, it just... died."

[teaching] That feeling, knowing something failed without being able to name it, is the vocabulary gap that keeps intermediate improvisers stuck. So let's fill it.

[curious] Okay, give me the vocabulary.

[serious] Every scene failure falls into one of three collapse modes. Latency, fracture, or decay.

[leaning in] Three modes. That's it?

[teaching] That's it. Let's go through them. Latency means the responses came too slow or landed on the wrong beat. Someone retreated into their head, what we'd call internal computation, and by the time they resurfaced, the scene had moved on. The rhythm broke. The energy dropped. Both players felt the lag but couldn't name it.

[contemplative] Latency is the collapse mode of the overthinker.

[conversational] Exactly. Now, fracture. This is when two players end up in different scenes. One thought they were having a serious conversation. The other thought they were doing a bit. The signals diverged somewhere, an offer was missed or misread, and the shared reality split.

[curious] And neither player can see it because they're both inside their own version.

[warm] Right. They can't figure out why it feels off because they can't see the scene from outside. And then there's decay. The scene goes thin. Details stop mattering. Earlier threads are dropped. Nothing accumulates. The scene doesn't break or lag. It just fades.

[contemplative] Like a campfire that nobody's feeding.

[teaching] Perfect analogy. Now, within those modes, specific antipatterns are usually at work. And this is where it gets really useful for self-coaching.

[leaning in] Walk me through them.

[serious] Performing cleverness. You're crafting a joke instead of responding to your partner. The audience gets a punchline but the scene gets nothing. This is the intermediate trap. You're skilled enough to be funny and it's killing your scenes.

[curious] That's brutal. What else?

[conversational] Hesitation. The gap between impulse and action. You had the response but you second-guessed it. The moment passed. In an irreversible system, the scene registers that absence. There's a hole where your response should have been.

[teaching] Bulldozing. You're driving your agenda regardless of what your partner offers. You're in the scene but not in the same scene. You're generating but not receiving.

[contemplative] And then there's steering, which sounds like bulldozing but subtler?

[warm] Much subtler. You're accepting your partner's offers on the surface, but redirecting everything toward where you want the scene to go. You look collaborative. But you're not. Your partner can feel it even if they can't articulate it.

[curious] What about when scenes just get too heavy? Too much going on?

[teaching] Overcomplication. You added a fourth element when two would have served. You introduced a new character when the relationship needed deepening. You invented plot when the game needed heightening. The scene got heavy and stopped moving.

[serious] And finally, judgment. You're evaluating the scene while you're in it. "This isn't working." "My partner's choices are weird." "I should have said something different." The evaluation circuit fires and the creation circuit shuts down.

[leaning in] You can't build and audit simultaneously.

[warm] You really can't. The moment you step outside the scene to judge it, you've left the scene. You're a critic, not a player.

[curious] So how do you actually use this vocabulary? Like, after a scene?

[teaching] Three questions. First: which collapse mode was it? Latency, fracture, or decay? Second: which antipattern was operating? Was I computing, performing cleverness, hesitating, bulldozing, steering, overcomplicating, or judging? Third, and this is the one that turns diagnosis into practice: what would the opposite behavior have looked like?

[contemplative] The opposite behavior. So if I was hesitating, the opposite is committing to the first impulse.

[conversational] If I was overcomplicating, the opposite is stripping down to the simplest version. If I was judging, the opposite is staying inside the scene and building.

[warm] The vocabulary isn't just for naming what went wrong. It's for seeing what to try next. That's what makes it a tool and not just a taxonomy.
