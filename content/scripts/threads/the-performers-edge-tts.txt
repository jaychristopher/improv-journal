[curious] So there's this shift that happens, right? You're a few years in, you know the techniques — find the game, heighten, edit, support — and then one day the question just... changes on you.

[teaching] Yeah. It stops being "how do I do this" and it becomes "who am I when I do this." And that's a completely different conversation. The first one has answers. The second one has a lifetime.

[curious] Okay, so let's start where it actually starts. Performance state. Because I think people imagine the pros out there, calm, unbothered, and the rest of us are backstage sweating through our shirts.

[teaching] [warm] The secret is they're sweating too. The physiology of stage fright and stage readiness — it's basically the same. Same adrenaline, same heart rate, same tight chest. The difference is entirely in the head.

[curious] Wait, really the same?

[matter-of-fact] Really the same. There's actual research on this. What separates a performer who thrives from one who freezes isn't the size of the wave — it's what they call it. Anxiety reappraised as excitement. Same energy, different name, totally different experience.

[curious] [short pause] Huh. So it's a relabeling thing.

[teaching] It's a relabeling thing, and it's a redirection thing. Here's the other half. When you're anxious on stage, all that attention is pointed inward. How do I look? What did I just say? Why did that land weird? The experienced performer takes that exact same surveillance system and aims it at their partner. Watching their face. Hearing their breath. Catching the micro-shift when they pivot.

[curious] So the anxiety doesn't go away. It just gets a job.

[emphatic] It gets a job. Yes. That's it exactly. They don't eliminate the adrenaline — they ride it. The energy that was going to eat them becomes the energy that wakes them up.

[curious] Okay. And this isn't just a stage thing, is it? Because I feel this before meetings. Before hard conversations.

[warm] Same mechanism. The Tuesday morning presentation hits your body the same way a Harold hits mine. And the move is the same — stop scanning yourself, start watching the room. Anxiety redirected outward becomes attention. Attention is a gift to whoever you're with.

[curious] [pause] Alright. So that's the foundation. What about the audience? Because this is the thing I keep getting wrong. I keep treating them like a jury.

[teaching] Most people do. And that's the trap — playing AT them. Pushing a joke out, waiting to see if it clears the bar, adjusting the next joke based on the verdict. It's transactional. And the audience can feel it.

[curious] What's the alternative? Because they ARE watching.

[confident] They're not watching. They're co-creating. Their laughter, their silence, the restlessness when the scene's dragging, the held breath when something real is happening — that's all live feedback, and it's shaping the show whether you acknowledge it or not. The question is whether you let them in on the fact that you know.

[curious] What does that look like in practice?

[teaching] It looks like including them in the discovery. You get a suggestion — "dentist" — and instead of treating that as raw material you go off and mold privately, you hold it up with them. The suggestion is a social contract. They said "dentist," and now we're all finding out together what a scene about a dentist could be. They're not judging the result. They're inside the result with you.

[curious] [contemplative] That reframes the whole thing. They're not the customer. They're a player.

[warm] They're a player who can't speak but whose reactions are part of the text. And once you feel that — really feel it — the show gets a lot less lonely. You stop performing and start playing with a very large, very quiet scene partner.

[curious] Okay. Next one. And this is maybe the hardest. Failing forward. Because I can tell you what the books say — commit, recover, metabolize it — but in the actual moment when something dies, I freeze.

[teaching] Everyone freezes the first hundred times. And then something shifts. You start to notice that the recovery is almost always more interesting than the original plan would have been.

[curious] Say more about that.

[emphatic] [short pause] Del Close said "follow the fear." Susan Messing said "if you're not having fun, you're the asshole." Those sound like different notes, but they're pointing at the same door. The impulse to retreat from a failing scene — that pull backward — that's exactly where the material is. Unskilled performers pull away from it. Skilled performers lean in. Commit harder. Name what's happening. Get emotional. Make the failure the scene.

[curious] The failure becomes the scene.

[teaching] The failure becomes the scene. And here's the thing the audience knows that performers forget — they don't want perfection. Perfection is boring. They want to see you caught out and find your way back. That's the whole drama of live performance. Can this person, with nothing, handle what just happened? When you say yes to that question, you become watchable in a way no amount of clever premise writing will ever get you.

[curious] [pause] I think this is the life lesson I keep almost learning.

[warm] It's the big one. The move you avoid in the scene is the move you avoid at the dinner table, in the email you haven't sent, in the conversation with your kid. The recovery is more compelling than the original plan. That's true on stage and it's true off.

[curious] Okay. Last one. And this one scares me a little, honestly. Finding your voice.

[teaching] Why scares you?

[curious] Because I don't know if I have one. And I don't know how you'd know.

[warm] [contemplative] You can't design it. That's the first thing. You don't sit down and decide "my voice is going to be emotionally grounded with absurdist undertones." People try. It never works. [curious] Examples. Give me examples.

[teaching] Sure. TJ Jagodowski — patience. Emotional depth. He'll sit in a moment three beats longer than feels safe, and that's where his magic lives. Razowsky — almost spooky present-moment awareness, like he's hearing frequencies you can't. None of those came from a whiteboard. They came from thousands of shows.

[curious] So voice is what's left over.

[emphatic] Voice is what remains when you stop trying to be someone else. That's the actual definition. It's the residue of having burned off every impression, every hero imitation, every "what would a good improviser do here" — until what's left is just what you actually do.

[curious] [short pause] But there's a tension there, right? Improv is collaborative. We say "serve the scene." Doesn't strong individual voice fight that?

[teaching] It feels like it should. It doesn't. The best ensembles aren't interchangeable parts — they're distinctive voices in conversation. Think of any great group you've seen. You can tell who's who blindfolded. That's not ego. That's an ensemble made of actual people.

[curious] So your voice feeds the ensemble rather than competing with it.

[warm] It feeds it. A monochrome team is worse than a team where everyone brings a different color. Serving the scene doesn't mean disappearing. It means showing up as fully yourself and trusting that your self is useful.

[curious] [pause] Okay. One more thing. Because I've been sitting with this. How do you tell voice from schtick? Because some of my "voice" might just be... moves I do.

[confident] That's the cleanest question in this whole conversation, and there's a cleaner answer than you'd think. Voice adapts to context. Schtick ignores context.

[curious] Oh.

[teaching] Voice is the values and instincts you bring to every scene — they show up differently in a divorce scene than in a spaceship scene, but they're still there. Schtick is the move you pull regardless of what the scene needs. One is artistry. The other is a habit wearing artistry's clothes.

[curious] And the test is whether it bends.

[emphatic] The test is whether it bends. If your thing works in every context, it's voice. If your thing keeps showing up whether the scene wants it or not, you're doing schtick and calling it style.

[curious] [contemplative] That's brutal.

[warm] It's brutal and it's freeing. Because once you see the difference, you can stop defending the habits and start trusting the instincts. And that's the whole arc, really. Performance state, audience, failure, voice — they're all the same project. They're all about becoming someone who can stand on a stage with nothing and be fully present as yourself. [pause] The techniques get you on stage. This is what keeps you there.
