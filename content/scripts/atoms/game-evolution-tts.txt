[teaching] Exactly. The game pivoted but the engine stayed the same. And this is what keeps scenes alive when you've explored the original expression. You've done all the Super Bowl moves you can think of, and instead of forcing one more, you let the game breathe and find a new home.

[matter-of-fact] That's the whole risk. Pivot too early and it reads as dropping the game. Your scene partner is building something and you just walked away from it. Pivot too late and the scene died three lines ago while you were squeezing a dry sponge.

[contemplative] The instinct you're developing is sensing when an expression is explored — not exhausted, but explored. There's still juice in it, but you've made the point. That's pivot time.

[warm] Inversion. This one's beautiful. The pattern reverses. Think about the chronic apologizer — they've been apologizing for everything, escalating absurdly, apologizing for the weather, apologizing for gravity. [emphatic] Right. The game ate itself. And that moment always gets a huge response because the audience feels the architecture fold in on itself. They were tracking the pattern, and now the pattern is looking at itself in the mirror.

[conversational] Status inversions are a specific version of this. The boss who's been dominating the scene suddenly needs something from the intern. The power flips and the game evolves through reversal instead of escalation. The scene gets new oxygen without starting a new game.

[curious] The third one you mentioned is combination?

But it happens within single scenes too. Two characters each have their own game, and at some point those games start interacting. The collision produces something neither game had alone.

[conversational] It's like counterpoint in music. Two melodies that are each interesting on their own, and then you play them simultaneously and this third thing emerges.

[curious] And the fourth one — transcendence?

[contemplative] This is the one that changes how you think about game entirely.

[teaching] Transcendence is when the game stops being a mechanical pattern and becomes emotionally real. The apologizer isn't just doing a bit anymore. Somewhere in the heightening, you realize — oh. [curious] That's the blowout.
