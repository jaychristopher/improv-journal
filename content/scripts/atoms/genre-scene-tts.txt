[curious] Genre Scene. So you're doing improv in the style of film noir or horror or whatever?

[teaching] Yes, but there's an important distinction. This is not parody. The key instruction is "play it straight." Commit to the genre rather than commenting on it or winking at the audience. [short pause] Half-hearted noir is just a funny voice. Committed noir is a world the audience enters.

[conversational] How do you set it up?

[matter-of-fact] The ensemble picks a genre. Film noir, romantic comedy, horror, Western, sci-fi, Shakespeare, whatever. You briefly discuss the conventions. Tropes, character types, language patterns, physical vocabulary, typical settings, narrative structures. Then you perform scenes within that genre.

[curious] What skills does this actually develop? It seems like it could be a gimmick.

[emphatic] Four specific things. First, specificity. Genre demands specific choices. Noir requires vocabulary like "the rain came down like God was trying to drown his regrets." It requires character types, tonal commitment. It forces you out of vague, generic scene work. [short pause] Second, commitment. Playing genre authentically means going all the way. That muscle transfers to everything. Third, shared vocabulary. When the ensemble shares genre knowledge, they share expectations that work like a second language. Fourth, heightening through convention. Genre conventions are pre-built heightening structures. Horror escalates naturally. Rom-com has built-in arc.

[contemplative] What goes wrong?

[serious] The biggest failure is lazy parody. Players play cliches instead of genuine conventions. They comment on the genre instead of living in it. [short pause] The side-coaching for that is direct. "You're commenting on the genre, not living in it." "Commit more." "What does this world sound like?"

[curious] What if someone doesn't know the genre well?

[warm] That's a real practical issue. Genre knowledge is unevenly distributed. The exercise can accidentally exclude performers unfamiliar with a genre's tropes. [short pause] The fix is to spend real time on that pre-scene discussion. Make sure everyone has enough shared vocabulary to play. And choose genres the group actually knows.

[emphatic] When it works, genre is one of the most powerful tools in improv. It gives the ensemble a shared blueprint that makes every scene structurally richer. The audience gets a world, not just a conversation.
