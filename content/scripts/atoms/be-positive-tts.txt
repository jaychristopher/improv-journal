[curious] "Be Positive." So this is the Yes And principle, right? Just agree with everything?

[matter-of-fact] Not quite. "Positive" doesn't mean happy. A scene about a divorce can be great improv. This is mathematical positivity, addition not subtraction. [short pause] It's Yes And understood as a systems command rather than a social nicety.

[conversational] A systems command? What system?

[teaching] The system of shared reality. When your partner makes an offer, that offer becomes part of the world you're building together. If you reject it, even subtly, the shared reality forks. Two incompatible worlds now compete for the audience's belief, and neither wins.

[curious] Can you give me a concrete example?

[warm] Tina Fey's classic from Bossypants. Someone says "Freeze, I have a gun." If you respond "That's not a gun, that's your finger," you've fractured the reality. But if you say "The gun I gave you for Christmas! You bastard!" [short pause] state preserved, state extended, relationship and emotion established in a single line.

[emphatic] That's the difference. One response destroys. The other builds.

[conversational] But here's what I struggle with. What if my partner gives me something I hate? Like, "you killed my dog"?

[contemplative] And that's exactly why blocking happens. People block not because they fail to process data, but because acceptance is painful. Accepting "you killed my dog" or "you're my father and you abandoned me" requires vulnerability. [short pause] Blocking is almost always a fear response. Which is why Be Brave is a prerequisite for this principle.

[curious] What about accepting things at different levels of intensity?

[teaching] Great question. Acceptance has amplitude. You can accept reluctantly, where the offer is real but you resist its implications. You can accept fully, receiving and extending with energy. Or you can do what Johnstone calls overaccepting, taking a small offer and treating it as enormous. [short pause] Someone says "it's Tuesday" and you panic because that's when your surgery is scheduled. All three are positive. What matters is that the shared state is preserved.

[conversational] But doesn't always saying yes make you passive?

[confident] This is Mick Napier's important counter-frame. Yes And as a first principle can produce weak, passive improvisation if the performer has nothing of their own to bring. [short pause] His alternative: enter with a strong personal commitment, and acceptance becomes natural because you're grounded enough to receive anything. Acceptance without investment is compliance. Acceptance with investment is collaboration.

[curious] And one more thing. Does "yes and" mean you have to do whatever your partner tells you?

[warm] Critical clarification. Yes And means accepting the reality, not the command. "Jump off this cliff" can be met with "It's a high cliff, I'm too scared to jump." You accepted the physics. You kept your agency. That's still yes and. [pause] As Johnstone put it: "Those who say yes are rewarded by the adventures they have." Reject the input, and the adventure dies.
