[teaching] It's math. Seriously. Think of it as mathematical positivity — addition, not subtraction. A scene about a brutal divorce can be extraordinary improv. A scene about a funeral can be the best thing you do all year. "Positive" has nothing to do with the emotional weather. It means you're building on what exists instead of dismantling it.

[conversational] And actually, before it's even a mental decision, it's a body thing. Your partner establishes something — "Mom, I found the letters" — and there's this split second where it either lands in your body or it bounces off. When it lands, you can feel your posture adjust. Your breathing shifts. Your attention moves toward them.

[curious] And when it doesn't land?

[matter-of-fact] Tension. You feel yourself brace against the offer. Your shoulders tighten. Your brain starts problem-solving instead of receiving. That tension is the signal. That's the moment you need to override.

[contemplative] Because blocking isn't really a processing failure, is it? It's not that you didn't hear the offer.

[warm] No. You heard it fine. You just didn't want it. Accepting "you killed my dog" or "you're my father and you abandoned me" — that requires vulnerability. Blocking is almost always a fear response. Which is why being brave comes first. You can't accept what you're too scared to feel.

[curious] Okay so give me the mechanics. What actually breaks when someone blocks?

[teaching] The shared reality forks. That's the architecture underneath all of this — you and your partner are building one world together. When you reject an offer, even subtly, now there are two incompatible worlds competing for the audience's belief. And neither one wins.

[conversational] There's that great example from Tina Fey. "Freeze, I have a gun." And someone responds, "That's not a gun, that's your finger."

[emphatic] Fracture. Done. The audience doesn't know what's real anymore, and worse, neither do the players.

[curious] Versus?

[warm] "The gun I gave you for Christmas! You bastard!" One line. State preserved, state extended, and now we have a relationship, an emotion, a history. That's mathematical positivity. You took what existed and added to it.

[curious] But there are different ways to add, right? Like, acceptance isn't just one move.

[teaching] Right, it has amplitude. You can accept reluctantly — the offer is real but you resist its implications. You can accept fully — receive it and extend with energy. Or you can overaccept. Someone says "it's Tuesday" and you panic because that's when your surgery is scheduled. A tiny offer, treated as enormous.

[conversational] All three are positive. What matters is that the shared state is preserved.

[curious] Here's where I used to get confused though. If someone says "jump off this cliff" — am I supposed to just jump?

[confident] No. And this is the clarification that changes everything. "Yes, and" means accepting the reality, not the command. "It's a high cliff... I'm too scared to jump." You accepted the physics. You accepted the danger. You kept your agency. That's still yes-and.

[contemplative] That applies to your own offers too, by the way. The word that escaped your mouth before you could stop it, the weird position you stumbled into — accept what you already did. Don't try to backtrack. The backtrack always costs more than the original accident.
