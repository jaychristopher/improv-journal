[curious] "Emotional Truth." How is this different from Be Honest? They sound like the same thing.

[teaching] Emotional truth is a quality of Be Honest. It's what emerges when techniques like active listening, commitment, and presence are all working together. [short pause] Meisner's formulation captures it: "Living truthfully under imaginary circumstances."

[conversational] Living truthfully under imaginary circumstances. So the circumstances are fake but the feelings are real?

[warm] That's the core of it. And there's an important distinction. Emotional truth refers to the performer's commitment to the character's internal state. The character may be lying, hiding, or deflecting, but the performer is honestly playing that lie, that hiding, that deflection. [short pause] The honesty lives in the commitment, not in the character's words.

[curious] Where does emotional truth start? In the mind?

[emphatic] In the body. Always the body first. If your character is scared, let the fear live in your posture, your breathing, your eye contact before you speak about it. [short pause] The body commits faster than the mind. If you wait for the mind to decide whether fear is the right choice, performing cleverness will fill the gap.

[conversational] So instead of asking "what should I feel," you ask "what am I already feeling"?

[teaching] Exactly. There are two modes of this check. Before the scene, you scan your body. Where is the tension, the energy, the impulse? Let that inform your entry. During the scene, you notice what's already happening. Your shoulders are tight. Your stomach dropped. You're leaning away. [short pause] The feeling is already there. Your job is to stop suppressing it.

[curious] And then specificity comes in?

[confident] Specificity is what makes emotional truth legible. "I'm angry" is a report. "My hands are shaking because you promised you'd be here and you weren't" is truth. [short pause] That second version aligns every channel, voice, body, and words, around a single truth. The first leaves everyone guessing.

[conversational] What does emotional truth feel like from the inside versus the outside?

[contemplative] From the inside, it feels like mild risk and physical activation. The sensation of being seen without armor. [short pause] From the outside, it reads as behavioral congruence. Voice, body, and words all saying the same thing. The audience doesn't detect your feelings directly. They detect whether your channels are aligned. That's why specificity matters. It's not detail for detail's sake. It's congruence made legible.

[curious] Does emotional truth mean locking into one emotion?

[serious] The opposite, actually. Locking into one emotion and defending it against change is rigidity, not truth. If your partner's offer genuinely changes how you feel, the truthful move is to follow the feeling, not to protect the one you started with. [short pause] This is where Be Changeable and emotional truth converge.

[warm] There's a vulnerability contract here. When you play an emotion straight, no winking, no hedging, you offer something real. Cleverness protects you but muddies the signal. Truth exposes you but gives everyone solid ground to stand on. [pause] The counter-position is Diderot's Paradox: the great actor feels nothing and portrays emotion through pure craft. Improv's tradition sides firmly with Meisner. Feel it for real.
