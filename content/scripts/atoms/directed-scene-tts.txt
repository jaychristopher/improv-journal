[curious] Have you ever been in a scene where someone from the side just... calls something out? Like, mid-scene, you hear "slower" or "it's about the relationship" — and suddenly the whole scene shifts?

[teaching] Trains. It's one of those exercises that feels chaotic the first time and then becomes one of the most useful things you've ever done on stage.

[curious] Walk me through the setup.

[teaching] Simple. Two or more people start a scene. A director watches from the side and calls out directions in real time. "Faster." "What do you want?" "Heighten that." And you just... do it. No discussion, no negotiation. You hear the call, you integrate it, and the scene keeps going.

[curious] That sounds like it would break every scene.

[teaching] That's what you'd think. But here's what actually happens — and this is the counterintuitive part — the scenes often get better. Because the director is seeing what you can't see from inside it. You're so deep in your choice that you've stopped listening to your partner. You're so committed to the bit that you've forgotten there's a relationship underneath it. The call from outside is a gift. It's someone saying "hey, the interesting thing is over here."

[pause]

[contemplative] And it teaches you something you can't learn any other way. How to adjust without stopping.

[curious] What do you mean?

[teaching] In most of improv training, when something isn't working, you either push through it or the scene ends and you get a note afterward. Trains puts the note inside the scene. You have to metabolize direction in real time. Somebody says "be more affected" and you can't go "okay, let me think about what that means." You just have to let it hit you. Your body figures it out before your brain does.

[curious] That's a totally different skill than getting notes after a set.

[emphatic] Completely different. Notes after a set are intellectual. You process them, you file them away, you try to remember them next time. Direction inside a scene is physical. It rewires your instincts. You start building the muscle of receiving input and translating it into behavior instantly.

[short pause]

[curious] Okay but here's my question. Doesn't it make you dependent on the director? Like, if you always need someone telling you to slow down or find the relationship — aren't you just outsourcing your own awareness?

[teaching] That's real. Spolin actually drew a sharp line between side-coaching and directing. Side-coaching redirects your focus — "notice the space object," "listen to each other." It leaves the creative choice to you. Directing controls the action — "pick up the cup," "sit down." Trains uses both, and yeah, if the balance tips too far toward directing, performers can start waiting for permission instead of making choices.

[contemplative] But here's the thing. The goal isn't to need the director forever. The goal is to internalize the director. You do this exercise enough times and you start hearing those calls in your own head. You're mid-scene and something in you says "slower" or "what do I actually want right now?" That's the exercise working.

[curious] You're training an internal side-coach.

[warm] Exactly. You're building a second channel of awareness that runs alongside your scene work. One part of you is in the scene, living it. Another part is watching, adjusting, staying responsive.

[pause]

[curious] What makes a good director in this exercise? Because I've definitely seen it done badly.

[serious] Oh, a bad director ruins it. Contradictory calls, calls that come too fast, calls that are about the director's vision instead of what the scene needs. The director has to be watching the same scene the performers are in, not the scene they wish was happening.

[teaching] The best directors I've worked with give calls that are questions disguised as instructions. "What do you want?" isn't really a direction. It's a redirect. It says "you've been doing a lot of activity but I don't know what's driving you." The performer hears it and suddenly has to make a choice they were avoiding.

[curious] That's kind of beautiful. The direction creates a gap and the performer fills it.

[warm] And the performer fills it with something they already had. That's the part people miss. The director isn't adding anything from outside. They're pointing at what's already there that the performer hasn't committed to yet.

[short pause]

[curious] Where does this show up off stage?

[contemplative] Everywhere you're mid-flow and reality intervenes. You're giving a presentation and someone asks a question that redirects the whole thing. You're in a conversation and someone says something that changes the temperature in the room. You're executing a plan and the circumstances shift. The question is always the same — can you integrate new information without stopping, without getting brittle, without clinging to what you had planned?

[teaching] Most people, when they get an unexpected input, they either ignore it and barrel forward, or they freeze and reset. Trains builds the third option. You absorb it and keep moving. The scene continues. You're different now, but the scene continues.

[pause]

[curious] There's something about ego in this too, right? Like, you have to let go of your plan for the scene the second that call comes in.

[emphatic] Every single time. "Switch characters." Okay, your whole internal architecture for this scene? Gone. Rebuild from whatever your partner is giving you right now. "Start over with what you know now." Everything you've built, but from the top, and it's going to be different this time because you can't unknow what the scene just taught you.

[teaching] That's the ego dissolution piece. You practice letting go of ownership mid-stride. Not at the end when it's comfortable, not in reflection when you can frame it as a choice — right now, while you're holding the thing you built, someone says "let go" and you do.

[contemplative] And you discover the scene doesn't collapse. It often gets sharper. Because the thing you were holding onto was comfortable, not necessary.

[long pause]

[warm] The real lesson of Trains is that responsiveness isn't weakness. Adjusting to outside input doesn't make you less of an artist. It makes you more of one. The improvisers I admire most aren't the ones with the strongest plans. They're the ones who can hear "it's about the relationship" and let everything else fall away in a heartbeat.

[curious] The ones who've internalized the director.

[teaching] The ones who don't need the call anymore — but who remember what it felt like to receive one, and stayed open to it anyway.
