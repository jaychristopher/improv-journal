[curious] Directed Scene. This one sounds like it could be chaotic. What's the setup?

[teaching] Two or more performers start an improvised scene. Normal scene. But there's a director watching, and they call out directions in real time as the scene unfolds. The performers have to integrate those directions instantly, without stopping, without discussing it, while keeping the scene's emotional and narrative thread alive.

[conversational] What kind of things does the director call out?

[matter-of-fact] It ranges. Pacing calls like "Slower" or "Faster" or "Pause." Focus calls like "It's about the relationship" or "What do you want?" Structure calls like "Heighten that" or "Call back to the beginning." Character calls like "Switch characters" or "Be more affected." Even edit calls like "That's your edit point" or "New scene, same dynamic."

[contemplative] That's a lot to process while you're in the middle of a scene.

[emphatic] That's the point. It trains four things simultaneously. [short pause] Adaptability, because you're adjusting in real time with no warning. External awareness, because you have to keep one ear open while staying present. Ego dissolution, because taking direction mid-scene means letting go of your own plan. And concept-to-execution speed, because you have to translate something abstract like "make it about the relationship" into a specific behavioral change within seconds.

[curious] What's the difference between side-coaching and directing here?

[teaching] Great question. Viola Spolin drew a clear line. Side-coaching guides attention. "Focus on the space object." Directing controls action. "Pick up the cup." [short pause] True side-coaching redirects the performer's focus while leaving creative decisions to them. This exercise actually uses both, and that's fine for training purposes.

[conversational] What goes wrong with this exercise?

[serious] The quality depends entirely on the director. Unskillful direction is confusing, contradictory, or demoralizing. If the director calls out too much, performers can't find their footing. If the calls are vague or contradictory, the performers just freeze.

[curious] Is there a risk of performers becoming dependent on outside direction?

[warm] That's the legitimate counter-argument. Heavy side-coaching can prevent performers from developing their own internal compass. [short pause] The best use of this exercise is building the adaptability muscle, not creating dependency. You want performers who can respond to anything, including a director's voice, including a partner's unexpected move, including a weird audience reaction. The external voice is just a training tool for that flexibility.

[emphatic] The transfer is direct. If you can seamlessly integrate a random direction mid-scene, you can handle anything your partner throws at you.
