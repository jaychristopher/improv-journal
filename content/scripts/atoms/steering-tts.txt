[curious] Okay, so blocking is obvious. Someone says "no" and you can see it. But steering... steering is the one that messes with your head because it looks like good improv.

[teaching] It looks like great improv. That's what makes it so disorienting. A steerer says "yes, and" — they accept your offer, they build on it, they're smiling, they're present. But the scene keeps drifting back to wherever they wanted it to go before you opened your mouth.

[curious] So it's like... they're agreeing with the words but ignoring the spirit?

[warm] Exactly. They receive the offer but refuse to let it change their trajectory. And that word — change — is key. This is the shadow of being changeable. If being changeable means letting your partner's offers genuinely alter your direction, steering is the craft of appearing changed while staying exactly on course.

[teaching] Which is why naming it matters so much. Because if you can't name it, you internalize it. You think you're the problem. But the scene is fracturing — both players are technically in the same reality, but they're drifting in different directions. It doesn't split violently the way blocking does. It just... slowly comes apart. The audience feels the scene "going nowhere" even though everyone's talking.

[curious] So why do people steer? If they know "yes, and" — if they're past the beginner stage — what's driving it?

[matter-of-fact] Two roots. The first is the ego-plan. You walked into the scene knowing what you wanted it to be. Maybe you had a great idea in the wings. Maybe the suggestion sparked something and you built a whole movie in your head before the first line. So when your partner takes it somewhere else, the ego whispers: I had a better idea. I can accept their thing and still get back to mine.

[curious] And the second?

[contemplative] Fear. You steer away from uncomfortable territory — a death scene, a vulnerable confession, an emotional escalation — not because you have a plan, but because you're terrified of where the scene is going. This is anxiety dressed as cooperation.

[short pause]

[curious] That second one seems way harder to catch.

[emphatic] It is. And it connects to one of the sneakiest manifestations — emotional armor. You accept the factual offers but refuse the emotional ones. The words enter, the feelings don't. Your scene partner says something devastating, and your character just... keeps going. Unmoved. And from the outside? It can look like strong character work. It can look like composure. But it's actually a wall.

[curious] So the scene has all the right ingredients but no heat.

[teaching] Right. And there's a cousin to that — topic redirecting. Your partner establishes an emotional reality, and you acknowledge it, then immediately pivot. "Yes, I know you're scared, but let's talk about the mission." You've technically received the offer. You said the word "scared." But you didn't let it in. You used acknowledgment as a bridge back to your territory.
