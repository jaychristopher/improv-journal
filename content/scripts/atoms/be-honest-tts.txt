[curious] "Be Honest" sounds like a moral instruction. Like, don't lie on stage?

[matter-of-fact] That's the most common misreading. Be Honest is not a moral instruction. It's a clarity instruction. [short pause] It means: raise the signal-to-noise ratio. Make what you transmit match what is actually happening in the scene.

[conversational] Signal-to-noise ratio? That sounds more like engineering than theater.

[teaching] That's exactly right, and it's intentional. Think about signal fidelity. Your words, your tone, your body, your timing, all these channels are broadcasting simultaneously. When they all say the same thing, the signal is clear. When they contradict each other, saying "I'm fine" through a clenched jaw, your partner has to decode which channel to trust. That burns their bandwidth.

[curious] So honesty is about channel alignment?

[emphatic] And the most reliable way to achieve that alignment is emotional authenticity. When you're genuinely engaged, not performing a performance, your channels align automatically. The audience may not consciously name a fake, but they feel the incoherence. Trust erodes and the scene loses ground.

[conversational] But what about characters who lie? Great scenes are full of lying characters.

[warm] Absolutely, and this is the critical distinction. The honesty is the performer's, not the character's. [short pause] A husband who says "I don't know anything about a receipt" while his hands shake? That's a lying character played by an honest performer. The audience reads both layers. Character honesty is optional and often dramatically wrong. Performer honesty is what makes the scene work.

[curious] OK, so how does honesty express itself practically?

[teaching] Through specificity. "I'm upset" is a report. "I found a Marriott receipt in your coat for two guests on the night you said you were at your mother's" is truth. [short pause] The specific detail gives your partner an exact thing to react to instead of a vague emotional weather report. Two people doing their taxes isn't interesting, unless the husband is genuinely terrified of what his wife will find.

[conversational] Del Close had something to say about this, right?

[confident] "The truth is funny. Honest discovery, observation, and reaction is better than contrived invention." [short pause] Ninety percent of beginners prioritize novelty over legibility. Pirates, aliens, talking parrots. They think they need to be original. But Keith Johnstone flipped that: "The more obvious he is, the more original he appears."

[curious] So Johnstone's version of "be honest" is really "stop censoring yourself"?

[contemplative] Exactly. He reframes the problem. It's not dishonesty, it's censorship. Performers have been trained to reject their first impulse in favor of something better. [short pause] The honest response is whatever you were going to do before you decided it wasn't good enough.

[conversational] And Napier has a different take?

[warm] Napier would caution that "be honest" as an instruction can itself become a trap. One more rule to self-monitor. His alternative: do something, commit fully, and honesty emerges as a byproduct of total commitment rather than a separate directive. [pause] Either way, the destination is the same. Honest signals are clear. Clever signals are muddy. And your partner needs something solid to grab onto.
