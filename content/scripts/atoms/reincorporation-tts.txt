[curious] You ever watch a longform set and at the end think... did they write that?

[warm] That's reincorporation doing its job. When it works, the audience can't tell the difference between improvised and scripted. That's not a trick — that's actual craft.

[curious] So what is it, precisely? Because people throw the word around a lot.

[teaching] Something gets established early. A name, an object, a throwaway detail. The scene moves on, everybody forgets about it — or seems to. Then later, that thing comes back. But into a changed context. And suddenly it means something it didn't mean before.

[contemplative] The gun on the mantelpiece.

[confident] Chekhov's gun, exactly. If it's on the wall in Act 1, it fires in Act 3. But here's what's wild about doing that in improv — you hung the gun on the wall without knowing it was a gun. You just said a thing. Made a choice. And then twenty minutes later, your scene partner or someone across the stage picks it up, and it fires. And the audience gasps like it was planned from the opening.

[curious] Okay but how is that different from a callback? Because callbacks also bring things back.

[matter-of-fact] This is the distinction that matters. A callback brings something back for a laugh. Recognition plus surprise — the audience remembers it and the return is funny. That's a comedy tool. Reincorporation brings something back for the story. The element returns with added weight, changed meaning, resolution. It doesn't have to be funny at all. It can be devastating.

[contemplative] So a callback is like... the audience laughs because they remember the thing. Reincorporation is when the audience feels something because the thing means something different now.

[emphatic] Yes. And in improv comedy they overlap constantly — something comes back and it's both funny and narratively satisfying. But the intention is different. If you're only thinking callback, you'll get laughs. If you're thinking reincorporation, you'll get a story that haunts people on the drive home.

[short pause]

[curious] Johnstone has that line about walking backwards.

[teaching] The improviser is like a man walking backwards. He sees where he's been but pays no attention to the future. That's the whole philosophy. You're not inventing forward — you're mining backward. Every detail you've already established is material. Every name dropped, every gesture, every emotional shift. Those aren't spent — they're loaded. Your job is to notice when one of them is ready to come back.

[curious] But you can't remember everything. In a Harold, there's so much information flying around in the first beat alone.

[warm] And you shouldn't try to remember everything. That's the trap. Forced reincorporation — where someone's clearly thinking "I need to mention the bicycle again" — it feels mechanical. You can smell the effort. Organic reincorporation happens when you were genuinely present when the detail was established, and then your brain surfaces it at the right moment. Not because you catalogued it. Because you felt it.

[contemplative] So it's less like keeping a list and more like... trusting your attention.

[confident] Exactly. If you were really in that scene — not planning, not judging, just there — the details live in your body. And when the context shifts, the right one floats up. That moment onstage where you suddenly think "wait — the gun." That's not memory. That's presence paying dividends.

[pause]

[curious] You said something interesting — that reincorporation is what separates a sequence of events from a story.

[teaching] That's the Johnstone principle from Impro for Storytellers. Without reincorporation, a longform set is just... things happening. Scene, scene, scene. They might be individually great. But the audience leaves feeling like they watched sketches. When elements return and connect, suddenly there's architecture. The audience feels like they watched something with a beginning, middle, and end — even if the performers never planned one.

[serious] That's the difference between a good show and one people talk about for years.

[warm] And here's the part that surprises people — this shows up everywhere off stage. You know when you're telling a friend about your week and some detail from Monday suddenly explains what happened on Friday? And the story clicks into place? That's reincorporation. Your brain found the thread.

[curious] Or in a conversation — when someone circles back to something you said an hour ago, and it lands completely differently now.

[emphatic] And you feel seen. Because they were listening. That's what reincorporation communicates to an audience too — someone was paying attention. Someone cared enough about what was established to bring it back. That's why it feels like art.

[curious] Now, I know UCB folks might hear all this and say — we don't really think in terms of reincorporation. We think in terms of game. Finding the pattern and heightening it.

[matter-of-fact] And they're not wrong. Game-based improv replaces narrative return with pattern escalation. You don't need to bring back the gun — you need to find the unusual thing and do it bigger. Those are different engines. Story through return versus comedy through pattern. But they're not enemies. The best performers use both. The game gets the laughs in the moment. The reincorporation makes the show feel like it meant something.

[contemplative] Two tools, different jobs.

[teaching] And the performers who can do both — who can play the game hard and then, in the third beat, bring back that detail from the opening that nobody expected to matter — those are the ones who make audiences lose their minds.
