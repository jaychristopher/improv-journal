[curious] Okay so here's something that messes with people. You tell an improviser "be obvious" and they hear "be boring."

[teaching] Every time. And it's exactly backwards. The obvious choice is almost always the most alive choice in the scene. Johnstone nailed it — the more obvious you are, the more original you appear.

[curious] That sounds like a paradox.

[teaching] It is one. But watch it play out. Two improvisers get a suggestion, let's say "birthday party." The experienced player walks in and says "happy birthday." The newer player walks in and says "I'm a time-traveling robot sent to destroy your cake." Now — which one actually has somewhere to go?

[curious] The happy birthday one. Because the robot person just spent all the oxygen in the room.

[teaching] Right. "Happy birthday" costs nothing to process. Your scene partner hears it, feels it, and can respond from a real place. The robot thing? Now your partner has to build a world, figure out the rules, decide how they feel about time travel — and the scene hasn't even started yet.

[short pause]

[contemplative] There's this idea I love. Bandwidth budgeting. Every offer you make has a processing cost for your partner. Complex offers are expensive. Simple offers are cheap. And the whole point of improv is connection, not comprehension.

[curious] So you're saying we should be spending our shared brainpower on actually relating to each other, not on decoding each other's premises.

[teaching] Exactly. When you walk in with something simple and grounded — a clear emotion, an obvious relationship, a real reaction — you're handing your partner a gift they can actually use. When you walk in with something layered and clever, you're handing them a puzzle.

[curious] But here's the thing. I know this intellectually and I still catch myself reaching for the clever thing. What is that?

[warm] It's protection. And this is the part nobody talks about. The obvious choice feels risky because it feels exposed. If I walk into a scene and just feel grief — real, open, undecorated grief — I'm standing there with no armor on. But if I make a joke, or add a twist, or build some elaborate premise, I've got layers to hide behind. The complexity is a shield.

[curious] So being obvious is actually the braver move.

[emphatic] Always. That's why commitment to being brave is a prerequisite for this. You cannot be simple if you're afraid to be seen. The obvious choice strips you bare. And that's exactly why audiences lean in.

[short pause]

[curious] Okay so practically — I'm in a scene, I feel that urge to be clever. What do I do?

[teaching] Downgrade. Ask yourself: what's the simplest version of this impulse? You want to say something witty about your scene partner's confession — downgrade. What would a human actually feel hearing that? Probably hurt. Probably confused. Maybe relieved. Whatever it is, it's not a pun.

[curious] The "what would a human actually feel" test.

[teaching] That's the whole thing. Obvious doesn't mean generic. It doesn't mean low energy. It means emotionally true. Your scene partner says "my husband left me." The obvious response is some shade of devastation. Not clever, not deflective, not a callback to something from earlier in the set. Just — the real thing, played fully.

[contemplative] And here's what's wild. Grief played with full commitment is riveting. Grief played carefully — hedging, half-smiling, keeping one foot out — that's boring. The variable was never the choice. It's always the commitment.

[curious] So boring improv isn't caused by obvious choices. It's caused by obvious choices played without conviction.

[emphatic] That's it. That's the whole misunderstanding. Students conflate "obvious" with "safe" when it's actually the opposite. Safe is the clever move with the escape hatch built in. Obvious is standing in the fire.

[short pause]

[curious] You know where I notice this off stage? Conversations. Real ones. When someone tells you something hard and your brain scrambles to say the smart thing, the helpful thing, the thing that makes you look good —

[warm] And the right move is almost always the simple one. "That sounds awful." "I'm sorry." "Tell me more." The obvious response. The one your ego thinks isn't enough.

[curious] Because your ego wants to perform.

[teaching] Your ego always wants to perform. In scenes and in life. Your first thought is connected to the moment. Your second thought is connected to your ego trying to improve on the moment. The practice is learning to trust that first impulse before the editor gets to it.

[curious] Even when the first impulse feels boring.

[confident] Especially then. Because "this feels boring" is almost never your artistic judgment talking. It's your fear that you won't be seen as interesting. And the great irony — Johnstone saw this so clearly — no two people are alike. The more obvious you are, the more yourself you appear. When you stop trying to be original, your actual originality comes through. It can't help it. It's you.

[pause]

[contemplative] The obvious choice leaves space for the scene to grow. The complex choice fills the space before the scene has even started. One is an invitation. The other is a monologue disguised as an opening line.

[warm] So be simple. Be obvious. Be the person who walks in and says happy birthday — and means it.
