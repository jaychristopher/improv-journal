[teaching] That's the run. And it's one of the most misunderstood moves in longform. People think it's just "go fast at the end." But a run isn't about speed. It's about culmination.

[confident] Exactly. The pace creates a condition where the audience's pattern recognition fires faster than their analytical mind. They're not thinking "oh, that's a callback to the dentist scene." They're just laughing. The speed bypasses the intellect and hits something more primal.

[curious] What does a run actually look like mechanically? Like, what's happening on stage?

[matter-of-fact] Sweep edits coming every ten, fifteen seconds. Sometimes faster. Tag-outs creating this machine-gun rhythm. Someone initiates a two-line scene that callbacks the opening, gets swept, someone else collides two characters who never met, gets swept, someone recreates the stage picture from the first beat but with the dialogue from the third. Five seconds. Swept. And it keeps accelerating until it peaks and you black out or you land on the image from the opening and the lights go down.

[curious] That's a lot of trust.

[emphatic] It's enormous trust. And here's the thing people miss — the run doesn't test your ability to be fast. It tests whether you were listening for the entire show. You cannot run if you weren't tracking. Every callback requires shared memory. Every collision requires knowing what someone else built thirty minutes ago.

[short pause]

[teaching] Del Close's people talked about two styles. [curious] Oh, that's brutal. And beautiful.

[warm] Right? It forces you to have been watching the whole show, not just waiting for your scenes. And it kills ego. You can't run your greatest hits. You have to prove you were paying attention to everyone else's.
