[curious] Gift Giving. This is based on Johnstone's Presents exercise, right?

[teaching] It is, with a twist. Two players face each other. Player A mimes handing over an invisible object. But here's what matters. [short pause] The giver does not decide what the object is. They hand over an undefined shape. It's a blind offer.

[conversational] So the receiver decides what it is?

[emphatic] Exactly. Player B receives it, names it out loud, and then personally justifies why this gift matters to them. Not performed enthusiasm. A specific reason this object is meaningful. Then B gives A an object in return.

[curious] Can you give me an example?

[warm] A hands over an invisible something. B takes it, examines it, and says, "A jar of pickles! You remembered I've been craving these ever since the camping trip. Thank you!" [short pause] Then B hands something to A. A takes it and says, "A broken compass. This is the one from grandpa's desk, isn't it? I thought we lost this."

[contemplative] So the magic is in the justification, not the naming.

[teaching] That's exactly right. There is no wrong gift because the receiver decides what it is and why it matters. The gratitude isn't performed. It emerges naturally from discovering why this object is meaningful to you. When you find the why, the thankfulness is real.

[curious] What goes wrong when people try this?

[matter-of-fact] Four common failures. The giver pre-shapes a recognizable object, which defeats the blind offer. The receiver names something clever for a laugh instead of something personally meaningful. The receiver goes generic with just "Oh wow, thanks!" and no specific justification. [short pause] And players rush past the moment of discovery. You need to slow down and let the object arrive before naming it.

[conversational] Is there a harder version?

[confident] Yes. In the advanced variation, the receiver deliberately names something that sounds bad. A handful of dirt. A crumpled receipt. A single sock. And then finds the personal justification that makes it perfect. [short pause] "Dirt from Mom's garden. You went back for this?" The skill isn't receiving obviously good things well. It's discovering that nothing is bad when you commit to finding the meaning.

[emphatic] The transfer to scene work is direct. Your partner's unexpected move is the gift. Your job isn't to judge whether it's a good gift. Your job is to receive it and discover why it's exactly what the scene needed.
