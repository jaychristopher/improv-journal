[curious] So there's this thing that happens on stage — and I think every improviser has been on the receiving end of it — where you're in a scene, you've got something building, and then someone just... rolls right over it.

[teaching] Bulldozing. And what makes it tricky is that it doesn't look like blocking. Blocking says no to your offer. Bulldozing never even lets your offer exist.

[curious] Right, because with blocking you at least got to say the thing. With bulldozing, you open your mouth and there's already no room.

[teaching] Exactly. Your partner becomes a prop. Not a co-creator — a piece of furniture in someone else's one-person show. And the scene doesn't even fracture, which is the weird part. It just... flattens. There's one reality on stage, and it's not shared.

[short pause]

[curious] What does it actually look like, though? Because I think people hear "bulldozing" and picture someone literally shoving their scene partner out of the way.

[teaching] Sometimes it's that obvious, yeah. Scene-stealing — jumping into something your partner is building because you want to ride that wave too. But the subtler forms are the ones that do the real damage.

[conversational] Like laugh-stealing. Your partner is in a genuine emotional moment, something real is happening, and you drop the joke that gets the audience reaction but completely undercuts what they were building. You traded their scene for your laugh.

[curious] And it felt good in the moment. The audience responded.

[teaching] Which is exactly the problem. You got rewarded. So you do it again. And again. And now you've trained yourself to optimize for the wrong thing.

[short pause]

[matter-of-fact] Then there's talking over. Filling every silence. Jumping in before your partner's thought has landed. And this one is important because it's not just a style choice — it's a bandwidth attack. You've consumed all the shared channel capacity. Your partner literally cannot contribute because there's no space left.

[curious] That's a good way to put it. It's not that they don't have ideas. It's that you've taken up all the oxygen.

[teaching] All of it. And then there's self-casting — always grabbing the high-status role, the big emotional arc, the character the audience watches. Leaving your partner the straight man every single time. Not as a generous choice, but as a default.

[contemplative] And here's what I find interesting. We want to say bulldozing comes from insecurity — the need to prove you belong, the fear that if you're not the funniest person on stage, you haven't earned your spot. And sometimes that's true.

[curious] But not always.

[teaching] Not always. Sometimes it's just unmodulated enthusiasm. Some performers are big. Their energy fills a room before they've said a word. They're not selfish — they just haven't learned to yield. Their default setting overwhelms.

[warm] And I actually have compassion for that, because the note isn't "be less." It's "aim it differently."

[curious] Johnstone would probably say something about status here.

[teaching] He would. Some people bulldoze because they're genuinely comfortable with dominance. It's not fear, it's not enthusiasm — it's just where they live. High status is their home base. Nobody ever asked them to leave it.

[short pause]

[serious] But here's the systemic cost, and this is the part that matters beyond any individual scene. A team of bulldozers can be good. They can even be impressive. But they can never be great. Every player is locally maximizing their own output while degrading the total. Trust erodes because nobody feels safe — your offer might get overridden. Risk decreases because vulnerability gets punished. You end up with a collection of soloists.

[curious] Which sounds exciting on paper.

[teaching] And is exhausting to watch. Because great improv isn't about five brilliant individuals. It's about five people building something none of them could build alone. And bulldozing makes that impossible.

[pause]

[curious] So what's the correction? Because you can't just tell a bulldozer to do less. That's not useful.

[teaching] No, the shift is attentional, not energetic. It's moving from "what do I want to do" to "what is my partner doing that I can serve." Active listening. Containment. Hold space. Play grounded. Ask the simple question that lets your partner's offer develop.

[warm] The energy doesn't disappear. It gets aimed outward instead of inward. And that's actually a harder, more interesting skill than being the loudest person on stage.

[curious] There's a Napier quote I keep coming back to — something like "get over yourself and selfishly make a strong choice." Which sounds like it's endorsing bulldozing.

[teaching] It does. But there's a critical distinction. A strong choice gives your partner something rich to react to. Bulldozing ignores what your partner is giving you. The line between them is whether you're listening while being bold.

[contemplative] You can be the biggest energy in the room and still be the most generous player on stage. Those two things aren't opposites.

[short pause]

[curious] This shows up off stage too, doesn't it. Meetings, conversations, relationships — people who fill every silence, who are already formulating their response before you've finished talking.

[teaching] Constantly. And the tell is the same. You can feel when someone is listening to you versus waiting for their turn. On stage it costs you the scene. Off stage it costs you the relationship.

[pause]

[warm] The most valued ensemble players — and honestly, the most valued people in any room — are the ones who make everyone else shine. That's the irony. The bulldozer is fighting for attention they'd get freely if they just aimed their energy at their partner.

[contemplative] Containment isn't smaller. It's stronger.
