[curious] So "Be Brave" is one of the North Stars. But what does bravery actually mean in improv? Aren't you just... talking on stage?

[contemplative] It sounds simple, right? But think about what happens at the top of a scene. Two people walk out. Nobody has a script. Somebody has to say the first thing. [short pause] And that moment, the gap between silence and the first offer, is where most improvisers freeze.

[conversational] Because they're trying to think of something good to say?

[emphatic] Exactly. And here's the thing Mick Napier figured out: it is far more important that you DO something than WHAT you actually do. The quality of your first offer matters way less than the fact that you made one. Do something, notice what you did, then commit to it.

[curious] OK, so bravery is just... starting? Going first?

[warm] That's level one. Physical initiation, stepping off the backline, opening your mouth. But there's a deeper level. [short pause] Emotional exposure. The willingness to be sincere and specific instead of hiding behind irony or vagueness.

[conversational] What does that look like in practice?

[teaching] Compare these two openings. "I work in an office." Safe, generic, nothing at stake. Now try: "I'm a tax accountant who just found a forty thousand dollar discrepancy in my wife's charity." [short pause] That second one is braver because it's specific, and specific choices can fail. You've committed to a reality that might not work. There's nowhere to hide.

[curious] So specificity is bravery?

[matter-of-fact] Specificity requires bravery. Terry Withers puts it perfectly: "Don't try to be funny. Be brave and be yourself." [pause] And then there's a third level. Sustained bravery. The courage to stay exposed after your initial offer. To say "I love you" when the scene has been circling it for two minutes. To let a silence land instead of filling it with a joke.

[conversational] That sounds exhausting. Like you need some deep reserve of willpower.

[warm] And this is where it gets interesting. Keith Johnstone would actually resist the word "bravery" entirely. His frame is that the problem isn't that improvisers lack courage. It's that they've been trained out of their natural spontaneity. Education installed a censor, and what looks like bravery is actually just releasing that censor.

[curious] So Napier says "act despite the fear" and Johnstone says "remove the thing causing the fear"?

[emphatic] Right. Opposite frames, same destination: action before deliberation. [short pause] And here's the bandwidth paradox that makes it all click. Hesitating and planning actually burns MORE cognitive bandwidth than just acting. When you're standing there computing your next move, you're using the exact resource you need for listening and responding.

[conversational] So the brave move is actually the cheaper move, cognitively?

[confident] The brave move is the cheaper move. Act, and you free up bandwidth. Hesitate, and you burn it. [pause] But none of this works without trust. Without ensemble trust, bravery is just recklessness. With it, bravery becomes a sustainable practice. You build it like a muscle, by repeatedly crossing the threshold in a container safe enough to fail in.

[contemplative] So the counter-position is Johnstone's: it's not about summoning courage, it's about recovering spontaneity you already had.

[warm] Exactly. Recovery versus conquest. Either way, the scene needs you to move before you're ready. [short pause] The scene always needs offers more than it needs perfect offers.
