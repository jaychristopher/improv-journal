[curious] Fracture Repair Drill. I haven't heard of this one. What's a fracture in improv terms?

[teaching] A fracture is when two players are in the same scene but they're actually in different scenes. They've diverged on what's true. Maybe one thinks they're at a restaurant and the other thinks they're at a living room. Maybe one thinks the tone is playful and the other thinks it's dead serious. [short pause] This exercise manufactures that fracture on purpose so you can practice fixing it.

[conversational] How do you manufacture it?

[matter-of-fact] Two players begin a scene. Before they start, a coach secretly whispers a different context to each player. Player A is told "you're at a funeral." Player B is told "you're at a surprise party." They begin playing. The audience watches the fracture form in real time.

[curious] That's devious. So at some point someone has to fix it?

[emphatic] Right. At any point, either player can attempt to repair. And there are two paths. You can yield, which means you simply drop your version and join your partner's reality. Or you can bridge, which means you find a justification that makes both realities true. [short pause] Maybe it's a funeral where someone planned a surprise celebration of life. Now both are true.

[contemplative] I imagine most people's instinct is to push harder on their own version.

[serious] That's the natural instinct and it always makes it worse. Always. The repair only happens when someone yields or bridges. [short pause] And yielding is the faster repair. Players need explicit permission to do it. You can just drop your thing. That's not failure. That's support.

[curious] What does the side-coaching sound like?

[warm] Early on it's just "Notice the gap." Helping players learn to detect the fracture. Then "You don't have to be clever, you can just go with theirs." And the diagnostic question, "What does your partner think is true?"

[conversational] Does this actually happen in real performances?

[teaching] Constantly. But in performance, nobody whispers to you. You create these fractures yourself by accident. A missed offer, a misread tone. [short pause] This drill builds two things. The diagnostic skill, noticing that the fracture exists, and the repair skill, yielding or bridging as a practiced reflex.

[emphatic] The transfer is simple. In any scene, when something feels off, when your partner's response doesn't match what you expected, ask yourself: are we in the same scene? If not, yield first, ask questions later. A unified scene has a floor. A fractured scene has no floor at all.
