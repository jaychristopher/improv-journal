[curious] There's a harder version of this, right?

[teaching] Two harder versions. The Double Blind — both players start moving simultaneously, no speaking, and then one of them has to justify both movements into a single scene. You're pattern-matching two unrelated physical streams into one reality. It's significantly harder because now you're not just reading one person, you're synthesizing two.

[curious] And the other variation?

[matter-of-fact] Supporting Player. This one's where it gets real. You run a normal scene, but one person is privately designated as the support. Their only job is to make the other person's offers shine. No stealing focus, no redirecting, no pursuing their own ideas over their partner's.

[contemplative] That's a completely different skill than the base exercise, isn't it.

[emphatic] Completely different. In Trains, B has no competing impulse. They walk in with nothing — no character, no idea, no agenda. Of course they're generous, they have nothing else to do. But in Supporting Player, you have your own ideas. You see a move you want to make. You feel the pull to be interesting. And you choose not to. You choose to serve what your partner is building instead.

[serious] That's the hard form. Not reading your partner — that's perceptual, you can drill it. The hard form is seeing what your partner needs, having your own thing, and choosing theirs.
