[curious] So there's this game type where you take a completely ordinary situation and play it through the lens of something wildly different. Two roommates arguing about dishes, but they're treating it like a UN summit.

[teaching] Mapping. And it's one of my favorite things in improv because it does something almost no other game type does — it tells the truth by lying.

[curious] What do you mean by that?

[warm] Okay, so picture it. Two players, they're roommates. There's tension about chores. Normal stuff. And one of them starts going, "I will not negotiate with someone who has shown such bad faith regarding the recycling bin." And suddenly we're in a diplomatic crisis between rival nations. Except we're still in the apartment. We're still talking about the recycling bin.

[curious] And the audience is tracking both realities at the same time.

[teaching] Exactly. And here's the thing — the reason it's funny isn't just the absurdity. It's that the mapping is often MORE accurate than the real conversation would be. Like, this roommate situation? It actually IS a power struggle. It actually IS about territory and respect and broken agreements. The diplomatic framework makes that visible.

[short pause]

[contemplative] Comedy as X-ray.

[warm] That's a great way to put it. The absurd frame illuminates the genuine dynamic. You're watching someone negotiate about whose turn it is to take out the trash, and because they're doing it as world leaders, you suddenly see — oh, this is really about who has authority in this relationship.

[curious] There's a literary theory behind this, right? Tenor and vehicle?

[conversational] Yeah. The tenor is the actual situation — the roommates, the chores. The vehicle is the imposed framework — the diplomatic summit. And the scene lives in the gap between them. Every beat is about finding a new specific correspondence. "Your ambassador — your mother — called to broker a ceasefire. I rejected her terms."

[curious] That line is doing so much work. It's advancing the real story AND the mapped one.

[teaching] And that's the skill of it. Every move has to land in both worlds simultaneously. A breakup played as a hostage negotiation — "I have demands. First, the cat. Second, safe passage to my mother's house." That's a real breakup beat. People really do negotiate over the cat. But the hostage framing gives it this heightened urgency that makes you laugh AND wince.

[pause]

[curious] Here's what I want to dig into though. This feels like it breaks a rule. We talk a lot about simplicity in scene work — just be a person, just react, don't get in your head. And mapping is explicitly asking you to manage two realities at once.

[serious] You're right, and that tension is real. Mapping is one of the few game types where complexity IS the point. You are running two operating systems at the same time. But — and this is crucial — within the mapping, each individual move still has to be simple and legible. If the audience can't instantly read the parallel, you've lost both threads.

[curious] So the complexity is structural, but the execution is still clean.

[emphatic] Yes. Think of it like this. The choice to map a restaurant debate onto a presidential election — that's the complex part. That's the architecture. But the actual moves inside it? "My opponent would have you believe that Thai food is 'fine for Tuesday,' but I ask you — is that the leadership this family needs?" That's one clean move. One clear parallel. You get it instantly.

[contemplative] It's like the mapping does the heavy lifting so the individual lines don't have to.

[warm] Exactly. The frame carries the comedy. You just have to keep finding the next specific correspondence. And specific is the word that matters there. Not "we're in a war" — but "your ambassador, your mother, called to broker a ceasefire." The more precise the parallel, the funnier and truer it gets.

[short pause]

[curious] What goes wrong when people try this and it doesn't work?

[matter-of-fact] Two things, usually. First, they pick a vehicle that doesn't actually illuminate the tenor. Like, playing a trip to the grocery store as a space mission — okay, there are some surface-level parallels, but what truth does that reveal about grocery shopping? You need a mapping where the vehicle says something real about the situation. A mechanic explaining car repairs as a doctor delivering bad news works because we TREAT our cars like bodies. There's a real emotional parallel.

[curious] And the second thing?

[teaching] They abandon the base reality. They get so excited about the vehicle that they stop tracking the actual situation. And then it's just a scene about diplomats. The whole engine of mapping is that both realities stay alive. The moment you lose the mundane, you lose the gap. And the gap is where all the comedy lives.

[pause]

[contemplative] There's something Johnstone would say about this, isn't there? That it's too cerebral?

[warm] Yeah, his closest concept is overaccepting — reinterpreting an offer at a higher level of abstraction. But he wouldn't formalize it into a technique. He'd probably say, just follow the narrative. Stop imposing a pattern and let the scene discover its own logic.

[curious] And he's not wrong about that as a general principle.

[teaching] Not at all. But mapping, when it works, isn't really imposed from outside. The best mappings emerge because someone recognizes the truth that's already in the scene. The roommates really ARE in a cold war. The mapping just names it. It's pattern recognition dressed as absurdity.

[short pause]

[curious] You know where I see this off stage? Every family dinner I've ever been to.

[warm] Right? "Please pass the salt" means "I'm still upset about Thanksgiving 2019." We're all constantly mapping in real life — the surface conversation is never really about the surface conversation. Improv just makes it visible and turns the volume up.

[contemplative] So mapping isn't a trick. It's just — honest about how people actually communicate.

[emphatic] Through indirection. Through the lens of something else. Always. The question is whether you have the courage to name the real frame. On stage, that's the game. Off stage — well. That's just being a person who pays attention.
