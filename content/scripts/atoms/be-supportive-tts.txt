[curious] "Be Supportive." This one seems obvious. Be nice to your scene partner, right?

[contemplative] It goes much deeper than that. The first seven principles are all individual commands. How should I process data, allocate attention, send signals, act, adapt. Be Supportive is the first that is explicitly other-directed. [short pause] It answers a different question: am I working for myself or for the ensemble?

[conversational] Can't you follow all the other principles and still be selfish?

[emphatic] That's exactly the loophole. You can accept offers, stay present, value surprises, send clear signals, keep it simple, act boldly, and let yourself be changed, all while making yourself the center of every scene. Be Supportive orients all of that energy outward.

[curious] So what does it look like in practice?

[warm] Del Close and Charna Halpern said it best: "The only star in improv is the ensemble itself. The best way for an improviser to look good is by making his fellow players look good." [short pause] And Close had another formulation I love: "If we treat each other as if we are geniuses, poets and artists, we have a better chance of becoming that on stage."

[conversational] That sounds beautiful but also a bit abstract. What's the mechanism?

[teaching] It's a virtuous cycle. If everyone is working to make everyone else look good, nobody needs to protect themselves. Trust grows because it's earned continuously. Each demonstration of support is a deposit in the trust bank. Risk increases because the safety net is real. Discovery accelerates because fear decreases. [short pause] The system amplifies when everyone invests in each other. It constrains when everyone protects themselves.

[curious] But what about Napier's take? Doesn't he push back on this?

[confident] He does, and it's an important corrective. Napier argues that a strong, committed individual choice IS support. It gives your partner something real to respond to. [short pause] Waiting passively for your partner's needs isn't generosity, it's a burden. Sometimes the most supportive move is to lead decisively, not to defer. Support does not mean deference.

[conversational] How is this different from Be Positive?

[teaching] Be Positive says don't reject the offer. Be Supportive says actively elevate it. [short pause] Patti Stiles sharpens this further. She prefers "inspire your partner" to "support your partner." Support can be passive, just affirming and holding. Inspiration is specifically generative. Releasing your partner's imagination, pushing them into territory they'd avoid on their own.

[curious] Are there different modes of support?

[warm] At least two. Amplifying, which means actively elevating your partner's offer with specifics, stakes, and enthusiasm. And containing, which means holding space, playing grounded while your partner explores, asking the simple question that extends their run. [short pause] TJ and Dave trade these roles fluidly. One holds while the other explores, then they switch.

[serious] And here's the critical failure mode. Self-erasure. If you suppress your own voice and creative instincts entirely in service of your partner, you remove an agent from the system. [pause] The ensemble needs full humans, not doormats. Support means serving the scene with your whole self, not disappearing from it.
