[curious] So here's something I've been sitting with. Everyone says "listen" in improv. It's like the first note you get. But I've started to wonder if most of us — even experienced players — are doing something that looks like listening but isn't actually listening.

[teaching] Oh, absolutely. There's this counterfeit version that's incredibly common. You're on stage, your partner is talking, your eyes are on them, you're nodding — and meanwhile there's this whole other scene running in your head. You're workshopping your next line. You're casting ahead to where you think the scene should go.

[curious] Right, and the thing is, it feels like listening. You're tracking what they're saying. You could repeat it back.

[teaching] But you're processing, not receiving. There's a huge difference. Processing is cognitive — you're running their input through your filters, your agenda, your idea of what the scene is. Receiving is... it's more like you let their thing land in your body before your brain gets to it.

[contemplative] Del Close had this line — an improviser has to consider what is said and what is left unsaid. And that's the part that gets me. [curious] So it's not really about ears at all.

[warm] It's about contact. Full-body contact with another person's reality. And when you're actually doing it — when you drop the planning and just let their state hit you — something wild happens. Your responses start coming from a different place. They're not constructed, they're... arising. You stop choosing what to say and it just comes.

[curious] TJ Jagodowski talks about this. He describes it as heightened sensation — like you can suddenly see better and hear everything more.

[teaching] Yes. And that's not poetry, that's a real phenomenological shift. When you stop splitting your attention between your partner and your internal planning, you suddenly have all of your perceptual bandwidth available. Time genuinely seems to slow down. You start anticipating each other. It looks like magic from the outside, but it's actually just... full attention. Which is so rare that it does feel magical.

[curious] Here's what trips me up though. You can be doing all of that — really hearing them, really seeing them — and still block the offer. Still redirect the scene to your thing.

[emphatic] That's exactly it. Johnstone doesn't even use the word "listening." He talks about accepting offers. And that distinction matters. Listening is necessary but not sufficient. That's listening without acceptance. It's a more sophisticated form of blocking.

[contemplative] So real active listening has to include the willingness to be changed by what you receive.

[teaching] That's the whole game. You have to be willing to abandon your plan — not just adjust it, abandon it — based on what your partner gives you. Your clever idea, your perfect callback, your elegant game move. All of it has to be lighter than what's actually happening in front of you.

[curious] Which is terrifying.

[warm] Of course it is. You're agreeing to walk on stage with nothing. Not "nothing plus a safety net of prepared moves." Actual nothing. And trusting that the listening itself will generate everything you need.

[curious] And then in longform, this gets even bigger, right? You're not just tracking one person.

[teaching] Right, in a Harold or any ensemble piece, listening scales to the whole room. You're tracking the group pattern. [warm] Everyone does it. And the moment you notice yourself doing it — that's the same muscle as noticing yourself planning on stage. The practice is identical. Drop the plan. Come back to the person in front of you. Let their thing be the only thing for a second.

[contemplative] It's funny. Listening is the simplest instruction in improv. And it might be the one we spend our whole careers learning.

[teaching] Because it's not a skill you acquire. It's a habit you keep interrupting. The planning mind doesn't go away — you just get faster at catching it and letting go.
