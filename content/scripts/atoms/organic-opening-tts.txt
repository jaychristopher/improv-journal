[curious] So you get a suggestion from the audience — let's say "laundry" — and then the whole ensemble just... riffs on it for a few minutes before any scene starts. What's actually happening in an opening?

[teaching] Two things at once, and that's what makes it interesting. On the surface, you're generating material — images, themes, connections you can mine for the rest of the show. But underneath that, you're doing something more important. You're syncing up. You're getting eight brains to start breathing in the same rhythm.

[curious] It's a warm-up disguised as content generation.

[warm] Or content generation disguised as a warm-up. Either way, the dual function is the point. If you're only doing one, something's off. An opening that generates brilliant ideas but doesn't connect the ensemble? You'll get eight solo shows. An opening that feels great but produces nothing specific? You'll watch people grasp for ideas in the first beat.

[contemplative] I think about the pattern game version — the one that became standard at UCB. Someone makes a statement connected to the suggestion, the group finds the underlying pattern, and then you follow and heighten until it's exhausted, then pivot to a new one. It sounds mechanical when you describe it, but when it's working...

[teaching] When it's working, it's the fastest way I know to watch group mind happen in real time. Someone says "laundry" and the first person says "I only do laundry when I've run out of underwear." And the group doesn't just laugh — they hear the pattern underneath. Procrastination. Doing the bare minimum. So the next person says "I only call my mom when she's already left a voicemail." And now you're somewhere. You've got a theme you can build scenes on all night.

[curious] And you might get three, four, five of those threads from a single pattern game.

[matter-of-fact] Easily. And here's what matters — those threads are richer than anything one person would have come up with alone. That's the whole promise. The suggestion "laundry" in one person's head is their experience of laundry. The suggestion "laundry" run through eight people's free associations is procrastination, ritual, intimacy, the smell of your parents' house, class, labor, transformation. You've got a palette.

[emphatic] A palette, not a plan. I want to underline that because this is where people go wrong.

[teaching] This is the biggest trap. The opening generates raw material, and then performers treat it like a shopping list. "OK, we hit procrastination, intimacy, and transformation — I'll take procrastination in scene one." That's not how it works. The opening gives you an emotional and thematic landscape. You're supposed to be *inspired* by it, not transcribing it.

[curious] What about invocations? Those always felt like a different animal to me.

[warm] They are. A pattern game lives in your head — it's verbal, it's logical, it's "if this, then what." An invocation lives in your body. The whole ensemble speaks as one voice, addressing the suggestion like it's a god or a force of nature. "Oh, laundry, you promise us we can start fresh..." It's poetic. It's weird. And it generates completely different material — images, emotional tones, textures — rather than ideas you can articulate.

[contemplative] Which means the scenes that come out of an invocation feel different too.

[teaching] Completely. Scenes after a good invocation tend to be more atmospheric, more emotionally committed. Scenes after a good pattern game tend to be sharper, more thematically connected. Neither is better. But knowing which tool you're reaching for matters.

[curious] And scene painting is the third option — building an environment together.

[confident] Right. One person adds a detail, another adds a sound, someone walks into the space as a character. You're constructing a world. And here's what's sneaky about it — the world itself generates the themes. A laundromat at 2 AM and a laundromat on a Sunday morning are completely different shows. The specificity of the environment does the thematic work for you.

[short pause]

[curious] OK, here's the thing I've been thinking about. Some people say you don't need an opening at all. Just take the suggestion and go.

[matter-of-fact] And they're not wrong. Plenty of great longform skips the opening entirely. The Deconstruction goes straight into scenes. Lots of montage forms do too. The opening isn't sacred.

[curious] So when is it worth doing?

[teaching] When the ensemble needs it. And I mean that literally — needs the sync-up, needs the shared material, needs the three minutes of breathing together before the first scene. A group that's been performing together for years might not need it. A group that just met backstage? They probably do.

[serious] The real danger is when the opening becomes performative. When it stops being for the ensemble and starts being for the audience. Pattern games are especially vulnerable to this — they can turn into competitive joke-writing sessions where everyone's trying to get the biggest laugh on their line. The moment that happens, you've lost both functions. You're not syncing up and you're not generating usable material. You're just doing a bit.

[contemplative] It's funny — that tension between genuine exploration and performance exists everywhere in improv, but it's most visible in the opening because there's no scene to hide behind.

[warm] That's exactly right. There's no character, no relationship, no stakes except the real ones — can this group of people think together? Can they listen to each other? Can they build something none of them could build alone?

[short pause]

[teaching] You know what I find beautiful about it? The opening is the one moment in a Harold where the ensemble is explicitly doing the thing improv promises. Not pretending to collaborate. Actually collaborating. Free-associating out loud, in public, trusting that the connections will emerge.

[curious] And then you let it go.

[confident] And then you let it go. You take the palette into the scenes and you don't look back. The opening did its job. The themes are in your bones now. Trust that.

[contemplative] It's like stretching before a run. You don't think about the stretches while you're running. But your body knows.

[warm] Your body knows. The ensemble knows. That's the whole thing.
