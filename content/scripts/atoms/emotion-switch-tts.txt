[curious] Emotion Switch. I've seen versions of this but I want to hear what it actually trains.

[teaching] It trains changeability. The ability to fully shift your emotional state in response to input. Not performing a shift. Actually shifting.

[conversational] How does it work?

[matter-of-fact] Two players in a scene. A coach periodically calls out an emotion. Joy. Grief. Anger. Tenderness. Every ten to twenty seconds, faster as the players get better. Both players must transition to that emotion immediately, but here's the key constraint. [short pause] It has to be justified within the context of the scene. The facts don't change. Only the emotional reality shifts.

[curious] So they keep the same scene going but the feeling underneath completely changes?

[emphatic] Exactly. And there's a deeper instruction that makes this work. Don't just switch faces. Let the new emotion change what you want from your scene partner. [short pause] "Pass the salt" in grief is completely different from "pass the salt" in joy. The words are the same. What you need from the other person is totally different.

[contemplative] That's a subtle distinction. Just switching the expression versus letting it change the want.

[teaching] And that's where the side-coaching gets specific. If players are indicating, just putting on an angry face without actually feeling it, the coach asks, "What do you want from them now?" If they drop the scene to service the emotion, "Stay in the conversation." If every emotion lands at the same low intensity, "That's a three out of ten sad." [short pause] And a big one. If they transition through neutral, going from happy to blank to angry, the call is "No airlock. Straight across."

[curious] What do people get wrong most often?

[serious] The biggest failure is performing the emotion instead of having it. You can see it immediately. The body knows the difference between felt emotion and performed emotion. [short pause] The second failure is dropping the scene. Players get so focused on the emotion call that they forget they're in a conversation with another person about something.

[warm] There's a beautiful paradox at the heart of this exercise. You commit completely to each emotional state, and you release each state completely when the next one arrives. Commitment and adaptability turn out to be the same muscle.

[conversational] How does this transfer to regular scenes?

[emphatic] When your partner says something that should change you, and you feel the instinct to stay the same, that's exactly the muscle this exercise trains. The goal is to make the emotional shift feel like relief rather than loss.
