[teaching] Presence is internal. It's your attention, your listening, your connection to what's happening right now. Stage presence is the outward projection of that. It's the bridge between your inner truth and the audience actually receiving it.

[matter-of-fact] And then there's the stage itself. Inexperienced improvisers cluster at center stage like penguins on an ice floe. Strong stage presence uses the full width, the full depth, and — this is the one people forget — levels.

[curious] Levels. Sitting, standing, kneeling.

[teaching] Lying down. Crouching. Standing on a chair. When everyone's standing at the same height having a conversation, it's visually flat. The moment someone sits or kneels, the picture changes. You're communicating status, relationship, power — all without saying a word.

[curious] Now here's the one that surprises me every time I see it work. Stillness.

[teaching] Stillness is the secret weapon. Because our nervous system, when we're anxious on stage, tells us to move. Shift weight. Touch our face. Pace. And all of that reads as nervous energy. The audience feels it.

[emphatic] But when someone can stand still and hold space — just be there, grounded, not fidgeting — that reads as power. Johnstone mapped this directly. High-status characters move less. They take up more space. They hold eye contact longer. They pause before responding. Their head stays still.

[curious] And low status is the opposite.

[teaching] Contracting. Fidgeting. Breaking eye contact. Trailing off mid-sentence. And here's the key — these are signals, not personality. A performer deploys them consciously. You're not a high-status person or a low-status person on stage. You're choosing to project those signals for the scene.

[contemplative] Which means stage presence isn't some innate charisma you either have or don't. It's a set of physical choices you can practice.
