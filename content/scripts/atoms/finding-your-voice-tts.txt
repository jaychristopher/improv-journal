[curious] So here's something that bugs me. Everyone talks about "finding your voice" in improv, but nobody can tell you how to actually do it. It's like the one thing you can't put on a syllabus.

[teaching] Because it's not a thing you do. It's a thing that happens to you. Your voice is the pattern that emerges after thousands of choices on stage. What you reach for, what you avoid, how you handle silence, what makes you lean in. You don't design it any more than you design your handwriting.

[curious] But you can look at someone's handwriting and know it's theirs.

[warm] Exactly. And you can watch TJ Jagodowski do a scene and know it's him within thirty seconds. Not because he's doing a bit. Because of what he doesn't do. He doesn't rush. He doesn't grab the easy laugh. He sits in a moment way longer than you'd think possible, and he finds the real feeling underneath the comedy. His voice is defined as much by his restraint as his choices.

[curious] That's interesting — voice as what you consistently don't do.

[teaching] Compare that with Matt Besser. Sharp, aggressive, relentless game play. Where TJ lingers in emotion, Besser accelerates through logic. He finds the pattern and he heightens it until it breaks. His voice was so distinctive it literally became encoded in UCB's entire pedagogy. A whole institution shaped by one person's instincts about what's funny.

[curious] And then you've got someone like Susan Messing, who's basically the opposite of restraint.

[warm] Total opposite. Radical commitment, zero self-censorship. She goes further than anyone expects — emotionally, physically, energetically. Her voice is the absence of a filter. And then Razowsky, who's all about present-moment awareness, being changed by each moment. Meditative, surprising, Viewpoints-influenced. Four completely different voices, all undeniably distinctive.

[short pause]

[curious] Okay but here's what nags at me. Improv is collaborative. "Serve the scene, not yourself." If everyone's busy expressing their distinctive voice, aren't we just doing parallel monologues?

[teaching] That's the tension everyone feels but nobody names well enough. And the resolution is actually beautiful. A strong voice doesn't dominate — it contributes a distinctive color to the palette. Think about TJ and Dave together. Two very strong, very different voices that combine into something neither would create alone. That's what the best ensembles do. Cook County Social Club, same thing. The individual voices don't compete. They compose.

[contemplative] It's like a band. You don't want five guitarists who all sound the same. You want a bassist who sounds like a bassist and a drummer who sounds like a drummer. The ensemble gets richer when each person brings something only they can bring.

[curious] So when does voice become a problem? Because I've definitely seen performers who have a "thing" and they just do it every scene regardless.

[serious] That's the line between voice and schtick. And it matters. Voice is a consistent set of values and instincts that adapts to context. Schtick is a repeated set of moves that ignores context. Voice serves the scene through a distinctive lens. Schtick serves the performer regardless of what the scene needs.

[short pause]

[curious] You can feel that difference as an audience member, right? One feels alive and the other feels like a rerun.

[emphatic] Every time. The schtick performer, you know what they're going to do before they do it. The performer with a genuine voice — you recognize them, but you can't predict them. That's the distinction. Recognition without prediction.

[curious] So how do you develop voice without falling into schtick?

[teaching] You don't develop it. That's the counterintuitive part. You do the work and it develops you. Johnstone had this frame — your voice is what remains when you stop trying to be someone else. It's not additive, it's subtractive. You strip away the things you think you should be doing, the moves you borrowed from people you admire, the habits you picked up from whichever system trained you. What's left is yours.

[curious] Wait — "whichever system trained you." That's a whole thing, isn't it? UCB performers sound like UCB performers. iO performers sound like iO performers.

[warm] It's real. Your voice is both individual and institutional. The system you train in shapes your instincts, your vocabulary, what you think a good scene looks like. Part of artistic maturity is recognizing which of your instincts are genuinely yours and which ones are just the water you've been swimming in.

[contemplative] That's actually true way beyond improv. Your taste in anything — music, food, how you run a meeting — is partly yours and partly the culture you absorbed it from. Knowing the difference is a lifetime project.

[curious] There's also this thing where younger improvisers try to find their voice too early, right?

[serious] And it backfires. They end up performing a character of themselves instead of being present. Voice requires a foundation. Years of it. You need enough technique that you're not thinking about technique anymore. Then the choices that emerge from that freedom — those are your voice.

[short pause]

[teaching] Think of it this way. A beginning writer who tries to "write in their voice" produces something self-conscious and mannered. A writer with twenty years of practice just writes, and their voice is unmistakable. The voice was always there. The craft had to catch up to it.

[curious] So the advice is basically... don't worry about it?

[warm] The advice is do the work. Do an enormous volume of work. Pay attention to what keeps showing up. Notice what you're drawn to and what repels you. Notice what you do when you're not trying to do anything. That's your voice, waving at you from inside the work you've already done.

[contemplative] And maybe the most honest version of it — your voice isn't the thing that makes you special. It's the thing that makes you specific. Which, on stage and off, turns out to be far more useful.
