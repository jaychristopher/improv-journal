[curious] Status Transfer. This is Johnstone territory. What's the exercise?

[teaching] Two players. One begins high status, confident, still, expansive. The other begins low status, fidgeting, deferential, small. They play a scene honestly. Make offers, listen. And at some point during the scene, the status shifts. [short pause] Don't plan when or how. Let it happen when something lands.

[conversational] Give me an example of that shift.

[warm] A boss is dressing down an employee about missing a deadline. The boss is high, the employee is low. Then the employee reveals the deadline was missed because the boss's spouse called the office in tears. [short pause] The boss's posture collapses. The employee straightens. Becomes the one with information, with composure. Status transferred. And you can see it with the sound off.

[curious] What does this train?

[emphatic] Status awareness and the ability to shift status dynamically within a scene. Status lives in the body before the words. Watch for the physical signals. Who's making eye contact, who's breaking it. Who's still, who's fidgeting. Who's taking up space, who's shrinking.

[contemplative] What goes wrong?

[serious] Three common failures. Both players drop status simultaneously, the dead zone where neither commits to high. The coach says "Someone take the power." [short pause] Volume equals status, where players signal high through aggression and loudness instead of stillness and space. The coach says "Quieter. Slower. Status is in the body." And the single-moment flip, where the transfer happens as one dramatic reversal instead of a gradual seesaw. Status shifts in inches, not explosions.

[curious] That last one is interesting. It should be gradual?

[teaching] The most dynamic scenes have status shifting throughout, not locked in from the top. It's a constant seesaw. One person rises a little, the other drops a little, then it reverses. [short pause] The exercise trains the hardest form of letting yourself be changed. Yielding power. That's identity-level change.

[conversational] Is there a variation?

[matter-of-fact] Status Party. Four or more players at a party. Each is secretly assigned a number one through ten, ten being highest. They interact freely. After three minutes, everyone must find their place in line from highest to lowest without speaking. [short pause] Tests whether you can read status as well as play it.

[emphatic] The deeper lesson is that status is not who you are. It's what you do. And it's always in motion.
