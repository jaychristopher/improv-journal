[curious] Group Mind Cultivation. This sounds abstract. What are we actually doing?

[teaching] It's a collection of exercises that build collective attention. Shared decision-making without verbal negotiation. This isn't mystical. It's the practical result of deep mutual attention. [short pause] I'll walk you through the key exercises.

[conversational] Let's hear them.

[matter-of-fact] The first is Counting to Twenty. The group stands in a circle with eyes closed. Goal is to count from one to twenty, one number at a time, with only one person speaking at a time. If two people speak simultaneously, restart at one. No system, no pattern, no turn-taking. Only listening and impulse.

[curious] That sounds impossibly frustrating.

[warm] It is at first. But it teaches the group to feel collective readiness and hesitation. You start sensing when someone else is about to speak. [short pause] The second exercise is Simultaneous Movement. The group moves through space without a designated leader. Someone begins to walk, others follow. The group turns, stops, speeds up, slows down as a unit. The goal is to make it impossible for an observer to tell who's leading.

[contemplative] So the leadership is distributed.

[teaching] Exactly. The third exercise is Group Hum. The ensemble creates a collective sound, a hum or a tone or a rhythm, and modulates it together. Louder, softer, faster, slower. [short pause] All organically, no conductor. It trains collective sensitivity to energy levels and transitions.

[curious] Is there a progression? Do you do these in order?

[emphatic] Yes. The progression goes mirroring with two people, to counting and group sound with the whole ensemble on simple tasks, to simultaneous movement which is more complex, and finally to Viewpoints work which is the most sophisticated. Each level asks the group to coordinate more dimensions of experience without talking about it.

[conversational] What's the failure mode?

[serious] The pragmatic critique is real. Counting to twenty in a circle is different from navigating a Harold together. These exercises don't always transfer reliably to performance. [short pause] Some teachers argue the best group mind training is just performing together frequently.

[warm] But here's what these exercises do that performance alone doesn't. They make the invisible visible. You can actually feel the moment when the group clicks. When five people turn at the same time without a signal. That felt experience of connection becomes something you can recognize and seek in performance.
