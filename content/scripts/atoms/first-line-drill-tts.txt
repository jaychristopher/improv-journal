[curious] First Line Drill. This sounds like it's about getting over the fear of starting?

[emphatic] Exactly. It isolates the single hardest moment in improv. The threshold. The leap of stepping out and saying something before you're ready.

[conversational] Walk me through the setup.

[teaching] Players line up. One at a time, each steps out and delivers a single declarative first line that commits to something. A feeling, a relationship, a situation. [short pause] No response. No scene. Just the first line, then sit down. Next player. Run it rapid-fire, at least two or three rounds, so everyone gets past their first attempt.

[curious] What makes a good first line?

[matter-of-fact] Specificity and conviction. "Mom, I know you said not to call, but the house is on fire." "Doctor, I've been waiting in this room for two hours and I'm starting to think the test results are bad." "I can't believe you wore that to Dad's funeral." [short pause] Notice how every one of those instantly creates a world. A relationship. Stakes. You lean in.

[conversational] And what makes a bad one?

[serious] Vague hedging. "So, uh, what do you want to do?" That's not an initiation, that's a dodge. The core rule is commit fully, don't hedge. One specific commitment is enough. You don't need to establish everything.

[curious] What does the coaching sound like during this?

[energetic] Fast and direct. "Don't think, go." "Faster." "You already know what to say." "Let the line be boring, just say it." [short pause] The teacher should praise speed over cleverness. And call it out when someone is visibly crafting in their head before stepping out. That crafting pause is the exact habit this drill breaks.

[contemplative] What if people freeze up?

[warm] That's expected. There's a great warm-up for this. Before the drill, have each player step forward, take a big theatrical bow, and say "I failed!" The group cheers. It defuses the fear of exposure before you even start the real exercise. [short pause] The fear before stepping out is almost always worse than the moment itself. The line doesn't have to be brilliant. It has to exist.

[conversational] How does this transfer to real scenes?

[emphatic] Every scene begins with someone making this leap. The drill isolates the leap itself. No scene to sustain, no partner to manage, just the raw act of starting. Once that threshold muscle is strong, initiating in full scenes becomes automatic.
