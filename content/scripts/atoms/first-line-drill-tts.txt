[curious] The threshold moment. That tiny gap between knowing you should go and actually going.

[teaching] That's exactly what Be Brave isolates. It's a drill, dead simple in structure. Players line up. One at a time, you step out, deliver a single first line — declarative, committed — and sit down. No scene. No partner response. Just the line.

[curious] Just the line. That's it?

[teaching] That's it. And it's terrifying. Because there's nowhere to hide. You can't defer to your partner, you can't ask a question to buy time, you can't feel out the room first. You step out, you say the thing, you sit down.

[teaching] Specific ones. "Mom, I know you said not to call, but the house is on fire." "I can't believe you wore that to Dad's funeral." Lines that commit to a feeling, a relationship, a situation. One clear stake in the ground.

[curious] Not "So, uh, what do you want to do?"

[teaching] Right. No hedging. No vague openers. The whole point is that the line lands with conviction. It doesn't have to be brilliant. It has to exist.

[short pause]

[curious] Because you stopped blocking yourself.

[teaching] Exactly. The drill runs rapid-fire — two, three rounds minimum — because you need to get past your first attempt. First time, everyone's performing. By the third round, people are just talking. And the lines get better. Not because they're trying harder, but because they've stopped trying so hard.

[curious] I've seen coaches do a thing before this where everyone takes a bow and says "I failed." What's that about?

[warm] The Circus Bow. Matt Smith and Edward Sampson brought it out of Seattle improv in the mid-eighties — they borrowed it from trapeze artists, actually. You step forward, big theatrical bow, announce "I failed!" and everyone cheers.

[curious] Defuses the bomb before the drill even starts.

[teaching] It survives the spotlight for you. Once you've already performed failure and gotten applause for it, stepping out with a mediocre first line doesn't feel like a risk anymore. The worst thing already happened and it was a party.

[curious] What does the side-coaching sound like on this one?

[matter-of-fact] Fast. "Don't think — go." "Faster." "You already know what to say." And the crucial one — "Let the line be boring. Just say it." You're watching for that visible moment where someone is crafting in their head before they step out. That pause, that look up and to the left. That's the exact habit the drill is designed to break.

[curious] You're praising speed over cleverness.

[emphatic] Every time. The clever line that took four seconds of visible thought teaches nothing. The boring line that came out instantly? That's the muscle firing. [contemplative] And that's the real lesson, isn't it? Every line, no matter how simple, creates a world. You step out, you say one committed thing, and suddenly there's a relationship, an environment, stakes. From nothing.

[teaching] From nothing. Which means the fear that you have nothing to say is always a lie. You have something to say. You just haven't said it yet.

[teaching] Yeah — Do Something First. You walk out sweeping, or pacing, or crying, and you hold it for five seconds before you speak. Forces the body to lead. Your mouth can't script the line if your arms are already busy mopping a floor.

[warm] And the line that comes out is almost always more grounded. Because you're in something. You're not standing at the edge of a cliff deciding whether to jump — you already jumped and now you're just describing what you see on the way down.
