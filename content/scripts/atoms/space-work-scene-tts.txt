[curious] So here's a question. When was the last time you did a scene where you actually knew where you were? Not because someone said "welcome to my office" — but because you could feel the desk under your hands?

[teaching] That's the thing. Most of us treat space work like set dressing. A little mime at the top to establish location, then we abandon it completely and just talk at each other. Trains strips all of that away. No dialogue. Two people. Build a world with your hands.

[curious] No words at all?

[teaching] None. Two to three minutes of pure physical interaction. With the environment, with each other. And what happens is kind of remarkable — you find out how much story you've been leaving on the table.

[short pause]

[curious] I've done versions of this, and the thing that always surprises me is how nervous people get. Like, experienced players. People who can handle any suggestion. You take away their words and suddenly they're lost.

[teaching] Because dialogue is a crutch. And I don't mean that dismissively — it's a powerful tool. But it lets you skip the physical world entirely. You can say "hand me that wrench" without ever building the wrench. You can say "this place is freezing" without your body showing it. Words let you narrate instead of inhabit. Trains forces you to actually live somewhere.

[contemplative] And the wild part is, the audience reads it. They read everything. Two people doing dishes — one washing, one drying — and the whole relationship is in the silence between them. How close they stand. Whether they make eye contact. Who reaches for the next plate first.

[curious] Right, it's like the physical choices become the dialogue.

[teaching] They ARE dialogue. Every object you touch is an offer. Every step toward your partner or away from them is a line. And when you're really in it — when you slow down enough to feel the weight of the glass in your hand, the temperature of the water — the audience leans in. They're reading a story that nobody's telling them. They're building it themselves from what they see.

[short pause]

[curious] What goes wrong? Because I've also seen this exercise fall completely flat.

[matter-of-fact] Three things, almost always. First — speed. People mime too fast. They're washing dishes at triple speed because they're anxious, and suddenly the objects have no weight. A plate becomes a flick of the wrist. The water's not wet. You've lost the whole world because you rushed through it.

[teaching] Second — charades. Instead of living in the space, people start performing AT the audience. They pick up a "big heavy thing" with exaggerated strain, mugging for the laugh. That's not space work. That's pantomime. The difference is whether you're in the room or presenting the room.

[curious] And the third?

[serious] Ignoring your partner. Two people doing solo space work in the same room. They're each building their own little world, and there's no shared reality between them. The whole point is that you're in this together. If your partner puts a cup down on the counter, that cup exists now. You can pick it up. You can move it. You can knock it off the edge. But you have to see it first.

[pause]

[contemplative] That last one feels like it's about more than space work.

[warm] It's about everything. How often in a scene — or honestly, in life — are you so busy building your own thing that you don't notice what someone just put down right in front of you? Your partner makes a choice, and you walk right past it because you had your own plan.

[teaching] The silence in Trains makes that impossible to hide. When there are no words, all you have is attention. You're either watching your partner or you're not. You're either responding to what they just did or you're ignoring it. There's nowhere to hide.

[curious] There's a variation, right? One word per minute?

[teaching] Yeah, and it's brutal in the best way. Same setup, but someone's timing you, and each player gets exactly one word per minute. So you might go ninety seconds of pure physical work and then someone says — "stay." And it lands like a thunderbolt because the silence gave it all that weight.

[contemplative] It teaches you what words are actually for.

[teaching] Exactly. Words should be the thing you say when the body isn't enough. Not the first resort. The last one. When you've been doing dishes in silence for two minutes and your scene partner finally says one word — that word carries everything the silence built. It's enormous.

[short pause]

[curious] So how does this transfer? You can't do a whole Harold in silence.

[matter-of-fact] No, but here's the test. In any scene, ask yourself — if the sound cut out right now, would the audience still know where we are? Would they know how these people feel about each other? If the answer is no, your space work has decayed. You've floated up into talking heads.

[teaching] And that's most scenes, honestly. Two people standing center stage, nothing in their hands, not relating to any environment, just talking. The words might be great. But the world is empty.

[warm] When you've done Trains — when you've really felt what it's like to build a kitchen with your hands and have an entire audience understand a marriage without a single word — you don't let that go. You bring it back into every scene. The door has weight. The coffee is hot. The distance between you and your partner means something.

[contemplative] It's funny. We spend so much time learning what to say in scenes. Maybe the deeper skill is learning what the room looks like while you're saying it.

[teaching] Spolin knew that sixty years ago. She built entire curricula around "where" before she ever got to "what" or "who." The environment isn't the background. It's the foundation. Everything you say, everything you feel — it happens somewhere. And if that somewhere is real, everything on top of it gets more real too.

[warm] So next time you're in a scene and you feel yourself reaching for a clever line — try reaching for a doorknob instead. See what your hand knows that your mouth doesn't.
