[curious] Emotional Honesty Scene. This sounds like it could get intense. What are we doing here?

[teaching] Two players get a specific shared physical activity. Folding laundry. Assembling furniture. Doing dishes. Something physical, not conceptual. And the constraint is one positive instruction. [short pause] Mean everything you say.

[conversational] That's it? Just be honest?

[serious] That's it, and it's the hardest thing in improv. If a joke happens and you mean it, fine. What's off limits is performing a version of a feeling designed to get a reaction rather than actually having the feeling. [short pause] There's a second layer too. Respond to what you actually see in your partner, not to the topic. Read their body, their breathing, their eye contact. Let what you notice in them determine what you say.

[contemplative] So it's not about creating drama. It's about truth.

[warm] Exactly. If the truth is awkward silence while you both stare at a receipt, that's the scene. If the truth is quiet fear about money, that's the scene. The goal is not drama. The goal is truth. Keep it to two or three minutes. Sincerity fatigue is real past that.

[curious] What does the side-coaching sound like?

[matter-of-fact] When someone starts narrating feelings instead of having them, "Don't report it, have it." When something genuine lands and the performer starts to bail, "Stay with that." When attention drifts to the topic instead of the partner, "What do you actually see in them right now?" And this is important. When the scene starts feeling performatively somber, "This isn't about being serious. It's about meaning what you say."

[curious] What goes wrong?

[teaching] People perform sincerity. They put on a quiet voice and a serious face and think that's honesty. But that's just another mask. [short pause] The other failure is bailing. Something real surfaces, a genuine feeling, and the performer panics and makes a joke or changes the subject. That bail-out moment is exactly where the exercise does its work.

[contemplative] Why is this so hard?

[emphatic] Because sincerity on stage feels exposed. There's no ironic distance to hide behind. No character to blame. You're just you, meaning what you say, in front of people watching. [short pause] The bravery is the exercise.

[warm] And here's what's beautiful. When it works, the room goes quiet and leans in, even though "nothing is happening." Mundane truth is more compelling than manufactured wackiness. Every time.
