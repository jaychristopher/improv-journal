[curious] Mirroring. Everyone's done this one. But I suspect there's more to it than people think.

[teaching] There is. This is Viola Spolin's core exercise. Taught at every school, in every first class. But most people only experience the first stage. There are three, and the third is where the real training lives.

[conversational] Walk me through all three.

[matter-of-fact] Stage one. Two players face each other. Player A moves slowly. Player B mirrors A's movement as precisely as possible. Sustained eye contact. No speaking. B's job is to match A so exactly that an observer can't tell who initiates. A's job is to move slowly enough that B can follow. [short pause] Speed is the enemy. The slower you go, the deeper the connection.

[curious] And then you switch?

[teaching] Stage two. B leads, A follows. Same discipline. [short pause] Stage three is the exercise's peak. Neither leads. This is called Follow the Follower. "Who is the mirror?" becomes unanswerable. Both players follow. Movement emerges from the space between them rather than from either individual.

[contemplative] That sounds almost magical when it clicks.

[warm] It is. In stage three, a kind of group mind emerges at the dyad level. Two people moving as one organism. This is the smallest possible version of ensemble coherence. [short pause] If you can do it with one person, you can learn to do it with seven.

[curious] What goes wrong?

[serious] Moving too fast because ego wants to be interesting. Anticipating instead of responding, where planning leaks into the body. Breaking eye contact to check your own body. And leading when you're supposed to follow, which is just steering. [short pause] The side-coaching is almost always the same word. "Slower."

[conversational] Why is speed such a problem?

[emphatic] Because speed is control. When you move fast, you're making it impossible for your partner to follow, which means you get to lead. Slowing down is an act of generosity. It creates space for genuine mutual responsiveness.

[warm] Here's the deeper lesson. Every improv scene is a mirror exercise with words. You're never just generating. You're always responding to what your partner gives you, and they're responding to what you give them. [short pause] The mirror makes this visible. When it works, neither player leads. The scene leads.
