[curious] "Be Simple." This feels counterintuitive. Aren't the best improvisers the ones who come up with the most creative, complex stuff?

[warm] That's what our egos tell us. We want to be the cleverest person in the room. [short pause] But Keith Johnstone discovered the opposite: "The improviser has to realise that the more obvious he is, the more original he appears."

[conversational] How can obvious be original?

[teaching] Think about what happens when you say "happy birthday" versus "I'm a cyborg from the future here to steal your toaster." With "happy birthday," the relationship has room to breathe. The audience doesn't need five minutes of exposition. They immediately project the history, the emotion, the stakes. [short pause] The scene can live in the space between two people rather than inside one person's elaborate premise.

[curious] So simplicity creates space?

[emphatic] Simplicity is not the absence of depth. It is the condition for depth. [pause] And there's a concrete mechanism behind this. The audience has limited processing capacity. A complex plot with three layers of irony is a massive download. The connection chokes and they disengage. But "I'm sad because you ate my cake" downloads instantly.

[conversational] It downloads instantly, and then what?

[warm] And then something magical happens. It activates the audience's imagination. They co-create the relationship, the history, the stakes. Simplicity doesn't just avoid overload. It invites participation. [short pause] That's why simple scenes often feel richer than complicated ones. The audience is doing half the work.

[curious] Is there a generosity angle to this?

[confident] Absolutely. A simple offer is a gift to your partner because it's immediately usable. They can receive and build on it without cognitive overhead. [short pause] Overcomplication often reads as low status, reaching for approval. Economy reads as grounded. You choose to be legible because you value your partner's ability to play.

[conversational] But saying something simple on stage sounds terrifying. "I love you" is way scarier than a monologue about quantum physics.

[teaching] And that's exactly why Be Brave is a prerequisite. Simple offers strip away armor. There is nothing to hide behind. [short pause] Mick Napier adds that simplicity isn't itself the goal, but the natural consequence of commitment. Fully commit to your first impulse and the result is naturally simple. The directive "be simple" risks becoming one more rule to self-monitor.

[curious] Does this apply to emotions too?

[matter-of-fact] Especially to emotions. One clear feeling is more playable than a complicated emotional cocktail. "I'm angry" gives your partner ground. "I'm angry but also nostalgic and a little amused" gives them nothing to grab. [pause] The success condition is straightforward. Your partner can immediately receive and build on your offer. The audience can follow without explanation. And the relationship has room to develop.

[contemplative] The counter-position comes from UCB's game framework, which asks improvisers to identify and heighten patterns, a more analytical process. The tension is real: simplicity in execution versus sophistication in pattern recognition. [short pause] But even in that framework, each individual move should be simple enough to track.
