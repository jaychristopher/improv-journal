[curious] "Be Changeable." Isn't that the opposite of commitment? I thought we were supposed to commit fully to our choices.

[teaching] That's the most common confusion, and it's worth untangling. [short pause] You can accept every offer your partner makes. You can stay present, be thankful, follow all the other principles, and still remain fundamentally rigid. Absorbing inputs politely while your internal state stays fixed. Be Changeable closes that loophole.

[conversational] What do you mean by "internal state stays fixed"?

[warm] Picture this. Your partner says "you're afraid of heights." You say "yes, I am." That's acceptance. That's Be Positive. But your posture doesn't shift. Your energy doesn't change. Your voice stays exactly the same. [short pause] You accepted the data without letting it touch you.

[curious] So what does changeability actually look like?

[emphatic] When your partner says you're afraid of heights, your knees buckle slightly. Your hands grip the railing. And you say something like, "I've always been afraid of heights. That's why I took this desk job at the agency." [short pause] You didn't just accept the offer. You let it rewrite your character's history. Will Hines calls this rewriting history, giving the present a past.

[conversational] That's a great example. But where does this principle come from theoretically?

[contemplative] Keith Johnstone built his entire pedagogy around the observation that improvisers instinctively resist alteration. It's a survival instinct, protecting the self from change. His narrative exercises and status work are all designed to override that defensive reflex. He framed it as the fundamental engine of drama: one person being changed by another. Without change, there is no story.

[curious] And Viola Spolin?

[warm] Spolin comes at it from the opposite direction. Where Johnstone sees resistance you need to override, Spolin sees changeability as the natural consequence of genuine presence. If you're truly present to the shared focus, you don't need to will yourself to change. The change happens because you're actually there.

[conversational] OK, but help me with the commitment paradox. How can I commit fully and also be changeable?

[teaching] Commitment creates the conditions for visible change. [short pause] A character with no commitments can't be changed, because there's nothing to change. You commit fully to this moment's truth. When your partner's offer shifts the ground, you commit fully to the new truth. The audience sees the arc because the starting position was clear.

[matter-of-fact] Commitment without changeability is rigidity. Changeability without commitment is shapelessness.

[curious] So what happens when a scene has acceptance but not changeability?

[serious] You get what people call "polite improv." Everyone agrees, nobody is moved. The offers are accepted but they don't land. The shared reality exists but nothing is at stake because nothing is changing anyone. [pause] TJ and Dave's philosophy, "look to your partner, listen to your partner, respond to your partner," suggests that changeability isn't even a separate skill. It's the inevitable result of deep listening. If you're genuinely hearing your partner, you cannot remain unchanged.

[contemplative] So resistance to change is really just a symptom of not listening?

[confident] That's the deep insight. Listening, at its deepest, is the willingness to be changed. [short pause] Lisa Rowland names it explicitly: changeability is ultimately a question of faith. Whether you trust the unfolding scene enough to let it reshape you.
