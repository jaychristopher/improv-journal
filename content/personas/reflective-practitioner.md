# The Reflective Practitioner

## Profile

Intermediate improvisers — roughly Level 3-4, 1-3 years of training — who have internalized the basics but hit a plateau they can't diagnose. They can do scenes. They know yes-and. They've performed in shows. But they can't articulate WHY some scenes work and others don't. They feel like they're "going through the motions" without growing. They need vocabulary for self-coaching, diagnostic frameworks for analyzing their own work, and the confidence to develop without constant teacher feedback.

The plateau is real AND perceptual. Research confirms: knowledge (conceptual understanding) grows faster than ability (execution), creating a widening gap that FEELS like regression even when it's growth. The Reflective Practitioner needs both the truth ("you are improving") and the tools ("here's how to see it and steer it").

## Jobs to Be Done

**Functional:**
1. **Diagnose what's going wrong in scenes** — "I know it didn't work but I can't tell you why." They need a vocabulary for failure modes (bulldozing, steering, problem-prov, nicey-nice, robotic game).
2. **Self-coach between classes** — Break dependency on teacher notes. Holly Mandel's 10-point self-assessment framework exists precisely because this gap is real.
3. **Break through specific technical plateaus** — Weak group scenes, can't find game consistently, editing instincts are off, scenes feel flat.

**Emotional:**
4. **Regain the joy** — Carrane: "I have been improvising for six years now, and the problem is I don't know if I am getting any better." The measuring kills the play.
5. **Validate that the plateau is normal** — "At the 2-year mark, every improviser begins to tell themselves the same thing: 'I am terrible at improv.'" — Improv College

**Social:**
6. **Earn respect from peers and teachers** — Move from "competent" to "someone you want to play with." Stop being merely reliable and start being surprising.

## Pains

### 1. The Plateau (~2 years / Level 3-4)
Universal consensus across every source. Not a myth — a structural inevitability.

> "No matter what you try, no matter how you practice, no matter how much you beat yourself up, you will be, at best, merely competent at your craft." — "The Vast Plateau of Competency," The House That Del Built

> Second City identifies THREE ruts: the Level B Rut (overcriticism), the Second-Year Rut (plateau that "feels like regression" after completing training), and the First Great Scene Rut (fixating on replicating a transcendent moment). — Second City, "Surviving the Improv Ruts"

> "You're learning about improv faster than your ability to execute is growing." — Improv College, "Here's Why You're Getting Worse at Improv"

**Atoms that address this:** `systemic-health-indicators`, `systemic-collapse-modes`, `decay-recovery`, `latency-recovery`

### 2. Can't Self-Diagnose
The core structural problem. They feel bad but can't locate the cause.

> Hines: improvisers feel "terrible" and worry they're "the worst person in your class" while not knowing what requires improvement. — Will Hines, "What Am I Doing Wrong?"

> When UCB coach Andy Secunda told a team "you are not saying yes," they understood intellectually but couldn't improve. "Students often can't self-diagnose." — Will Hines, "An Exercise Is Better Than A Note"

> "At the 2-year mark, coaches' feedback transforms from welcomed guidance into defensive ammunition, and any performance choice failing to generate laughter becomes emotionally devastating." — Medium

**Atoms that address this:** `internal-computation`, `judgment`, `cognitive-bandwidth`, `hesitation`, `performing-cleverness`

### 3. Vocabulary Gap
They lack the language to name what's happening — to themselves or to scene partners.

> Holly Mandel developed a self-assessment system that provides "a shared language for something that's usually felt rather than articulated." With it: "You can identify where connection tightened, where commitment dropped, or where curiosity opened new possibilities." — hollymandel.com

> Hines compiles diagnostic phrases from various teachers because no unified vocabulary exists: "If you don't know what to say, it's because you don't know who you are" (Craig Cackowski). — Will Hines, "Favorite Advice"

> The 75-25% Rule diagnoses "robotic improvisation" — improvisers who "think of a game move before their scene partner has finished talking." — Will Hines / John McInnes

**Atoms that address this:** `game-of-the-scene`, `heightening`, `rest-beat`, `discovery`, `scene-structure`, `coherence`

### 4. Performing but Not Growing
Going through the motions. Same characters, same moves, same comfort zone.

> "Improv-vu, doing the same flat, uninspired, boring scenes over and over again. Pulling the same stock characters out, making the same choices." — Jimmy Carrane, "How Do You Get Out of an Improv Rut?"

> Carrane played the same character (stern, uptight dad discovering children use drugs) "hundreds of times."

> A former student after six years: "I have been improvising for six years now, and the problem is I don't know if I am getting any better." Carrane's insight: "If you don't care if you are getting better, you get better faster." — Jimmy Carrane, "Want to Get Better? Just Have Fun"

**Atoms that address this:** `be-changeable`, `let-yourself-be-changed`, `be-brave`, `discovery`, `commitment`

### 5. Teacher Dependency
Can't grow without external feedback. Rules learned in class become cages.

> After two years of training, one improviser felt "paralyzed — overthinking every choice due to accumulated rules and restrictions." Rules create the hesitation they should eliminate. — People and Chairs review of Napier's *Improvise*

> Even with a coach, verbal feedback ("you are not saying yes") failed to produce change. If coaches can't fix things with notes, how can improvisers coach themselves? — Will Hines, "An Exercise Is Better Than A Note"

**Atoms that address this:** `fear-of-failure`, `obvious-choice`, `be-honest`, `emotional-truth`

### 6. Common Technical Traps
Specific failure modes that teachers see constantly at the intermediate level.

> Three most common mistakes: (1) saying too much information, (2) using words without emotion, (3) scenes too "nicey-nice" — avoiding conflict, mistaking yes-and for perpetual agreeableness. — Jimmy Carrane

> "Problem-prov": scenes centering on solving a declared problem. "This approach feels natural because it mirrors narrative structure from scripted stories." It represents "playing it safe." — Will Hines, "The Problem with Problem-Prov"

> Modern improv training "raised the floor" (reliable basic techniques) but "lowered the ceiling" (limited artistic ambition). — Will Hines, "How Do We Raise The Improv Ceiling?"

**Atoms that address this:** `bulldozing`, `steering`, `overcomplication`, `performing-cleverness`, `hesitation`

## Desires

1. **A diagnostic vocabulary** — language for naming what's happening in scenes, not just feeling it. The graph's antipattern atoms (bulldozing, steering, hesitation, performing-cleverness, overcomplication, internal-computation) ARE this vocabulary.
2. **Self-coaching frameworks** — tools for analyzing their own work without a teacher present. The systems atoms (health indicators, collapse modes, decay-recovery) provide this.
3. **Permission to be average** — Second City: "It's okay to be average." The plateau is normal. Rich Baker did 5,000 scenes before consistency.
4. **Reconnection to joy** — Carrane: "I am 'method' when it comes to joy. I need a little in my life to draw from." The be-thankful principle reframes practice as gift, not grind.
5. **Specific technical unlocks** — Not more fundamentals, but targeted interventions: game recognition, editing instincts, group scene focus, emotional honesty.

## Objections

1. **"I already know this stuff"** — They know the terminology. They don't have the diagnostic depth. The graph goes deeper than any single curriculum.
2. **"Reading about improv doesn't help"** — Partly true. But the vocabulary gap is real — you can't fix what you can't name. The graph provides the naming.
3. **"I just need more reps"** — Maybe. But undirected reps entrench bad habits. Deliberate practice requires knowing WHAT to practice.
4. **"This is too intellectual / analytical"** — Valid concern. The graph must pair every concept with exercises and embodied practice, not just theory.

## Journey Map

### Entry Point: "Why do some scenes work and others don't?"
Start with diagnostics, not fundamentals. They already know yes-and. They need the WHY behind what they already DO.

**Phase 1: The Diagnostic Frame** (Systems + Antipatterns)
1. `systemic-health-indicators` — What does a healthy scene/show/ensemble look like?
2. `systemic-collapse-modes` — The 7 ways scenes die (and which one keeps killing yours)
3. `internal-computation` — Recognizing when you're in your head
4. `performing-cleverness` — The intermediate trap: being smart instead of being present
5. `hesitation` — The gap between impulse and action where scenes go to die

**Phase 2: Named Failure Modes** (Antipatterns as vocabulary)
6. `bulldozing` — Steamrolling your partner's offers
7. `steering` — Trying to control where the scene goes
8. `overcomplication` — Adding complexity when simplicity would serve
9. `judgment` — Evaluating during creation
10. `fear-of-failure` — Why your best scenes happen when you stop caring

**Phase 3: The Mechanics You Skipped** (Deeper technique)
11. `game-of-the-scene` — Not just "find the game" but HOW games emerge and WHY they work
12. `heightening` — The physics of escalation
13. `rest-beat` — Why you need to breathe between game moves
14. `discovery` — Finding what the scene is about AFTER it starts
15. `scene-structure` — The architecture underneath

**Phase 4: Advanced Ensemble** (Group dynamics)
16. `group-scene` — Why group scenes fail and how to focus them
17. `editing` — When to end it (the instinct you can't teach from rules)
18. `support-moves` — How to make the scene about your partner
19. `reading-the-room` — Sensing what the show needs
20. `show-dynamic` — The arc of a full performance

**Phase 5: Recovery and Renewal** (Breaking the plateau)
21. `decay-recovery` — How skills degrade and how to reverse it
22. `latency-recovery` — Recovering from a bad moment mid-scene
23. `fracture-recovery` — Recovering from a scene that broke
24. `be-changeable` — The willingness to abandon what's working for something better
25. `be-thankful` — Reframing practice as gift, not grind (gratitude as plateau-breaker)

### Path Alignment
Neither existing path serves this persona. `beginner-foundations` is too basic. `physics-of-connection` is conceptual but beginner-targeted.

**Recommended:** Create a new path `self-coaching-toolkit` targeting `intermediate` audience. Sequence: diagnostics first → named failure modes → deeper technique → ensemble dynamics → recovery. The key insight: this persona doesn't need more information — they need better LANGUAGE for what they already feel.

## User Stories

1. **As a reflective practitioner**, I want a vocabulary for common scene failure modes (bulldozing, steering, problem-prov), so that I can name what went wrong instead of just feeling bad after a show.

2. **As a reflective practitioner**, I want a self-assessment framework I can use without a teacher, so that I can identify what to work on in practice and stop depending on class notes.

3. **As a reflective practitioner**, I want to understand the knowledge-ability gap, so that I can trust I'm improving even when it feels like regression.

4. **As a reflective practitioner**, I want specific exercises I can do solo or with one partner, so that I can deliberately practice weak areas between shows.

5. **As a reflective practitioner**, I want to reconnect with the joy of play, so that improv stops feeling like a job I'm failing at and starts feeling like the gift it was when I began.
