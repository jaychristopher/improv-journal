# The Improv Researcher / Writer

## Profile

Content creators, podcast hosts, bloggers, academics, and serious practitioners who write about improvisation with intellectual rigor. They cite Johnstone, Spolin, Close, Halpern, Napier, and Sawyer. They want sourced, cross-referenced, multi-tradition analysis — not another "5 tips for better improv" listicle. They're frustrated that the improv literature is repetitive, single-tradition, and poorly cross-referenced.

This is the smallest but most influential persona. A single improv researcher who references this graph in a blog post, podcast, or academic paper amplifies its reach to the other four personas. They're the connective tissue of the improv knowledge ecosystem.

## Jobs to Be Done

**Functional:**
1. **Find sourced, cross-referenced material** — "Every improv book says the same five things." They want depth beyond the standard Johnstone/Close/Spolin citations — specific chapters, specific claims, specific counter-positions.
2. **Compare traditions rigorously** — Johnstone vs. Spolin vs. Close vs. UCB vs. Annoyance. Not "they're all great in different ways" but "here's specifically where they disagree and why."
3. **Access the oral tradition** — So much improv knowledge lives in classrooms, never written down. When a teacher retires or a theater closes, that knowledge is lost. They want it preserved and cross-referenced.

**Emotional:**
4. **Feel like the field is being taken seriously** — Improv as intellectual subject, not just performance art. The fragmented academic landscape (management, cognitive science, theatre studies, education don't cross-cite) leaves them isolated in their own corner.

**Social:**
5. **Reference material they can cite** — Podcast hosts need structured material to discuss. Bloggers need claims they can link to. Academics need something between practitioner anecdote and inaccessible journal articles.

## Pains

### 1. Repetitive, Single-Tradition Literature
The same 5-8 books dominate every "what should I read?" thread.

Reddit r/improv "what should I read?" threads consistently recommend: Johnstone's *Impro*, *Truth in Comedy*, UCB Manual, Napier's *Improvise*, Spolin's *Improvisation for the Theater*, Salinsky & Frances-White's *The Improv Handbook*, Hines' *How to Be the Greatest Improviser on Earth*, Wasson's *Improv Nation*. After these eight, the answer is usually "there isn't really a good book on that."

Common frustration: "Is there anything that compares different approaches?" The demand for comparative, multi-tradition analysis is consistently expressed but the supply doesn't exist.

**Atoms that address this:** Every atom with counter-positions across traditions (the entire graph is designed to be multi-tradition)

### 2. Fragmented Academic Field
Researchers in different disciplines don't know about each other.

The academic study of improv is split across five silos that rarely cross-cite:
1. **Performance studies / theatre history** (Frost & Yarrow, Dudeck/McClure on Johnstone)
2. **Cognitive science / creativity** (Sawyer, Limb fMRI research)
3. **Management / organizational studies** (Crossan, Weick)
4. **Education / pedagogy** (Spolin lineage, drama education)
5. **Applied improvisation** (AIN community, therapy applications)

A management researcher citing Johnstone may be unaware of Sawyer's cognitive work. A drama educator citing Spolin may never have read Crossan's organizational improvisation papers.

**Atoms that address this:** The reference atoms (`ref-*`) bridge traditions; the axiom atoms bridge disciplines

### 3. Misrepresentation in Non-Improv Contexts
Business writers flatten "yes-and" into "agree with everything." Psychology writers reduce Johnstone to status hierarchies.

Common errors in non-improv citations:
- **"Yes-And" reductionism** — losing the distinction between accepting reality and blindly agreeing
- **Conflating traditions** — citing "improv teaches us..." as if one unified tradition exists
- **Ignoring failure/vulnerability** — cherry-picking positive principles, dropping the surrender aspect
- **Misattributing "Yes-And" to Johnstone** — it's Chicago-lineage (Spolin → Sills → Compass → Second City), not Johnstone
- **Treating Del Close as a theorist** — Close was an oral teacher; *Truth in Comedy* is Halpern/Johnson's interpretation of his teaching

**Atoms that address this:** `yes-and` (with honest attribution), `offers`, `blocking` (Johnstone's actual taxonomy), all reference atoms with specific chapter citations

### 4. The Oral Tradition Problem
Knowledge lives in classrooms, not publications. It dies when teachers retire.

Gary Schwartz (Spolin estate) has expressed frustration that Spolin's actual methodology is poorly understood even by people who cite her. The sidecoaching technique, the specifics of her games — these are oral knowledge that Schwartz works to preserve.

The Dudeck/McClure critical biography of Johnstone was welcomed precisely because it documented knowledge that previously existed only in Loose Moose workshops.

**Atoms that address this:** The graph's exercise atoms preserve specific exercise mechanics; the pedagogy atoms preserve teaching methodology

### 5. Anti-Theory Tension
A significant camp within improv argues that analysis interferes with performance.

> TJ Jagodowski doesn't think about "game" or structure while performing. Razowsky explicitly opposes game-based analysis.

> Common saying: "Improv is caught, not taught."

> Spolin herself was suspicious of intellectualization. Johnstone warns against "being in your head." Close would likely have been uncomfortable with how the UCB Manual codified his work.

The debate is really about **explicit vs. tacit knowledge** — even anti-theory improvisers have deep internalized principles; they just resist codification.

**Atoms that address this:** `internal-computation` (the cost of explicit processing), `obvious-choice` (trusting spontaneity), `discovery` (emergent knowledge). The graph frames itself as a reflection companion, not a replacement for practice.

## Desires

1. **A cross-referenced knowledge graph** — Not a linear book but a networked structure where every concept links to related concepts, source material, and counter-positions across traditions. This is literally what the project IS.
2. **Specific citations** — Not "Johnstone says..." but "Johnstone, *Impro*, Ch. 4 ('Narrative Skills'), p. 116." The graph's sourcing convention directly serves this.
3. **Honest counter-positions** — Not diplomatic "everyone's right" but "Napier argues X; UCB argues Y; here's why they disagree." The `**Counter-position:**` convention in every atom serves this.
4. **Bridge between academic and practitioner language** — Sawyer's "collaborative emergence" translated into performer language. Crossan's organizational improvisation connected to Johnstone's offers. The axiom atoms do this.
5. **Citable, linkable reference material** — Atoms with stable IDs that podcast hosts can reference, bloggers can link to, and writers can cite.

## Objections

1. **"You can't learn improv from a graph"** — True, and the graph doesn't claim to teach improv. It's a reference and reflection tool, not a curriculum replacement. Framed as "for reflection between practices."
2. **"This systematizes what should stay fluid"** — Valid concern. The graph holds multiple traditions in tension rather than resolving them into a single system. Counter-positions are structural, not optional.
3. **"Who are you to synthesize all this?"** — The graph is transparent about attribution. Original synthesis is labeled. Oral tradition is flagged. Unverifiable claims are noted. The methodology is the credibility.
4. **"This duplicates existing resources"** — No existing resource cross-references Johnstone, Spolin, Close, UCB, Annoyance, and TJ & Dave traditions in a single linked graph with specific citations and counter-positions. This is verified — it doesn't exist.

Start with the graph itself. This persona doesn't need a guided path — they need a map they can navigate freely.

**Phase 1: The Architecture** (How the graph works)
1. `reality-construction` — The central reframe that organizes everything
2. The six axioms — the underlying physics
3. The eight North Stars — behavioral principles derived from axioms
4. Counter-position convention — how disagreements are structured
5. Reference atoms — the sourcing methodology

**Phase 2: Tradition Comparisons** (Where the graph shines for researchers)
6. Atoms where Johnstone, Spolin, Close, UCB, Annoyance, and TJ & Dave traditions diverge:
   - `yes-and` (Chicago-lineage vs. Johnstone's offer acceptance)
   - `game-of-the-scene` (UCB game-first vs. Annoyance commitment-first vs. Johnstone narrative-first)
   - `status` (Johnstone's core vs. Spolin's peripheral treatment)
   - `presence` (Spolin's POC vs. Meisner's attention vs. mindfulness)

### Path Alignment
This persona doesn't want a linear path. They want a **reference guide** — an index into the graph organized by topic, tradition, and type.

**Recommended:** Create a path `reference-guide` targeting `advanced` audience that functions as an index rather than a journey. Organized by: traditions (Johnstone, Spolin, Close, UCB, Annoyance), concept types (axioms, principles, techniques, exercises, antipatterns), and cross-cutting themes (game, narrative, ensemble, pedagogy). No assumed reading order.

## User Stories

1. **As an improv researcher**, I want every claim in the graph to cite a specific source (book, chapter, page, or URL), so that I can verify claims and cite them in my own work.

2. **As an improv researcher**, I want to see where Johnstone, Spolin, Close, and UCB disagree on a specific concept, so that I can present multiple traditions accurately instead of conflating them.

3. **As an improv researcher**, I want the graph to be honest about what's sourced, what's oral tradition, and what's original synthesis, so that I can trust the attribution methodology.

4. **As an improv researcher**, I want a networked reference I can navigate by concept rather than read linearly, so that I can find exactly the depth I need on any specific topic.

5. **As an improv researcher**, I want the axiom layer to bridge improv practice and academic frameworks (cognitive science, sociology, education), so that I can connect improv knowledge to research in my own field.
